Abstract 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) en-hances the utility of Large Language Mod-els (LLMs) by retrieving external documents. Since the knowledge databases in RAG are pre-dominantly utilized via cloud services, private data in sensitive domains such as finance and healthcare faces the risk of personal informa-tion leakage. Thus, effectively anonymizing knowledge bases is crucial for privacy preser-vation. Existing studies equate the privacy risk of text to the linear superposition of the privacy risks of individual, isolated sensitive entities. The "one-size-fits-all" full process-ing of all sensitive entities severely degrades utility of LLM. To address this issue, we in-troduce a dynamic anonymization framework named TRIP-RAG . Based on context-aware entity quantification, this framework evaluates entities from the perspectives of marginal pri-vacy risk, knowledge divergence, and topical relevance. It identifies highly sensitive entities while trading off utility, providing a feasible approach for variable-intensity privacy protec-tion scenarios. Our theoretical analysis and experiments indicate that TRIP-RAG can ef-fectively reduce context inference risks. Ex-tensive experimental results demonstrate that, while maintaining privacy protection compa-rable to full anonymization, TRIP-RAG‚Äôs Re-call@k decreases by less than 35% compared to the original data, and the generation quality improves by up to 56% over existing baselines.

To address the aforementioned issues and chal-lenges, we propose TRIP-RAG, a novel dynamic anonymization framework tailored for massive texts within knowledge databases of RAG. As il-lustrated in Figure 1, we posit that textual pri-vacy does not necessitate the absolute protec-tion of all related entities. Instead, by selec-tively anonymizing a subset of entities, we can achieve semantic anonymity for the text, thereby maximizing utility while preserving privacy guar-antees. To resolve the priority issue of entity se-lection in localized anonymization, we focus for the first time on context-aware entity quantifi-cation within RAG scenarios, evaluating enti-ties through a three-dimensional manifold that encompasses marginal privacy risk, knowledge divergence, and topical relevance. Specifically, by utilizing a small number of annotated samples to train a language model-based privacy-aware sur-rogate function, we leverage the concept of differ-ential analysis to achieve marginal privacy risk de-tection for massive entities. Based on the seman-tic clustering property of high-dimensional vector spaces (Mikolov et al. [2013]), we quantify the sta-bility of an entity with respect to the overall knowl-edge structure of the text using the cosine simi-larity of the text before and after entity masking. Furthermore, drawing on the RAG framework‚Äôs re-liance on vector distance for retrieval (Lewis et al. [2020]), we treat entities as proxy queries to quan-tify their topical relevance to a document by com-puting the L2 distance between their respective la-tent representations. Through the comprehensive ranking of these three scores and dynamic thresh-old control, we realize an anonymization frame-work with adjustable privacy protection strength that fully preserves utility. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach maintains sub-stantial text utility while safeguarding privacy, validating the superiority of our method. 

The main contributions of this paper are as fol-lows: ‚ÄĘ We introduce a dynamic anonymization framework tailored for the massive text scenarios in RAG knowledge databases, uniquely integrating a multi-dimensional quantitative assessment of entity privacy, knowledge, and retrieval utility. ‚ÄĘ We provide a trade-off method between pri-vacy and utility for numerous downstream tasks in the field of text anonymization. ‚ÄĘ Cross-dataset, multi-model experiments demonstrate that our scheme quantifies and balances privacy and utility, achieving apercentage increase in utility with almost no impact on privacy. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 RAG Technology and Attack Methods 

Proposed by Lewis et al. [2020], Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a method de-signed to enhance the generative capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). By representing massive document corpora as dense embeddings 2to facilitate efficient semantic retrieval, RAG effec-tively mitigates LLM hallucinations and bolsters response reliability. Furthermore, its flexible doc-ument storage enables rapid updates to the model‚Äôs accessible knowledge base without requiring re-training. These advantages have established RAG as the preferred approach for deploying LLMs in knowledge-intensive domains, demonstrating sig-nificant utility in highly hallucination-prone sce-narios such as medical disease diagnosis(Liang et al. [2025]). However, while RAG enhances utility, it concurrently introduces significant privacy con-cerns. The knowledge databases utilized in RAG are typically deployed as cloud services (e.g., AWS(Enevoldsen et al. [2025])), which predom-inantly rely on proprietary black-box embedding models to drive Vector Database (VDB) embed-ding services. This paradigm renders the server-side inherently untrustworthy. Since texts in sen-sitive domains frequently contain sensitive data, storing original documents naturally leads to the leakage of private information. Furthermore, re-lying solely on a trusted server is insufficient to resolve these privacy issues, as numerous server-targeted attacks have recently emerged. Exploit-ing the instruction-following capabilities of LMs, Qi et al. [2024] constructed context-based prompt attacks that can easily induce data leakage . Simi-larly, Zeng et al. [2024] formulated customized at-tack prompts tailored to specific types of extracted information, successfully executing both targeted and untargeted attacks against private data. Subse-quently, several techniques have been proposed to mitigate privacy risks, including post-processing methods like reranking and summarization (Chase [2022]), but studies have indicated that these tech-niques cannot completely eliminate privacy risks (Zeng et al. [2024]). The inadequacy of these post-processing defenses in thwarting such at-tacks demonstrates the inherent vulnerability of storing raw data, highlighting the critical need for data-level privacy protection mecha-nisms. 2.2 Existing Protection Strategies for Knowledge Database Texts 

Currently, researchers have conducted preliminary explorations into the privacy protection of knowl-edge database texts, which can be categorized into synthesis-based methods utilizing LLM text gen-eration capabilities and perturbation-based meth-ods based on differential privacy. In terms of data synthesis, focusing on privacy concerns, Tang et al. [2023] proposed a few-shot data generation method to produce private in-context demonstra-tions from private datasets . Furthermore, Zeng et al. [2025] pioneered the implementation of data synthesis based on the contextual information of original data in the field of privacy protection for knowledge database texts; they extracted a small amount of information from the original data to assist large models in data synthesis, and ensured that the text did not leak privacy through adver-sarial privacy evaluation LLMs. However, these methods overlook the sheer scale of text within knowledge databases. Relying on LLMs for such extensive generation tasks not only incurs pro-hibitive costs but also leads to significant informa-tion loss and increased hallucination rates. To address these problems, researchers have shifted towards perturbation-based schemes. Dif-ferential Privacy (DP) (Dwork et al. [2006]) pro-vides privacy guarantees for each record in a dataset by adding extra noise to query results to limit the impact of individual records; Feyisetan et al. [2020] employed a word-level perturbation method based on word embeddings for differen-tial privacy perturbation, while Igamberdiev and Habernal [2023] proposed a sentence-level pertur-bation method based on paraphrase task language models from the perspective of semantic differ-ences. Targeting the privacy protection of knowl-edge database texts, He et al. [2025] shifted their focus from the entire text to private entities, per-turbing these private entities through a local dif-ferential privacy method that relies more heavily on the original text. However, for knowledge database texts requiring high accuracy to ensure the quality of LLM generation, such differential privacy methods suffer from uncertain entity per-turbation, which degrades text readability. Fur-thermore, current studies universally lack privacy difference evaluation , making the intensity of en-tity perturbation unsuitable for privacy protection scenarios requiring differentiated protection lev-els. They all share the limitation of conflating text privacy protection with the privacy pro-tection of scope entities , failing to properly de-fine what constitutes text-level privacy protection in their problem formulation, which consequently causes a massive loss in utility. 33 Problem Formulation 

3.1 Problem Definition 

Recalling the example in Section 1, existing research on privacy protection for knowledge databases mostly remains at the stage of ‚Äúscoped entity privacy protection‚ÄĚ, which assumes that the privacy risk of a text is equivalent to the linear superposition of the privacy risks from individ-ual, isolated sensitive entities. However, in the field of natural language processing, text is a con-tinuous representation entity located in a high-dimensional semantic space. The privacy leak-age risk of text primarily originates from the com-binatorial inference risks generated by the inter-twining of context, which shares a non-linear rela-tionship with sensitive entities; when certain sen-sitive entities are anonymized, the semantic pri-vacy risk of the text can be eliminated. Therefore, we posit that semantic privacy protection at the text level should not be the anonymization of all private entities, but rather maximizing the over-all utility of the text through local anonymiza-tion while severing the semantic reconstruction paths.

3.2 Threat Model 

We define the attacker A as an entity with polynomial-time computational capabilities. In the untrusted server scenario, A possesses white-box access privileges to the knowledge database; in the trusted server scenario, A possesses black-box access privileges to the RAG system and holds white-box knowledge regarding the anonymiza-tion mechanism ő†. Since the individuals to which the texts in the knowledge database may refer pos-sess the characteristic of being uncountable, the observation target of both types of attackers can be practically defined as a single generalized text. Furthermore, since data in sensitive domains is hard to obtain, attackers cannot actually gain back-ground knowledge in the same way as they can with ordinary data. The objectives of the attacker include: (1) En-tity inference: recovering specific sensitive enti-ties from the generalized text; (2) Document recon-struction: reconstructing the original document through combinatorial attacks utilizing residual in-formation.(Goldwasser and Micali [2019])

5 Experiment 

In this section, we demonstrate through cross-dataset and multi-model experiments that our pro-posed scheme effectively quantifies and balances privacy and utility. 5.1 Experimental Setup Datasets and Models. For privacy protec-tion scenarios, we adopted the ChatDoctor-HealthCareMagic-100k dataset utilized by He et al. [2025], which encompasses over 100,000 doctor-patient conversations containing extensive personal information such as names and ages. Based on scenario requirements, we selected the Enronqa dataset provided by Ryan et al. [2025]. This dataset was derived from the En-ron emails corpus through filtering operations to obtain 70,000 authentic workplace Q&A pairs, which include personal information such as names and private phone numbers. Thereby validating our method‚Äôs effectiveness in two representative sensitive domains: healthcare and business. Fol-lowing the evaluation protocols of the baseline methods, we allocated 99% of the dataset for re-trieval purposes and the remaining 1% for test-ing. We selected Llama2-7b-chat (Touvron et al. [2023]) as the LLM for performance evaluation, and utilized the Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B model (Zhang et al. [2025]) , the bge-large-en-v1.5 model (Xiao et al. [2024]) alongside the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model as the embedding models. 

Baselines. To verify the effectiveness of our method, we incorporated three baselines, includ-ing full generalized mapping replacement, para-phrasing methods, and a representative exist-ing knowledge database anonymization method-namely, the anonymization method based on local differential privacy proposed by He et al. [2025]. Detailed information regarding these methods is provided in Appendix G. Additionally, we in-cluded the results from the original data for com-parison. 

Metrics. We employ three categories of met-rics to evaluate post-anonymization retrieval per-formance, utility, and privacy protection. For retrieval performance, referring to the study by Thakur et al. [2021], we incorporated Re-call@k as the evaluation metric to illustrate the post-anonymization divergence from the original knowledge. For utility evaluation, we utilized the following metrics: ROUGE-L (Lin [2004]) to mea-sure the accuracy and overlap between the origi-nal text and the reconstructed text, and the BLEU score (Papineni et al. [2002]) to assess the n-gram similarity between the two. For privacy protection, following the privacy evaluation methodology of He et al. [2025], we adopted the privacy extrac-tion attack proposed by Zeng et al. [2024] to mea-sure the method‚Äôs resistance against contextual in-ference capabilities. This attack extracts relevant retrieved documents by formulating ‚Äúinformation + command‚ÄĚ prompts. It quantifies the privacy pro-tection of each baseline by counting the privacy entities present in the LLM-generated text.

We highlight the distinction between text semantic-level privacy protection and scope-entity privacy protection, correcting the misconceptions regard-ing text semantic privacy protection from the per-spective of problem formulation. We focus on the variability of privacy protection intensity and the disparities among entities, constructing a dynamic anonymization framework tailored for knowledge databases by defining the marginal privacy risk, knowledge divergence, and topical relevance of entities. Through cross-dataset and multi-model experiments, we demonstrate that our proposed scheme quantifies and balances privacy and util-ity, achieving a percentage improvement in util-ity while having almost no impact on privacy. The evaluation framework proposed in this paper can also be extended to the broader field of text anonymization and integrated with various down-stream anonymization methods, providing a novel reference and a controllable-intensity perspective for text semantic privacy protection. 

7 Limitation 

In this study, we rely on the GLiNER model to ex-tract private entities (Zaratiana et al. [2024]) for the experimental portion of the paper. Although our investigation indicates that this custom-label-based extraction method is currently the state-of-the-art and the most suitable approach for privacy protection scenarios, our manual audit of the ex-traction results reveals that this method still misses some entities that appear in uncommon positions. Nevertheless, our overall experimental and evalua-tion results are based exclusively on the identified entity content, which does not compromise the ex-perimental findings of this paper, but we look for-ward to future breakthroughs in entity extraction research.

A Reduction of the Problem of Finding the Optimal Generalized Entity Subset 

We prove this by performing a polynomial-time reduction from the classical 0-1 knapsack prob-lem to our anonymization optimization problem. Consider a special case scenario where the back-ground knowledge of the attacker faced by the sys-tem is extremely scarce, such that the generaliza-tion of any single entity is sufficient to satisfy the anti-reconstruction entropy constraint ő∑. Under this special case, non-reconstructability becomes a non-binding constraint and can be ignored. At this point, the optimization problem degenerates into: selecting a subset of entities X subject to a total ca-pacity limit, such that the total ‚Äúvalue‚ÄĚ ue of these entities is maximized. In this mapping: the entity‚Äôs privacy Sepriv is equivalent to the weight of an item in the knapsack problem; the entity‚Äôs utility contri-bution ue is equivalent to the value of an item in the knapsack problem; and the privacy threshold 

Bpriv is equivalent to the maximum capacity of the knapsack. Since the 0-1 knapsack problem is universally recognized as an NP-Hard problem in the classical combinatorial optimization field, and the aforementioned special case is merely a proper subset of the problem proposed in this paper, the original problem is at least as hard as the 0-1 knap-sack problem. Namely, this problem is strongly NP-Hard.

C.1 Data Sampling and Annotation Guidelines 

To construct a high-quality training dataset for privacy scores, we adopted a random sampling method. We randomly sampled 200 represen-tative text snippets rich in entities from the dataset, respectively. The specific regulation content we referred to is as follows: 

personal data means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (data subject); an identifiable natural person is one who can be identified, directly or indirectly, in partic-ular by reference to an identifier such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier or to one or more factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, eco-nomic, cultural or social identity of that natural person; 

We established the following 4-level scoring rubric to quantify the privacy importance score of entities: ‚ÄĘ Level 3 (Score 0.7-1.0): Entities that can di-rectly and uniquely pinpoint a specific indi-vidual. Examples: real names, identification numbers, telephone numbers, exact home ad-dresses, etc. ‚ÄĘ Level 2 (Score 0.4-0.7): Entities posing mod-erate isolated risks but potential inference risks in context, or personal information sus-ceptible to temporal factors.Examples: real age, biometric characteristics. ‚ÄĘ Level 1 (Score 0.1-0.4): Entities that con-tain certain personal characteristics but pos-sess a high degree of generalization within a large population, or entities for which auxil-iary inference information is difficult to ob-tain. Examples: broad geographical regions (e.g., California), medical diagnostic results, and major business decisions. ‚ÄĘ Level 0 (Score 0.0-0.1): General vocabu-lary or objective medical/business terminol-ogy that essentially does not involve personal privacy. Examples: common drug names (e.g., insulin), common symptoms, and gen-eral corporate operations.
