# Incentive-Aware Federated Averaging with Performance Guarantees under Strategic Participation 

Fateme Maleki 1, Krishnan Raghavan 2, and Farzad Yousefian 1∗†‡

March 24, 2026 

Abstract 

Federated learning (FL) is a communication-efficient collaborative learning framework that enables model training across multiple agents with private local datasets. While the benefits of FL in improving global model performance are well established, individual agents may behave strategically, balancing the learning payoff against the cost of contributing their local data. Motivated by the need for FL frameworks that successfully retain participating agents, we propose an incentive-aware federated averaging method in which, at each communication round, clients transmit both their local model parameters and their updated training dataset sizes to the server. The dataset sizes are dynamically adjusted via a Nash equilibrium (NE)–seeking update rule that captures strategic data participation. We analyze the proposed method under convex and nonconvex global objective settings and establish performance guarantees for the resulting incentive-aware FL algorithm. Numerical experiments on the MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets demonstrate that agents achieve competitive global model performance while converging to stable data participation strategies. 

# 1 Introduction 

Federated learning (FL) has recently emerged as a communication-efficient algorithmic framework that enables collaborative model training across multiple agents [ 1, 2 ]. While this collaborative advantage has been rigorously studied [ 3 , 4, 5], as discussed by Blum et al. [ 6 ], the effectiveness of FL fundamentally depends on the ability to recruit and retain a large number of participants 

willing to contribute their data and resources. In existing FL frameworks, it is assumed that the data participation level for each agent remains constant throughout the learning task. However, a participating agent may achieve their training goal even when they unilaterally reduce their data contribution. Further, in settings where some participating agents are competitors, they might not have an incentive to consider a full data participation. For these reasons, a fundamental challenge in federated learning arises around the following questions: (Q1) How can we develop a game-theoretic framework for FL that captures agents’ learning payoffs and participation costs, while guaranteeing the existence of a Nash equilibrium (NE)? (Q2) Building on this formulation, how can we design an incentive-aligned FL algorithmic framework that enables strategic data participation? (Q3) Under such a scheme, can we establish simultaneous performance guarantees for both collaborative learning and the stability of the resulting noncooperative participation strategies?

To address (Q1), the work in [ 6 ] considers a constrained cost-minimization formulation where each agent minimizes its data contribution subject to a payoff lower bound. However, an NE may fail to exist in general. To overcome this issue, [ 7 ] studies an unconstrained net utility loss formulation—minimizing cost minus payoff—which is more natural from a game-theoretic perspective (cf. [8, 9]). To address (Q2), recent works design incentive-aligned FL frameworks for strategic clients. Yi et al. [ 10 ] analyze participation incentives and propose mechanisms guaranteeing a Nash equilibrium in contribution strategies. Similarly, Alaei et al. [ 11 ] employ mechanism design to elicit truthful data reporting and allocate payments based on contribution value. Other approaches use randomized pricing, token-based, and blockchain incentives [ 12 , 13 , 14 ] to sustain participation, showing that careful incentive design can support reliable strategic engagement in FL. 

Gap. To our knowledge, no existing framework provides formal simultaneous performance guarantees for both the global learning objective and the stability of strategic data contributions. Previous approaches either focus on equilibria of participation or on designing incentives, but do not simultaneously ensure provable convergence of the global model under strategic client behavior. 

Contributions. Motivated by this gap, we propose a framework that integrates NE-based participation updates with federated averaging, aiming to maintain both collaborative learning performance and stable strategic engagement Our contributions are threefold. (i) We introduce an incentive-aware federated averaging algorithm in which clients dynamically adjust their local dataset sizes via NE-seeking updates at each communication round. (ii) We establish theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the global FL objective under both convex and nonconvex settings while ensuring stability of strategic participation. (iii) We provide empirical validation on MNIST and CIFAR-10, showing that clients achieve competitive model performance and converge to stable participation levels, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed method.

# 3 Algorithm outline 

To address (1) , we propose an FL scheme termed Incentive-enabled federated averaging, outlined in Algorithm 1. A key challenge is that the weights pi(N ∗), defined by the Nash equilibrium N ∗, are unavailable. To address this, we develop a federated framework with two coupled components: (i) Cooperative learning: at round r, given an estimate ˆNr of N ∗, clients perform standard local updates as in FedAvg; (ii) Noncooperative game: in the same round, clients update their participation strategy via a projected gradient method. 5.2 Observations and insights 

Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 present the numerical results The left plots show the global cross-entropy loss versus communication rounds for different local update steps H ∈ { 1, 5, 10 , 20 }, while the right plots show the evolution of client data contributions Ni,r , illustrating convergence to the equilibrium of the participation Nash game. 

Impact of local computation ( H) The loss curves in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 show that larger H

indeed accelerates convergence. In particular, H = 1 converges the slowest, while H = 20 reaches low loss values in fewer communication rounds. This reflects a standard federated learning trade-off: increased local computation allows clients to make more progress before synchronization, improving communication efficiency. The effect is particularly noticeable on CIFAR-10 despite its higher task complexity. Nash equilibrium stability The right plots in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 show that client contributions 

Ni,r converge to stable values, indicating the emergence of a sustainable Nash equilibrium. Clients with heterogeneous utilities settle at their contribution levels across both datasets. Interestingly, in both settings, there exist clients whose participation strategy has either decreased or increased in reaching stability, highlighting the trade-off between the payoff and cost function of each client. 

# 6 Conclusions 

W proposed IncentFedAvg , an incentive-aware FedAvg method that incorporates strategic data participation into federated learning. The approach couples cooperative local model training with a noncooperative Nash game determining client data contributions, allowing clients to adjust participation based on payoff–cost tradeoffs. We establish convergence guarantees for both convex and nonconvex objectives, ensuring optimality in the convex case and stationarity in the nonconvex case, while the participation vector converges linearly to the unique Nash equilibrium. Experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10 demonstrate competitive performance and stable equilibrium client contributions, validating the proposed mechanism. For future work, extending the convergence analysis to the strongly convex setting could yield sharper rates and tighter complexity bounds.
