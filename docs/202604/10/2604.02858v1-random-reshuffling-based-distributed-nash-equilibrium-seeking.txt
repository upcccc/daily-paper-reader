Abstract 

This paper studies random reshuffling (RR)-based distributed Nash equilibrium seeking for noncooperative games. The game is motivated as a sample-average approximation of an underlying expected-value stochastic game, while the algorithmic focus is placed on the resulting finite-sum equilibrium problem. Unlike existing distributed stochastic Nash equilibrium methods that mainly rely on with-replacement sampling, the proposed approach incorporates without-replacement component updates into equilibrium computation over networks. We first consider a full-information benchmark, for which an intermediate reference trajectory and a shuffling variance are introduced to characterize the epoch-wise dynamics induced by RR. The method is then extended to the more practical partial-decision-information setting, where each player updates its action using local estimates of the joint action profile. For the full-information case, a descent-type bound is established for the RR iterates. For the distributed partial-decision-information case, it is shown that, under constant parameters, the proposed algorithm converges linearly to a neighborhood of the Nash equilibrium, while under diminishing parameters, it converges exactly to the Nash equilibrium almost surely and in mean square. Numerical experiments on an EV charging game and a nonquadratic edge resource admission game demonstrate that RR consistently outperforms the conventional with-replacement SGD baseline in both steady-state accuracy and long-horizon performance.

[8] developed a distributed stochastic Nash equilibrium learning algorithm for locally coupled network games with unknown parameters. [9] proposed a consensus-based gradient-free distributed Nash equi-librium seeking method that requires only local cost-function measurements, rather than explicit knowledge of the cost-function expressions or their gradients. Despite these advances, existing stochastic distributed NE methods are still primarily built on stochastic-approximation and related independently sampled up-dates. While the distributed game literature has not yet fully separated the role of the sampling policy, related stochastic optimization and variational-inequality re-sults indicate that, compared with without-replacement passes, independently sampled updates may induce larger steady-state neighborhoods under constant step-sizes[8], impose stronger requirements for high-accuracy solutions[10], and revisit already-sampled components within an epoch[11], [12]. This motivates the investiga-tion of random reshuffling for distributed stochastic NE seeking. random reshuffling (RR), namely, the without-replacement counterpart of SGD [11], [12]. At the beginning of each epoch, RR generates a fresh permutation of the com-ponent indices and processes every component exactly once in the resulting order, thereby avoiding repeated revisits of already-sampled components within the same pass [10], [12]. Existing optimization results have shown that RR can outperform with-replacement SGD af-ter a finite number of epochs [11] and, under constant step-sizes, drive the iterates to a smaller steady-state neighborhood than uniform with-replacement sampling [13]. More recently, in variational-inequality problems, stochastic extragradient with random reshuffling has been shown to attain arbitrary accuracy without the large-batch requirements often needed by classical with-replacement schemes [10]. In addition, random reshuffling has also demonstrated favorable convergence behavior in distributed optimization over networks [14]. These results suggest that RR is a promising sampling mechanism for distributed stochastic Nash equilibrium seeking, and naturally raise the question of whether its advantages can be similarly exploited in distributed networked games. However, the role of random reshuffling in distributed Nash equilibrium seeking remains largely unexplored, especially under partial-decision information. Existing distributed stochastic NE methods mainly rely on inde-pendently sampled stochastic approximation updates, with primary emphasis on pseudo-gradient design, local estimation, and communication mechanisms. In con-trast, the effect of without-replacement sampling on coupled pseudo-gradient dynamics and network dis-agreement has received much less attention. This moti-vates the present work, where we study whether random reshuffling can serve as a principled sampling mecha-nism for distributed stochastic NE seeking and improve solution accuracy over conventional with-replacement SGD schemes. To the best of our knowledge, random reshuffling has not been systematically studied in distributed stochastic Nash equilibrium seeking. The main contributions of this paper are threefold. (1) We propose a random-reshuffling-based framework for distributed stochastic Nash equilibrium seeking in non-cooperative games. The framework covers both a full-information benchmark and the more practical dis-tributed setting under partial-decision information. (2) We develop an epoch-wise analysis for reshuffling-based game dynamics. In particular, we introduce an in-termediate reference trajectory and a shuffling variance to capture the cumulative error induced by without-replacement sampling, and we further combine this char-acterization with the network disagreement recursion in the distributed setting. (3) We establish convergence guarantees for the pro-posed algorithms. For the full-information case, we de-rive a descent-type bound for the RR iterates. For the partial-decision-information case, we show linear con-vergence to a neighborhood of the Nash equilibrium un-der constant parameters, and exact convergence almost surely and in mean square under diminishing parame-ters. Numerical experiments on an EV charging game and a nonquadratic edge resource admission game show that RR consistently outperforms the with-replacement SGD baseline in both steady-state accuracy and long-horizon performance.

5 Conclusion 

This paper investigated random reshuffling based Nash equilibrium seeking for noncooperative games. We first considered the full-information setting and then ex-tended the method to the distributed partial-decision information case. By introducing an intermediate ref-erence trajectory and the corresponding shuffling vari-ance, we characterized the epoch-wise structure of RR and established its convergence properties. In particu-lar, under constant parameters, the proposed algorithm converges linearly to a neighborhood of the Nash equi-librium, while under diminishing parameters, exact convergence to the Nash equilibrium is achieved almost surely and in mean square. Numerical results further showed that RR consistently outperforms SGD with re-placement under both oracle-complexity-matched and epoch-matched comparisons. Future work will focus on relaxing the strong monotonicity requirement and ex-tending the proposed framework to broader classes of distributed stochastic games.
