# Privacy-Accuracy Trade-offs in High-Dimensional LASSO under Perturbation Mechanisms 

## Ayaka Sakata ∗

## Department of Information Science, Ochanomizu University RIKEN center for AIP Haruka Tanzawa Department of Information Science, Ochanomizu University March 30, 2026 

Abstract 

We study privacy-preserving sparse linear regression in the high-dimensional regime, focusing on the LASSO estimator. We analyze two widely used mechanisms for dif-ferential privacy: output perturbation, which injects noise into the estimator, and objective perturbation, which adds a random linear term to the loss function. Using approximate message passing (AMP), we characterize the typical behavior of these estimators under random design and privacy noise. To quantify privacy, we adopt typical-case measures, including the on-average KL divergence, which admits a hypothesis-testing interpretation in terms of distinguishability between neighboring datasets. Our analysis reveals that sparsity plays a central role in shaping the privacy-accuracy trade-off: stronger regularization can improve privacy by stabilizing the es-timator against single-point data changes. We further show that the two mechanisms exhibit qualitatively different behaviors. In particular, for objective perturbation, increasing the noise level can have non-monotonic effects, and excessive noise may destabilize the estimator, leading to increased sensitivity to data perturbations. Our results demonstrate that AMP provides a powerful framework for analyzing privacy-accuracy trade-offs in high-dimensional sparse models. 

Keywords: Differential Privacy, Objective Perturbation, Output Perturbation, Sparse Es-timation, Approximate Message Passing

# 1 Introduction 

Differential privacy (DP) has become a foundational concept in modern data analysis, pro-viding formal guarantees that protect whether an individual’s data is included in a dataset [Dwork et al., 2006]. Its practical relevance is evidenced by its deployment in production systems by large-scale tech companies and governmental institutions. For example, the U.S. Census Bureau employed differential privacy for the 2020 decennial census data re-lease, marking one of the first large-scale governmental uses of DP [Kenny et al., 2021]. Similarly, technology platforms use DP to protect individual contributions in aggregate statistics derived from user behavior, recommendation systems [M¨ ullner et al., 2023], and natural language processing pipelines [Klymenko et al., 2022]. In addition, there is increas-ing demand for privacy-preserving analysis in domains such as healthcare, genomics, and finance, where both the dimensionality of data and the risk of privacy leakage are high. A common approach to achieve differential privacy is to randomize the process of pa-rameter estimation, a principle known as the randomized mechanism . Intuitively, such randomization prevents an adversary from reliably inferring whether a particular indi-vidual’s data was used in the analysis, by ensuring that the algorithm’s output remains probabilistically similar even when one data point is modified. Several randomized mech-anisms have been proposed for statistical estimation. For instance, [Dwork et al., 2006] introduced the Laplace mechanism, in which independent Laplace noise is added to the output of a query (i.e., a function of the dataset, such as an estimator) in proportion to its sensitivity, providing a concrete method to achieve ( ϵ, 0)-differential privacy. This method belongs to the so-called output perturbation framework, where noise is added directly to the estimated model parameters after optimization. Another widely used approach is objective perturbation (or loss perturbation), where noise is injected into the loss function before or during optimization [Chaudhuri et al., 2011]. These two classes of mechanisms exhibit different theoretical and empirical behaviors in terms of privacy guarantees and statistical 2accuracy, although in specific cases, such as least-squares estimation, they can be shown to be mathematically equivalent under suitable conditions. A fundamental challenge in differential privacy lies in balancing the trade-off between preserving privacy and maintaining the usefulness of data, often referred to as the privacy-utility trade-off [Dwork et al., 2014, Chaudhuri et al., 2011]. Utility in this context includes statistical accuracy, model interpretability, computational feasibility, and application-specific relevance. This trade-off is intrinsic to privacy-preserving mechanisms: stronger privacy guarantees typically require introducing more randomness, which can deteriorate the util-ity of the resulting model or statistical output. In supervised learning tasks, a prominent instance of the privacy-utility trade-off is the privacy-accuracy trade-off. Here, utility is measured in terms of predictive accuracy or estimation error, and the goal is to quantify how privacy mechanisms degrade the model’s performance. Output perturbation is often simpler to analyze, but it may fail to adapt to structural properties of the underlying parameters. In particular, in sparse estimation problems such as LASSO, adding noise directly to the estimated coefficients can destroy sparsity patterns and deteriorate the interpretability and statistical efficiency of the solution [Khanna et al., 2023]. To address this issue, several works have proposed variants of output perturbation that explicitly account for sparsity. A common approach is to first estimate the support of the parameters and then apply output perturbation only to the selected (nonzero) com-ponents [Lei et al., 2018]. Similar ideas appear in differentially private model selection procedures, where variable selection is performed prior to private estimation on the re-duced model [Smith, 2011, Talwar et al., 2015]. These approaches can improve utility by avoiding unnecessary noise on irrelevant coordinates, and have been shown to achieve fa-vorable accuracy-privacy trade-offs under sparsity assumptions. However, such two-stage procedures introduce additional challenges from a privacy perspective. Since the support selection step itself depends on the data, it may leak sensitive information unless it is prop-3erly privatized. By the composition property of differential privacy [Dwork et al., 2014], this typically requires allocating a portion of the privacy budget to the model selection step, which increases the overall complexity of the mechanism and may degrade perfor-mance. In contrast, objective perturbation injects noise directly into the loss function before optimization. This approach implicitly accounts for the regularization structure of the estimator, and can better preserve properties such as sparsity without requiring an explicit support selection step. This motivates us to analyze objective perturbation as an alternative mechanism within a unified framework. In the context of statistical estimation, particularly penalized regression methods such as the LASSO, several works have developed perturbation-based mechanisms to achieve differential privacy guarantees. However, these analyses typically rely on strong structural assumptions on the data or the design matrix, such as restricted eigenvalue conditions or exact sparsity [Chaudhuri et al., 2011, Talwar et al., 2015, Smith, 2011], which may not hold in practical high-dimensional settings. Existing results suggest that objective perturbation can outperform output perturbation under strong convexity and smoothness assumptions [Chaudhuri et al., 2011]. However, it remains unclear whether such advantages persist in high-dimensional sparse regimes, where the estimator is inherently non-smooth. Moreover, the interplay between privacy noise, regularization, and generalization performance remains only partially understood. To better understand these interactions, we focus on the high-dimensional regime, where both the number of samples and parameters grow to infinity with a fixed ratio. In such settings, worst-case guarantees from differential privacy can be overly conservative, as they are often dominated by rare pathological configurations that do not reflect typical data realizations. In contrast, typical-case analysis captures the behavior under random design and noise, which is more relevant for modern statistical applications. Despite its impor-tance, this perspective has received limited attention, and in particular, the role of sparsity 4in shaping effective privacy leakage has not been systematically characterized. In such settings, typical-case analysis requires a distributional characterization of the es-timator under random data. This naturally motivates an information-theoretic approach to quantifying privacy leakage. Specifically, we consider the problem of distinguishing whether an estimator was computed from a dataset D or from a neighboring dataset D′ differing in a single sample. This naturally leads to a binary hypothesis testing problem, where the optimal error exponent is governed by the KL divergence between the corresponding distributions. This connection provides an operational interpretation of KL-based privacy measures: a small KL divergence implies that even an optimal adversary cannot reliably infer the presence of an individual data point. Building on this formulation, we analyze the high-dimensional LASSO under both out-put perturbation and objective perturbation using tools from approximate inference, in particular approximate message passing (AMP). AMP provides an asymptotic characteri-zation of high-dimensional estimators under random design, enabling a typical-case analysis of both generalization error and privacy-related quantities. This framework allows us to quantify how sparsity, noise strength, and the sample-to-parameter ratio jointly influence both prediction accuracy and privacy, without relying on restrictive conditions such as restricted eigenvalue assumptions or worst-case sensitivity bounds. 

## 1.1 Related Works 

A substantial body of work has developed differentially private variants of regularized re-gression methods, including ridge regression and LASSO, using techniques including output perturbation, objective perturbation, and noise injection into sufficient statistics [Chaud-huri et al., 2011, Talwar et al., 2015]. While these methods provide rigorous privacy guaran-tees under certain modeling assumptions, their theoretical guarantees in high-dimensional settings often rely on structural conditions-such as restricted eigenvalue assumptions, which 5can be difficult to satisfy in overparameterized regimes. In the broader context of private empirical risk minimization, Bassily et al. [2014] es-tablished minimax optimal rates, clarifying the fundamental trade-off between privacy and utility. While these worst-case guarantees are foundational, they can be overly conserva-tive and may not reflect the typical behavior of learning algorithms. Related developments, including proximal perturbation methods for non-smooth optimization [Wang et al., 2018] and compressed noise injection techniques [Zhou et al., 2020], further expand the scope of private optimization, but remain primarily focused on worst-case analyses. Complementary to these works, on-average privacy measures such as the On-average KL divergence (OnAveKL) [Wang et al., 2016] have been proposed to capture typical-case privacy leakage. These metrics admit an operational interpretation in terms of hypothesis testing and membership inference, and provide a more refined view of privacy in practice. However, existing studies have largely focused on low-dimensional or unregularized settings, leaving open the question of how high dimensionality, sparsity, and regularization jointly affect on-average privacy. Despite these advances, a systematic understanding of privacy-accuracy trade-offs in high-dimensional sparse models remains lacking. In particular, the interaction between noise injection mechanisms, structural constraints such as sparsity, and typical-case privacy measures has not been fully characterized. 

## 1.2 Our contribution 

In this work, we analyze differentially private sparse linear regression in the high-dimensional regime, focusing on the LASSO estimator under both output perturbation and objective perturbation. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

• Asymptotic characterization of perturbation mechanisms: We derive an ex-plicit asymptotic characterization of the LASSO estimator under both output per-6turbation and objective perturbation in the high-dimensional limit. Using approxi-mate message passing, we reduce the high-dimensional problem to a scalar equivalent model, which captures the combined effects of sparsity, noise injection, and sample complexity. 

• Closed-form characterization of typical-case accuracy and privacy: We ob-tain explicit formulas for both prediction accuracy and the on-average KL divergence (OnAveKL) in the high-dimensional limit. Our results characterize the dependence of both quantities on the noise variance and the sparsity level induced by regularization. 

• Comparison between output and objective perturbation: We establish a quantitative comparison between output perturbation and objective perturbation within a unified framework. Our analysis reveals that objective perturbation can achieve smaller OnAveKL in regimes when the support of the estimator remains stable under the replacement of a single data point. 

• Implications for noise calibration: Our analytical characterization provides prac-tical guidance for selecting the noise level to balance prediction accuracy and privacy. In particular, we identify regimes where increasing noise leads to non-monotonic ef-fects on stability and privacy under objective perturbation. Taken together, our results provide a unified and quantitative understanding of privacy-accuracy trade-offs in high-dimensional sparse estimation, complementing existing worst-case analyses.

4.2.3 Convergence condition of AMP 

The AMP algorithm does not always converge. The stability of the AMP algorithm can be characterized by its local stability condition associated with state evolution. In the present setting, the stability condition is given by [Krzakala et al., 2012, Sakata, 2023] 

bρα < 1, (42) where bρ denotes the asymptotic fraction of nonzero components in the estimator. This condition simply requires that the number of active coefficients does not exceed the number 22 of observations. The results presented in this paper are valid only when (42) is satisfied. Figure 3(b) illustrates the parameter region where this stability condition is satisfied. The boundary of the stable region depends primarily on the ratio α, with weaker depen-dence on other parameters. Although the convergence condition is derived from AMP, it is also related to the behavior of coordinate descent algorithms, as shown in Fig.3(b). In prior work without privacy noise, non-unique solution regions of coordinate descent coin-cide with AMP divergence regions [Sakata and Xu, 2018, Obuchi and Sakata, 2019]. We find that this correspondence holds under privacy noise as well. Although the optimization problem with objective perturbation remains convex, the above condition shows that the estimator can enter a regime where AMP fails to converge. This behavior is closely related to the sparsity of the solution. In particular, objective perturbation tends to increase the number of active coefficients, making the estimator denser. As a result, the condition bρ/α < 1 is more easily violated compared to the noiseless case. In the high-dimensional limit p, n → ∞ with α = n/p = O(1), combining the state evolution analysis with an analysis by replica method gives the following expression for the state evolution parameter: 

V = bρα − bρ. (43) Equation (43) shows that V diverges as α approaches bρ, i.e., when condition (42) is violated. This divergence explains the observed divergence of the empirical ratio in Fig. 2(b).

# 7 Conclusion 

In this work, we investigated statistical estimation accuracy and privacy using the on-average KL criterion in high-dimensional LASSO with output and objective perturbation. We quantified how privacy noise affects estimation behavior. In the case of objective per-36 turbation, the estimator can become denser than in the noiseless setting, even under the same regularization strength. This property can lead to improved generalization perfor-mance compared to the noiseless case, particularly for sufficiently large λ and appropriately chosen noise levels. We analyzed the distributional properties of the estimator with respect to privacy noise using asymptotic analysis based on approximate message passing (AMP) and its associated state evolution. This framework allowed us to derive a statistically equivalent characteriza-tion of the AMP estimator and to quantify how the privacy level, measured by cwOnAveKL, depends on the noise strength and model parameters. Our analysis revealed several notable features. First, the privacy level is not a mono-tonic function of the objective perturbation noise. While adding output noise consistently improves privacy, excessively large objective noise can instead increase cwOnAveKL. This counterintuitive behavior arises from instability in the estimator, as captured by AMP, which is closely related to coordinate descent dynamics, and is reflected in increased gen-eralization error. In such regimes, the estimator becomes highly sensitive to data pertur-bations, and the privacy protection deteriorates. Second, cwOnAveKL depends strongly on the underlying model parameters. In partic-ular, weaker regularization, lower sampling ratios, and denser true signal tend to increase cwOnAveKL. These findings suggest that privacy is harder to maintain precisely in regimes where statistical estimation itself is challenging, while sparsity can play a beneficial role in enhancing privacy. To better understand the trade-off between prediction accuracy and privacy, we exam-ined the relationship between cwOnAveKL and the generalization error by treating the privacy noise as a parametric variable. In the case of output perturbation, the resulting curve exhibits an inverse relationship, whereas for objective perturbation it becomes non-monotonic. This indicates that moderate noise levels can simultaneously improve prediction 37 performance and provide privacy protection, while excessive noise degrades both. Based on this observation, we introduced the notion of an optimal privacy noise , defined as the noise strength that minimizes the distance to the origin in the generalization-cwOnAveKL plane. This point represents a balance between prediction accuracy and privacy. We fur-ther found that the optimal noise decreases monotonically with λ for output perturbation, while it exhibits non-monotonic behavior for objective perturbation. Moreover, objective perturbation tends to better preserve prediction performance, whereas output perturbation provides stronger privacy protection in terms of cwOnAveKL. Overall, our results highlight that privacy protection through noise injection must be carefully calibrated. Increasing objective noise does not necessarily improve privacy and may instead induce estimator instability and degrade prediction performance. The pro-posed framework provides a quantitative approach to analyzing this trade-off and offers guidance for selecting noise levels in high-dimensional statistical models. Future work includes extending this analysis to broader classes of models and inference methods, as well as to other privacy mechanisms beyond output and objective perturba-tion. In particular, it would be important to investigate data-dependent noise calibration strategies and to validate these findings in more complex statistical settings. Connections to differential privacy mechanisms such as output perturbation, objective perturbation, and gradient-based methods [Abadi et al., 2016] provide a promising direction for further study.
