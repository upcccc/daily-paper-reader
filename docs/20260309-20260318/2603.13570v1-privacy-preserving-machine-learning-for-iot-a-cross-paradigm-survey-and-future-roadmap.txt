# Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning for IoT: A Cross-Paradigm Survey and Future Roadmap 

Zakia Zaman, Student Member, IEEE, Praveen Gauravaram, Member, IEEE, Mahbub Hassan, Senior Member, IEEE, Sanjay Jha, Senior Member, IEEE, and Wen Hu, Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —The rapid proliferation of the Internet of Things (IoT) has intensified the demand for robust Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML) mechanisms capable of safeguard-ing sensitive data generated by large-scale, heterogeneous, and resource-constrained devices. Unlike centralised learning environ-ments, IoT ecosystems are inherently decentralised, bandwidth-limited, and latency-sensitive, exposing privacy risks across sensing, communication, and distributed training pipelines. These characteristics render conventional data anonymisation and centralised protection strategies insufficient. This survey presents a comprehensive, IoT-centric and cross-paradigm anal-ysis of PPML. We introduce a structured taxonomy spanning perturbation-based mechanisms (e.g., Differential Privacy (DP)), distributed learning paradigms (e.g., Federated Learning (FL)), cryptographic computation (e.g., Homomorphic Encryption (HE) and Secure Multi-Party Computation (SMPC)), and generative data synthesis (e.g.,Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)). For each paradigm, we examine formal privacy guarantees, computa-tional and communication complexity, scalability under heteroge-neous device participation, and robustness against major attack vectors including Membership Inference Attack (MIA)s, model inversion, gradient leakage, reconstruction, and adversarial ma-nipulation. We further analyse deployment constraints in wireless and distributed IoT settings, highlighting trade-offs between privacy budgets, communication overhead, model convergence, and system efficiency in emerging 5G/6G-enabled architectures. In addition, we consolidate evaluation methodologies across privacy, utility, and robustness dimensions, curate representative datasets and open-source frameworks, and identify unresolved challenges such as hybrid DP–cryptographic integration, energy-aware FL, privacy-preserving Large Language Model (LLM)s, and quantum-resilient learning. By bridging fragmented research across paradigms and grounding privacy mechanisms within practical IoT deployment constraints, this survey establishes a rigorous analytical foundation and forward-looking research roadmap for scalable and trustworthy PPML systems. 

Index Terms —Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning, Bloom-Filter-Encoding, Differential Privacy, Federated Learning, GAN.

The Internet of Things (IoT) is increasingly embedded in modern life, enabling applications across healthcare, smart homes, industrial automation, and energy management. These interconnected ecosystems depend on continuous data collec-tion, processing, and communication, often involving highly sensitive information such as biometric signals, audio streams, geolocation traces, and energy usage profiles. Privacy preservation in IoT environments remains highly challenging. PPML has emerged as a promising approach to embed privacy protections directly into the Machine Learning (ML) pipeline. Core paradigms include DP, FL [4], cryptographic schemes such as HE and SMPC, and generative models including GAN and LLM. While each of these techniques has been extensively studied, their adaptation to IoT environments introduces unique challenges, especially around scalability, adversarial robustness, and regulatory compliance. This survey presents a unified analysis of PPML meth-ods tailored to IoT, bridging fragmented research across perturbation-based methods, distributed learning, crypto-graphic computation, and synthetic data generation. We pro-pose a structured taxonomy, map adversarial threats to corre-sponding defences, consolidate evaluation methodologies, and identify open research gaps. Among recent surveys, this work uniquely emphasises an IoT-first, cross-paradigm perspective on PPML, with particular focus on lightweight, scalable, and practically deployable solutions tailored to adversarially exposed IoT environments.

B. Paper Selection Methodology 

This survey reviews over 200 publications spanning 2014 to 2025, comprising peer-reviewed journal and conference pa-pers, arXiv preprints, and technical reports; a small number of pre-2014 foundational works are included for theoretical con-text. Literature was retrieved from IEEE Xplore, ACM Dig-ital Library, SpringerLink, arXiv, USENIX, NeurIPS/ICML Proceedings, and PETs/PoPETs. Searches combined the fol-lowing keywords: “privacy-preserving machine learning” ,

“differential privacy” , “federated learning” , “homomorphic encryption” , “secure multi-party computation” , “generative adversarial network” , “synthetic data generation” , “IoT pri-vacy” , “membership inference attack” , “gradient leakage” ,and “Bloom Filter encoding” , combined via AND/OR opera-tors (e.g., “federated learning AND differential privacy AND IoT” ). Papers were included if they: (i) proposed or evaluated PPML methods in IoT or distributed settings; (ii) analysed privacy–utility trade-offs formally or empirically; or (iii) ad-dressed system-level constraints such as latency, energy ef-ficiency, or regulatory compliance. Preprints and technical reports were retained where they represented seminal con-tributions. Papers addressing privacy in non-ML contexts or lacking empirical or formal evaluation were excluded. Se-lected literature was organised thematically across DP, DP-BF-Encoding, FL, cryptographic approaches, synthetic data gen-eration, adversarial threats, evaluation metrics, datasets, and implementation frameworks. 3

F. Cross-Paradigm Taxonomy of PPML for IoT 

We present a structured cross-paradigm taxonomy of PPML techniques for IoT, organising existing approaches according to their underlying privacy-preserving mechanisms and de-ployment characteristics in resource-constrained environments. The taxonomy, illustrated in Fig. 2, delineates four principal methodological families: perturbation-based approaches, dis-tributed learning paradigms, cryptographic protocols, and gen-erative model–based techniques. Each family reflects a distinct strategy for mitigating privacy risks while accommodating the computational, communication, and energy constraints inherent to IoT systems. Perturbation-based methods are grounded in statistical noise injection, most prominently through DP mechanisms. By introducing calibrated randomness at the input, feature, or gradient level, via Laplace or Gaussian mechanisms, Local Differential Privacy (LDP), Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD), or probabilistic encoders such as DP-BF-Encoding, these approaches provide formal (ε, δ )-DP guarantees. Their lightweight computational profile makes them particularly suitable for edge devices and embedded IoT nodes. However, privacy protection is governed by the privacy–utility trade-off, requiring careful budget allocation to balance model performance against leakage risks such as Membership Inference (MI), attribute inference, and gradient leakage. The rapid convergence of ML and the IoT has enabled highly personalised, context-aware services across domains such as energy management, healthcare, and industrial automa-tion. However, IoT environments are inherently distributed ,

resource-constrained , and heterogeneous , often relying on untrusted networks and third-party services. This creates a complex privacy landscape in which sensitive data is con-tinuously generated, transmitted, and analysed at scale. This section introduces the privacy challenges in IoT-driven ML systems, illustrates how breaches can occur, and sets the stage for a systematic review of privacy-preserving solutions. We begin by contextualising IoT data pipelines, then highlight the inherent privacy–utility trade-off in ML, trace the evolution of privacy techniques, and summarise key paradigms that define the field of PPML. 

A. IoT System Context and Privacy Exposure 

IoT systems typically consist of pervasive sensing devices, edge gateways, and cloud-based analytics platforms. Fig. 3 illustrates a smart metering workflow, where energy readings are captured at high temporal resolution and transmitted for grid optimisation and dynamic billing. Comparable architec-tures exist in wearable healthcare, environmental monitoring, and industrial IoT applications. While data-driven pipelines in IoT ecosystems significantly enhance automation, personalisation, and real-time decision-making across sectors such as healthcare, smart homes, and urban infrastructure, they simultaneously introduce complex privacy risks. These risks arise from continuous data collec-tion, distributed processing, and collaborative learning, each increasing the vulnerability to adversarial exploitation. > •

> •

Traffic Analysis : Even encrypted communication patterns can disclose user activity schedules or device usage trends [20], [21]. These risks demonstrate that conventional anonymisation or encryption alone is insufficient in adversarial and decentralised IoT ecosystems. 6

B. Privacy Attack Taxonomy in Machine Learning 

The growing use of ML in IoT environments has expanded the attack surface for privacy breaches. Even when raw data is encrypted or anonymised, ML models may leak sensitive in-formation through their outputs, training dynamics, or internal representations. To understand and mitigate these risks, it is essential to classify privacy attacks by their behaviour, timing, and adversarial knowledge [22]. 

Passive privacy attacks involve adversaries who observe a model’s behaviour without modifying its training or inference process. These attacks are often performed during inference and are commonly referred to as “honest-but-curious” threats. By exploiting statistical signals from predictions or confidence scores, attackers can infer training data characteristics or user membership [23], [24]. When the adversary additionally has full visibility into model parameters and gradients, a white-box passive setting, attacks such as membership inference [23], model inversion [24], and data reconstruction [25], [26] become considerably more potent. Effective countermeasures in this setting include DP [27], gradient perturbation [28], regularisation to limit overfitting [29], confidence obfuscation, and restricting the retention of intermediate feature vectors [26]. 

Active privacy attacks , by contrast, involve adversaries who interfere with the ML pipeline. This includes injecting adver-sarial inputs, modifying queries, or manipulating training data to elicit specific behaviours or disclosures. Active attacks may occur during both training and inference, and are particularly damaging in collaborative or distributed settings [19], [30]. In the white-box active case, where the adversary has full access to model internals and can inject updates, threats such as gradient leakage [19] and data poisoning [31] are most severe. Defences in this regime include gradient clipping [32], secure aggregation [33], homomorphic encryption [34], and the application of DP during local training [27]. These attacks also vary by the level of knowledge the attacker possesses. Black-box adversaries have access only to model outputs, without visibility into internal parameters or gradients. In the black-box passive setting, attackers exploit output probabilities or confidence scores to mount output-based membership inference attacks [23] or property inference attacks [35]; corresponding defences include output pertur-bation via DP [36], model distillation [30], and confidence clipping [37]. In the black-box active setting, adversaries combine query access with the ability to craft and submit adversarial inputs, enabling model extraction [30], [38] and adversarial example generation [39]. Countermeasures in this case include query rate limiting [40], adversarial training [41], and input preprocessing [42]. 

C. Navigating the Privacy/Utility Trade-Off in Machine Learning 

As IoT systems increasingly rely on ML for prediction, classification, and control, they depend on high-resolution, real-time data. However, privacy-preserving constraints often necessitate transformations that degrade data quality, directly affecting model utility. This tension is not unique to energy systems; in healthcare, wearable device data must balance privacy with diagnostic precision, while in industrial IoT, production optimisation competes with confidentiality requirements. Consequently, de-signing PPML systems becomes a multi-objective optimisation 7

challenge, balancing the competing demands of privacy protec-tion, computational efficiency, and model performance across diverse IoT domains. 

D. Emergence of Privacy-Preserving Machine Learning (PPML) Paradigms 

To address the limitations of heuristic privacy techniques and mitigate the trade-offs outlined above, PPML has emerged as a promising paradigm that embeds privacy directly into learning and inference processes. Core paradigms include:  

> •

DP: A formal privacy framework that limits the influ-ence of individual data points by injecting calibrated noise [43].  

> •

FL: A distributed paradigm that keeps data local while collaboratively training global models [44], though still vulnerable to gradient leakage and poisoning.  

> •

Cryptographic Techniques: Schemes such as HE, SMPC, and TEE offer strong guarantees but face scalability and performance challenges [45].  

> •

Synthetic Data Generation: Generative models, including GAN and its DP-enhanced variants [46], [47], produce realistic data substitutes, enabling analytics and sharing while reducing disclosure risk. Collectively, these paradigms form the foundation of a new generation of privacy-preserving solutions, specifically adapted to the constraints and demands of IoT ecosystems. However, each approach presents inherent trade-offs, ranging from limited scalability and computational inefficiency to re-duced robustness in adversarial settings. In response, emerging research increasingly advocates for hybrid frameworks that integrate lightweight data encoding, formal privacy guarantees, and secure computation to strike a more practical balance. Building on this foundation, the following sections provide a structured analysis of traditional data privacy mechanisms (Section III), core PPML techniques (Section IV), adversarial threat models and corresponding defences (Section V), and the evaluation metrics used to assess their effectiveness (Sec-tion VI). III. T RADITIONAL DATA PRIVACY PARADIGMS 

Traditional data privacy mechanisms emerged in response to the increasing risk of re-identification in shared datasets. Domains such as healthcare, finance, and smart environments often relied on anonymisation [48] or probabilistic obfuscation [49], [50] to balance utility with confidentiality. However, most of these techniques were designed for centralised, structured datasets, and they struggle to meet the formal guarantees and operational constraints demanded by modern ML and IoT systems. This section reviews two foundational approaches: anonymisation and probabilistic data encoding, highlighting their underlying principles, practical applications, and limi-tations in the context of decentralised, high-frequency data environments. 

A. Anonymisation 

Anonymisation techniques aim to reduce identifiability by removing explicit identifiers (e.g., names, SSNs) and per-turbing quasi-identifiers (e.g., birth date, ZIP code). Classical approaches such as k-anonymity [51], l-diversity [52], and 

t-closeness [53] define indistinguishability criteria to ensure privacy under group-based transformations. In imaging appli-cations, methods like k-same [54], [55] average facial features across k individuals to generate synthetic, non-identifiable representations, forming the basis for privacy-preserving bio-metrics. 

Discussion: Although anonymisation techniques are com-putationally lightweight and conceptually intuitive, they ex-hibit well-documented vulnerabilities under adversarial con-ditions [56]. For example, auxiliary information and quasi-identifier correlation can render these defences ineffective [52], [57]. In IoT environments, where spatial and temporal data are essential to functionality, generalisation can severely degrade utility [58], [59], and the assumption of a trusted central curator introduces a potential single point of failure [60]. These limitations motivate the need for stronger, embedded privacy mechanisms. (See Section IX for a consolidated discussion of these limitations.) Despite these drawbacks, anonymisation remains influential in the design of lightweight, heuristic privacy solutions. However, alternative methods, particularly those based on probabilistic data transformations, offer acomplementary direction. 

B. Probabilistic Data Encoding: Bloom Filters 

Probabilistic encoding techniques, such as BF, have gained attention as lightweight and scalable alternatives for privacy-preserving record linkage in distributed environments [61], [62]. These structures encode categorical or string-based val-ues into fixed-length bit vectors using multiple hash functions, allowing approximate matching without direct disclosure of the original data [63]. This characteristic makes BF par-ticularly attractive in contexts requiring both computational efficiency and data minimisation. 

Discussion: While BF-based encoding offers a compu-tationally efficient approach to approximate record linkage, it lacks strong formal privacy guarantees and is susceptible to various inference attacks. Empirical studies have demon-strated the feasibility of recovering sensitive attributes—such as surnames—from Bloom filter encodings [64]. Moreover, recent adversarial methods exploit graph-based co-occurrence structures or bit-pattern frequency distributions to reverse-engineer encoded content [65], [66], [67]. These vulnerabilities are particularly pronounced in low-entropy domains such as personal names or geographic lo-cations, where attackers are likely to possess background knowledge. Enhancements like RAPPOR [68] incorporate ran-domised responses to counter frequency-based inference, but such methods remain largely experimental and lack validation in practical deployments. Consequently, probabilistic encodings such as BF highlight a broader trade-off in privacy engineering: they are simple, scalable, and effective for approximate matching, yet their re-silience against modern adversarial models is limited. Without rigorous adversary modelling and entropy-aware design, such techniques risk overestimating their privacy-preserving capa-bilities. Further discussion of future directions and mitigation strategies is provided in Section IX. 8

Anonymisation and BF-Encoding represent foundational techniques in the history of privacy-aware data processing. However, their heuristic nature and fragility in adversarial, high-dimensional, and decentralised IoT settings have led to growing interest in embedding privacy directly within the learning and inference process. (PPML) T ECHNIQUES 

PPML has gained considerable momentum in recent years, driven by increasing concerns over the exposure of sensi-tive data during model training and inference. A range of techniques has been developed to enable collaborative model training across multiple parties while preserving data confi-dentiality. These approaches primarily draw on cryptographic methods and perturbation-based mechanisms, such as DP [69]. The following subsections explore these core techniques, with particular emphasis on their relevance and applicability to IoT environments. Special attention is given to DP, widely regarded as the gold standard in data privacy, due to its formal privacy guarantees and practical adoption. Each paradigm discussed below corresponds to a primary branch in the cross-paradigm taxonomy (Fig. 2), with hybrid variants mapping to the interconnected sub-nodes. 

A. Differential Privacy (DP) 

DP offers a mathematically rigorous framework for safe-guarding individual data privacy by injecting carefully cal-ibrated noise into computations [70]. Unlike cryptographic methods, DP techniques are often more computationally ef-ficient, making them especially appealing for large-scale ML applications and resource-constrained IoT environments. At its core, DP ensures that the output of a computation does not significantly differ whether or not a particular data point is included in the input, thereby providing strong resistance against inference attacks.

B. Cryptographic Approaches 

Cryptographic methods constitute a cornerstone of PPML, enabling computations on distributed or encrypted data without exposing sensitive inputs. These techniques are particularly valuable in IoT environments where raw data must not be centralised due to privacy or regulatory constraints. The three dominant paradigms include HE, SMPC, and TEEs. Each provides strong formal guarantees, though with distinct trade-offs in scalability, latency, and trust assumptions. 1) HE enables arithmetic operations to be performed di-rectly on encrypted data, thereby ensuring that inter-mediate computations remain private. Fully Homomor-phic Encryption (FHE) supports arbitrary operations on ciphertexts [34], though at high computational cost; more lightweight schemes such as additive HE (e.g., Paillier) are often adopted in practice for efficiency. 9

Representative applications include privacy-preserving recommender systems [84] and secure gradient aggrega-tion in FL [85], [86], [87]. Despite these capabilities, HE imposes high computational overhead and slow encryp-tion/decryption cycles, rendering it unsuitable for real-time IoT scenarios where latency and energy budgets are tightly constrained. 2) SMPC allows multiple parties to jointly compute afunction without disclosing their private inputs [88]. Techniques such as Garbled Circuits (GC) [89] and Secret Sharing [90] form the basis of SMPC proto-cols, with applications spanning collaborative IoT data sharing, secure regression in distributed systems [91], and SMPC-based secure aggregation in FL [33], [92], [93]. However, SMPC protocols exhibit high communi-cation overhead and poor scalability for deep learning tasks, with limited efficiency for non-linear operations— constraints that are particularly prohibitive in bandwidth-and energy-constrained IoT networks. 3) TEEs (e.g., Intel SGX, AMD SEV) rely on hardware-enforced enclaves to securely isolate sensitive compu-tations from the host operating system, enabling con-fidential ML training and inference even on untrusted servers [45]. Recent IoT frameworks have integrated TEEs at the network edge to protect sensor-level com-putations and support enclave-based model execution in untrusted environments [94], [95]. Nevertheless, TEEs are constrained by limited secure memory, introduce centralised trust dependencies on hardware vendors, and remain vulnerable to side-channel and speculative execution attacks [96], [97]—concerns that are amplified in heterogeneous and long-lived IoT deployments. While these approaches provide strong privacy guarantees in distributed IoT environments, their limitations in terms of computational efficiency, scalability, and trust assumptions remain significant. We discuss these challenges in Section IX. 

C. Federated Learning (FL) 

FL [44], [98], [99], [100], [101] is a Distributed Machine Learning (DML) paradigm that enables multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared global model without exposing their raw data. In each communication round, a subset of participants trains the model locally using private data and transmits the updated parameters (e.g., gradient updates via SGD) to a central server. The server then aggregates these contributions, typically through weighted averaging (FedAvg), to update the global model [102]. While FL preserves data locality and reduces the risks associated with centralised data storage, it introduces new challenges. Local training imposes significant computational and energy overhead on client devices, particularly resource-constrained IoT nodes. Furthermore, recent work shows that gradients and model updates themselves can leak sensitive information about local training data [19]. To mitigate such risks, several privacy-preserving tech-niques are integrated into FL:  

> •

DP-enhanced FL: By injecting calibrated noise into local updates, DP mechanisms [83] prevent adversaries from confidently inferring membership or attribute informa-tion. For example, Wei et al. [103] and Ranaweera et al. 

[104] demonstrate DP-FL in distributed healthcare and IoT scenarios. Tang et al. [105] proposed a reputation-based, long-term contribution evaluation and aggregation framework for DP-based FL that detects and mitigates rogue clients injecting excessive DP noise, thereby pre-serving global model accuracy without compromising formal DP guarantees. Zhang et al. [106] propose a robust and secure FL framework with verifiable DP that simulta-neously guarantees formally provable DP compliance and Byzantine [107] resilience, preventing malicious clients from manipulating noise injection while preserving model accuracy under adversarial settings.  

> •

Secure Aggregation: Prior work, including [33], [108], proposes enhancing FL with secure aggregation proto-cols to ensure that the server observes only aggregated model updates rather than individual client contributions, thereby mitigating the risk of targeted inference attacks.  

> •

Cryptographic Enhancements: Homomorphic encryption and secure multi-party computation have also been in-corporated into FL. Aono et al. [109] proposed an HE-based approach where clients upload encrypted updates, which can still be aggregated without decryption. Boan 

et al. [110] proposed functional encryption enhanced FL method for resource constrained devices.  

> •

Hybrid approaches: Recent advances combine DP, cryp-tography, and lightweight compression schemes to reduce both privacy risks and communication overhead in IoT-FL [92], [111]. Despite its promise, FL presents several limitations in IoT deployments, discussed in Section IX.

However, this design incurs communication overhead and introduces gradient leakage risks during distributed training. To provide formal privacy guarantees, DP-GANs introduce calibrated noise during training via gradient perturbation [46], supporting privacy-aware synthetic IoT datasets for anomaly detection and predictive maintenance. While DP-GAN pro-vides rigorous (ϵ, δ ) guarantees, high privacy budgets lead to utility loss and vulnerability to mode collapse. PATE-GAN [47] takes a complementary approach, leveraging teacher-student training under the Private Aggregation of Teacher Ensembles (PATE) framework for safe sample generation in health-care IoT settings where privacy-sensitive attributes must be protected; its main constraint is the requirement for large, disjoint teacher datasets, which limits scalability in IoT en-vironments. VAE-GANs [121] combine Variational Autoen-coders with GANs to enhance sample diversity and pre-serve latent representations, producing synthetic IoT sensory data with improved distribution fidelity, though at higher computational cost and with persistent training instability. Finally, Transformer-based GANs [122], [123] integrate atten-tion mechanisms for stable training and sequence modelling, making them well-suited for smart grid demand forecasting and IoT anomaly detection with sequential synthetic data; this direction remains emerging, requiring large-scale data and compute, with robustness not yet well studied. Across all variants, GANs share common limitations: mode collapse, training instability, and susceptibility to member-ship inference attacks [124], [125]. Hybrid models such as DP-GAN and PATE-GAN mitigate but do not fully eliminate these risks, and privacy–utility trade-offs remain an open research challenge, discussed further in Section IX.

Despite their objective of protecting sensitive information, PPML systems remain susceptible to a variety of privacy threats. ML models in IoT pipelines are susceptible to sev-eral attack classes that exploit their internal or observable behaviour. Three prominent categories include reconstruction attacks, model inversion attacks, and membership inference attacks (MIAs) [22]. 

Reconstruction Attacks aim to recover original inputs from intermediate model representations. For instance, attackers may extract sensor readings, location patterns, or even recon-structed images from latent feature embeddings—bypassing standard anonymisation techniques and undermining user pri-vacy [26], [152], [153], [154], [155]. 

Model Inversion Attacks exploit outputs—such as class probabilities or confidence scores—to reconstruct sensitive input attributes. These attacks can succeed even with par-tial black-box access, allowing attackers to infer protected attributes like medical conditions, device usage, or household presence [18], [24], [156], [157], [158]. 

MIA s attempt to determine whether a specific data sample was included in a model’s training set [23], [159], [160], [161], [162]. While this may seem benign, in sensitive con-texts—such as healthcare, finance, or smart metering, it can expose an individual’s participation in confidential datasets, breaching privacy without accessing raw data. To systematically examine these threats, we adopt a taxon-omy (Sec. II-B) based on the adversary’s capabilities ( white-box vs. black-box ) and intent ( passive vs. active ) [161], [163], [164]. This framework facilitates the classification and com-parison of prominent attacks and their countermeasures. The following subsection discusses the privacy attack taxonomy in ML. Among these, two categories, MIAs and adversarial attacks, stand out due to their prevalence in literature and practical significance in IoT-based PPML. MIAs can be broadly classified into two categories: 1) Black-box attacks: where the adversary only has query access to the model and utilises output probabilities or confidence vectors to distinguish members from non-members [23], [165]. 2) White-box attacks: where the attacker has access to the internal model parameters, gradients, or loss functions, allowing for more precise inferences [166]. To enhance the effectiveness of these attacks, adversaries frequently employ shadow models that are trained to mimic the target model’s behaviour on surrogate data. This data may be generated using three main techniques: model inversion attacks [24], statistics-based synthesis [167], or by adding noise to real data [168]. 

B. Model Inversion Attack 

Model inversion attacks refer to techniques that reconstruct sensitive input attributes by exploiting model outputs, such as prediction probabilities or confidence scores. These attacks can succeed even with partial black-box access, allowing attackers to infer protected attributes like medical conditions, device usage, or household presence [18], [24], [156], [157], [158]. These attacks are especially dangerous when models overfit or exhibit high confidence on training samples. For example, inversion on softmax outputs can recover an average face representation for a target class. 

C. Reconstruction Attack 

Reconstruction attacks refer to methods that recover original inputs from intermediate model representations, such as latent feature vectors. By exploiting stored or exposed embeddings, adversaries can reconstruct raw inputs like sensor readings or images, compromising user confidentiality [26], [152], [153], [154], [155], [169]. An attacker with access to these feature vectors can exploit them to reconstruct substantial portions of the original training data, threatening data confidentiality. 12 

D. Gradient Leakage Attack 

Gradient leakage attacks refer to techniques that exploit gradients exchanged during distributed training to reconstruct original inputs. Unlike model inversion attacks, which rely on model outputs, gradient leakage enables precise input recovery even without prediction scores. Introduced by Zhu et al. [19], these attacks pose a critical privacy risk in FL [170], [171], and IoT systems where gradients are frequently shared between devices and servers. The attack typically involves solving an optimisation problem that searches for an input–label pair whose gradient best matches the observed gradient from the client. Techniques such as iDLG (Improved Deep Leakage from Gradients) [172] improve both convergence and recon-struction quality, enabling adversaries to extract data from as little as a single shared gradient step. This threat is particularly acute in IoT-driven FL, where constrained edge devices may inadvertently leak sensitive sensor or biometric information through gradient updates, despite never transmitting raw data. 

E. Adversarial Attack 

Adversarial attacks [173] involve the deliberate manipu-lation of input data through carefully crafted perturbations designed to mislead ML models into making incorrect predic-tions. These perturbations are often imperceptible to humans but can cause significant degradation in model performance, highlighting the fragility of modern ML systems. Although adversarial attacks are traditionally viewed as threats to model integrity and availability, they can also be leveraged to in-fer information about the training data, thereby intersecting with privacy concerns. A widely studied example is the Fast Gradient Sign Method (Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)) [173], which perturbs input data along the gradient direction to maximise the model’s prediction error. The implications of adversarial attacks become even more severe in real-world IoT applications. For instance, in electric load forecasting [174], [175] systems, where ML models predict future energy consumption using time-series data from smart sensors, adversarial perturbations can lead to inaccu-rate demand predictions [176]. Such disruptions may cause resource misallocation, increased operational costs, or even grid-level instabilities. Although these attacks primarily com-promise functionality, their relevance in the context of PPML stems from the shared requirement for resilient, trustworthy models in environments that handle sensitive or critical infor-mation. 

F. Defence Mechanisms 

1) Defences against MIA refer to strategies that reduce the distinguishability of training and non-training sam-ples. A prominent approach is DP, which introduces calibrated noise to model training and provides formal guarantees against membership leakage [27], [42]. Reg-ularisation techniques, such as dropout and weight de-cay, also reduce overfitting, thereby limiting information leakage [177]. Another effective method is prediction sanitisation, where output confidences are clipped or replaced with class labels, reducing the adversary’s abil-ity to detect membership [37]. Despite these defences, balancing privacy preservation with utility remains an unresolved challenge. 2) Defences against model inversion attacks refer to mech-anisms that restrict or obscure the model’s output infor-mation. A common countermeasure is enforcing strict black-box access, where the model exposes only class labels rather than detailed probabilities [24]. Confidence obfuscation and output sanitisation can further reduce leakage risks. Additionally, incorporating DP during training can prevent overconfident predictions that fuel inversion attacks [18]. 3) Defences against reconstruction attacks refer to tech-niques that prevent adversaries from recovering raw data from intermediate representations. One effective approach is avoiding the storage of explicit feature embeddings in deployed models [26]. Access control over internal model parameters also reduces reconstruc-tion opportunities. Finally, embedding obfuscation or differentially private training can distort latent features, making accurate recovery infeasible [152], [154]. 4) Defences against gradient leakage attacks refer to meth-ods that reduce the risk of input recovery from shared gradients. Gradient clipping and noise injection limit the precision of inversion [27], [32], while secure aggrega-tion protocols ensure that individual gradients are never directly exposed [33]. Other strategies include larger batch sizes and shuffling, which introduce ambiguity in gradient origins [178]. These techniques are particularly crucial for FL in IoT systems, where sensitive data is commonly shared through gradient updates. 5) Defences against adversarial attacks refer to methods de-signed to improve model robustness against maliciously crafted perturbations. Adversarial training, where mod-els are exposed to adversarial examples during training, is one of the most effective approaches [41], [179], [180]. Other strategies include gradient masking, input preprocessing, and certified defences that provide formal robustness guarantees. However, many of these tech-niques introduce significant computational overhead, are task-specific, or fail under adaptive adversaries [181]. As a result, developing resilient defences for adversarial ro-bustness remains an open research direction, particularly in IoT-based PPML systems. VI. E VALUATION METRICS IN PPML Evaluation in PPML requires simultaneous assessment of privacy guarantees and model utility, given the inherent pri-vacy–utility trade-off. Existing approaches can be grouped into three complementary categories: (i) formal privacy guarantees, (ii) empirical attack-based evaluation, and (iii) robustness analysis. 

A. Privacy Metrics 1) Formal Guarantees: The standard formal metric is the DP privacy budget ϵ, which bounds the distinguishability 13 

between neighbouring datasets. Smaller ϵ implies stronger privacy but typically reduces model accuracy. In practice, acceptable ϵ values are application-dependent, ranging from 

< 1 in statistical analysis to ≤ 10 in deep learning settings [72]. The effective privacy–utility balance further depends on dataset scale and model capacity. 

2) Empirical Privacy Risk: Since formal guarantees may not reflect practical leakage, empirical evaluation against MIAs [23] is widely adopted. Common indicators include: (i) MIA accuracy, (ii) attack success rate (ASR), and (iii) Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC)–Area Under the Curve (AUC), where an AUC of 0.5 denotes random guessing and higher val-ues indicate increased leakage [131]. These metrics quantify adversarial inference capability under realistic threat models. 

3) Robustness-Linked Metrics: Adversarial robustness is increasingly considered a proxy for privacy sensitivity [25], [182]. Metrics such as adversarial success rate (e.g., under FGSM [173]) and perturbation norms assess model sensitivity to input manipulations. High sensitivity may correlate with exploitable information leakage. 

B. Utility Metrics 

Utility evaluation measures predictive or generative perfor-mance under privacy constraints. For classification, accuracy and F1-score remain standard, with F1 preferred under class imbalance. For regression and forecasting, Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) quantify deviation from ground truth, with RMSE penalising large errors more heavily. For synthetic data in PPML, evaluation focuses on distri-butional fidelity, temporal consistency, and downstream task transferability. Widely used metrics include Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for statistical similarity [183], [184], Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) for temporal alignment [185], and Train on Synthetic, Test on Real (TSTR) for task-level generalisation [118]. Cov-erage–density metrics assess diversity and realism [186], while Fr´ echet Inception Distance (FID) remains relevant for image-based benchmarks [187]. Overall, effective benchmarking in PPML requires joint reporting of privacy parameters, empirical leakage measures, and task-level utility to characterise the full privacy–utility landscape. VII. IX. O PEN ISSUES , O PPORTUNITIES , AND FUTURE TRENDS IN PPML FOR IOTAlthough PPML has advanced significantly, a number of challenges remain for deployment in resource-constrained and heterogeneous IoT environments. This section synthesises open issues, emerging opportunities, and future research trends across privacy mechanisms, model training paradigms, emerg-ing architectures, and deployment governance. 

A. Privacy Mechanism Challenges in IoT 1) DP-Based Mechanisms: Challenges Across Deployment Contexts: DP provides strong formal guarantees but faces distinct challenges at each stage of the IoT pipeline. At the 

input level , local perturbation requires careful calibration to balance utility against energy, computation, and communi-cation costs; systematic approaches tailored to IoT-specific workloads remain limited despite recent adaptive noise alloca-tion strategies [202]. At the training level , DP-SGD assumes access to raw or minimally processed data, conflicting with decentralised IoT scenarios where data ownership and regula-tory constraints prohibit aggregation. At the prediction level ,output perturbation incurs minimal training overhead but does not protect model parameters or intermediate representations, limiting its effectiveness in long-lived IoT inference pipelines. When extended to synthetic data generation , DP introduces additional tensions. Differentially private variants such as DP-GAN [46], [149] and PATE-GAN [47] provide formal guarantees but degrade temporal fidelity, which is critical for IoT tasks such as energy forecasting, healthcare monitoring, and anomaly detection [203], [204], [205]. Generative models also remain prone to mode collapse, suppressing minority patterns that correspond to rare but operationally critical IoT events [125]. Furthermore, existing benchmarks primarily target visual or tabular datasets, providing limited insight into time-series or multi-modal IoT data [117], [206]. 

Future research should prioritise hybrid DP frameworks combining input-level privatisation with lightweight encod-ing or secure aggregation, with adaptive privacy budgets that account for data sensitivity and device-level constraints. For synthetic data, hybrid generative architectures such as Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE)–GAN [121] and transformer-based models [122] offer improved temporal modelling under integrated DP constraints. Integrated evaluation frameworks that jointly assess formal privacy guarantees, disclosure risk, and downstream utility are essential for practical IoT adoption [207]. 

2) Residual Challenges in Cryptographic PPML for IoT: 

Cryptographic PPML techniques provide strong theoretical guarantees but face substantial deployment barriers in IoT systems. HE enables computation on encrypted data but in-troduces prohibitive latency from ciphertext expansion and homomorphic evaluation, with memory and energy footprints exceeding typical microcontroller-class devices [208], [209], [210]. SMPC eliminates reliance on trusted central authorities but exhibits poor scalability for DL, with high communication overhead for non-linear operations in bandwidth-constrained IoT networks [92], [211], [212]. TEEs reduce cryptographic overhead through hardware-isolated enclaves but are con-strained by limited secure memory, vendor trust dependencies, and susceptibility to side-channel and speculative execution attacks [96], [97]. 

Future research should develop hybrid frameworks combin-ing cryptographic protection with perturbation-based methods such as DP to reduce overhead, harden TEE deployments through runtime attestation and enclave monitoring, and in-tegrate post-quantum cryptographic primitives for long-lived IoT systems. 

3) Probabilistic Encoding with DP-BF for IoT: DP-BF-Encoding offers a lightweight privacy mechanism well-suited to resource-constrained IoT devices [68], [130], [131], [213]. However, compatibility with end-to-end deep DL architec-tures remains limited, standardised benchmarks for compar-ing DP-BF against alternative PPML techniques are absent, and evaluations predominantly focus on smart metering with limited coverage of mobility, localisation, or ambient sens-ing domains. Future work should develop adaptive encoding mechanisms that dynamically adjust the privacy budget ( ϵ)based on device capabilities and workload, integrate DP-BF into FL workflows to limit gradient reconstruction risk, and extend validation across diverse IoT domains. 

B. Model Training Paradigms 1) Federated, Transfer, and Split Learning: Privacy and De-ployment Challenges: FL preserves data locality but remains vulnerable to gradient leakage [19], and integrating DP into FL pipelines can significantly impair convergence under the non-IID data conditions prevalent across heterogeneous IoT devices. Uniform privacy budget allocation exacerbates this by ignoring differences in data sensitivity and device capability, while system heterogeneity complicates synchronisation and straggler management [44]. FTL extends FL by enabling knowledge reuse across tasks and domains without centralising data, but introduces addi-tional privacy risks: transferred representations may encode sensitive patterns from source domains, and domain mismatch can reduce the usefulness of DP-protected representations. Cold-start conditions further limit standard FL effectiveness when devices lack sufficient local data. Split Learning (SL) [214] and its variants such as SplitFed [215] reduce device-side computation by transmit-ting intermediate activations, but these activations can leak 15 

sensitive information depending on the split configuration [6], [216]. Early split points expose features correlated with raw sensor data, while deeper splits shift computational bur-den back to resource-constrained devices. TinyML [217], [218], [219] enables on-device inference and minimises data transmission but lacks runtime support for adaptive privacy accounting, making static and overly conservative privacy configurations common. 

Future research should pursue unified edge-centric frame-works integrating FL, SL, and TinyML, with lightweight privacy mechanisms such as DP-BF-Encoding or activation perturbation at split interfaces. Adaptive privacy budgeting, privacy-preserving knowledge distillation, and cross-layer op-timisation jointly considering privacy, communication effi-ciency, and learning performance will be essential for scalable IoT deployment [220]. 

C. Emerging Architectures and Analytics 1) Privacy-Aware Foundation Models and Multimodal An-alytics for IoT: Foundation models, including LLMs, vision-language models, and multimodal transformers, are increas-ingly adapted for IoT analytics through numerical tokeni-sation, sequence prompting, and cross-modal representation learning [221], [222], [223]. Their ability to generalise across tasks and domains with minimal labelled data is particularly valuable in IoT environments where sensing configurations evolve continuously [224], [225], [226]. However, fine-tuning on sensitive domain-specific data—capturing energy usage, mobility trajectories, or physiological signals—can implicitly encode personal patterns recoverable through membership in-ference, data extraction, or prompt-based attacks [227], [228]. Multimodal architectures amplify this risk by enabling cross-modal re-identification even when individual streams appear privacy-safe in isolation [229]. Applying DP during fine-tuning degrades long-range temporal dependencies critical for IoT time-series tasks, and the computational footprint of these models limits on-device deployment [137], [230]. Cross-domain generalisation further compounds these chal-lenges: distributional shifts across IoT domains amplify DP-induced utility loss, and adversarial threat models differ across domains—biometric inference in healthcare versus load dis-aggregation in smart grids, complicating universal defence design. Naively reusing privacy budgets across tasks risks cumulative leakage, while domain adaptation can inadvertently expose sensitive features from source domains. Future research should explore modality-specific sensitivity allocation within transformer architectures [231], combine personalised FL with secure aggregation for distributed foun-dation model fine-tuning [232], establish systematic post-deployment auditing including cross-modal linkage probes and membership inference stress tests [233], and develop cross-domain privacy accounting mechanisms that track cumulative privacy loss during model reuse. IoT-specific benchmarking suites spanning multiple tasks, modalities, and deployment tiers remain urgently needed. 

2) Quantum-Aware Privacy for IoT: Quantum Machine Learning (QML) promises computational advantages for optimisation-heavy IoT workloads but introduces distinct pri-vacy risks [234], [235], [236]. Most practical QML systems rely on hybrid quantum–classical pipelines where intermediate states exchanged between classical and quantum components may expose sensitive representations. Classical DP is analysed under classical adversarial assumptions, but quantum-capable adversaries may leverage search acceleration and information amplification to weaken existing protections, a critical concern for IoT datasets requiring long-term confidentiality, such as healthcare records and smart grid measurements. Post-Quantum Differential Privacy (PQ-DP) has emerged to address this threat, but its deployment in resource-constrained IoT systems remains challenging: lattice-based post-quantum primitives incur substantial memory and computational over-head unsuitable for microcontroller-class devices, and com-position rules for classical–quantum hybrid pipelines remain unresolved [237], [238]. Future research should develop lightweight PQ-DP mechanisms emphasising local perturba-tion at the data source, quantum-aware privacy accounting and composition rules, and integration of PQ-DP with post-quantum secure FL combining lattice-based encryption with DP for end-to-end quantum-resilient PPML pipelines. 

D. Deployment, Governance, and Lifecycle Challenges 

Translating PPML into real-world IoT deployments requires a shift from mechanism-centric design toward system-level governance. Most techniques are evaluated in isolation, but deployed systems involve multiple coexisting privacy mech-anisms interacting across sensing, communication, learning, and inference stages—interactions that can introduce unin-tended leakage or utility degradation. Privacy budgets must be treated as managed system resources requiring runtime monitoring and policy-driven adaptation, with such adaptation remaining transparent and auditable to avoid introducing new side channels. As IoT analytics increasingly fuse data across devices, modalities, and administrative domains, privacy loss can accumulate across tasks and time; governance frameworks must therefore track cumulative exposure at the system level rather than individual components. Regulatory frameworks such as GDPR and HIPAA ad-ditionally impose accountability, transparency, and explain-ability requirements that many PPML techniques lack built-in support for, necessitating logging, provenance tracking, and post-hoc analysis tools. Over longer timescales, models are retrained, devices replaced, and regulatory interpretations revised—requiring lifecycle-aware strategies for safe model updates, controlled budget renewal, and periodic privacy risk reassessment. In next-generation IoT systems operating over 5G and 6G infrastructures [239], privacy preservation becomes intrinsi-cally a communications systems design problem. Over-the-Air Computation (AirComp) [240] enables efficient FL aggrega-tion [241] by exploiting wireless channel superposition, but injected DP noise interacts with channel distortion and fading effects in ways that remain poorly characterised. Low-power wide-area networks (LPWAN) such as LoRaWAN and NB-IoT impose sub-kilobit-per-second uplinks and duty-cycle con-straints incompatible with most existing PPML frameworks, 16 

requiring joint optimisation of update compression, com-munication scheduling, and privacy calibration [242], [243]. Additionally, PPML traffic patterns—gradient exchanges and synthetic dataset dissemination, introduce identifiable signals that enable side-channel attacks, extending privacy require-ments to slice-level resource isolation and traffic shaping in 5G/6G network slicing architectures [244]. 

Future research should develop integrated governance frameworks bridging algorithmic privacy with operational con-trol, policy-aware privacy orchestration, system-level privacy accounting across components and time, and communication-theoretic privacy analysis for next-generation IoT network architectures. 

E. Summary and Outlook 

Across all paradigms examined, a consistent theme emerges: effective privacy preservation in IoT requires system-level thinking beyond isolated algorithmic guarantees. IoT con-straints on computation, energy, and communication funda-mentally limit the feasibility of theoretically sound mecha-nisms on heterogeneous, resource-constrained devices. The increasing use of foundation models, multimodal analyt-ics, and cross-domain learning introduces new risks—model memorisation, cross-modal inference, and cumulative privacy loss—that challenge existing threat models, further amplified by emerging quantum adversarial capabilities. The transition to real-world deployment reveals the central role of gov-ernance, accountability, and lifecycle management: privacy budgets must be auditable over time and aligned with evolving regulatory frameworks. Future work should prioritise holistic privacy architectures coordinating mechanisms across the data lifecycle, standardised benchmarks jointly evaluating privacy and utility under realistic conditions, and deployment-aware frameworks aligning technical guarantees with regulatory and societal expectations. 

F. Lessons Learned 

The cross-paradigm analysis presented in previous sections, together with the taxonomy in Fig.2 and comparative sum-maries in Tables (II, IV), yields several system-level insights that inform the practical design of PPML for IoT ecosystems. 

1) Cross-paradigm integration is necessary for robust IoT privacy. The taxonomy in Fig.2 demonstrates that perturbation-based methods (DP), distributed learning (FL), cryptographic protocols, and generative models address dis-tinct threat surfaces and deployment constraints. As shown in Table II, no single paradigm simultaneously provides for-mal privacy guarantees, strong adversarial robustness, high utility retention, and lightweight IoT deployability. DP offers quantifiable (ϵ, δ ) guarantees but introduces utility degradation under tight budgets (Section IV-A). FL reduces centralised exposure but remains vulnerable to gradient leakage and Byzantine behaviour (Section IV-C, Table IV). Cryptographic methods provide strong confidentiality independent of sta-tistical assumptions, yet incur substantial computational and communication overhead (Section IV-B). Generative models reduce raw data sharing but lack formal guarantees unless integrated with DP (Section IV-D). These observations suggest that hybrid architectures, such as DP-enhanced FL, secure aggregation with lightweight encryption, DP-enhanced data encoding [131] or DP-integrated generative synthesis [207], represent a necessary direction for scalable IoT deployment. 

2) The privacy–utility–efficiency trade-off is amplified in IoT settings. Section II-C established the inherent privacy– utility tension in ML systems. In IoT deployments, this ten-sion extends into a three-way optimisation problem involving privacy guarantees, predictive performance, and system effi-ciency. Lower ϵ values in DP increase noise magnitude; SMPC increases communication rounds; HE raises latency and mem-ory costs; and FL under heterogeneous, non-IID sensor data may slow convergence (Sections IV-A–IV-C). Because IoT devices are resource-constrained and often latency-sensitive, privacy-preserving mechanisms must be evaluated not only for formal guarantees but also for energy consumption, bandwidth overhead, and convergence stability (Table IV). Privacy in IoT cannot be treated as an add-on; it must be co-optimised with system constraints from the outset. 

3) Threat models and defences must be jointly engineered. 

The attack taxonomy in Section II-B highlights that privacy threats vary across white-box/black-box and passive/active ad-versaries. Section V further shows that reconstruction, model inversion, gradient leakage, and membership inference attacks exploit different aspects of the learning pipeline. A defence ef-fective against one threat class may leave exposure elsewhere, for example, DP mitigates membership inference but does not inherently prevent model extraction or adversarial manipula-tion; secure aggregation protects against server-side inference but not malicious client updates. Therefore, privacy mecha-nisms must be selected in alignment with explicitly stated adversary models, rather than applied generically. Adversary-aware co-design is particularly critical in distributed IoT environments where trust assumptions differ across devices, gateways, and cloud infrastructure. 

4) Evaluation practices must reflect realistic IoT deploy-ment conditions. Although Section VI consolidates formal, empirical, and robustness-based evaluation metrics, a recurring pattern across the surveyed literature is reliance on standard image benchmarks or resource-rich GPU environments. How-ever, Section II-A emphasise that IoT systems operate un-der heterogeneous, bandwidth-limited, and intermittently con-nected conditions. Techniques validated on MNIST [188] or CIFAR [192] like datasets may exhibit degraded performance when deployed on microcontroller-class hardware or under non-IID sensor distributions. Future PPML evaluation must therefore incorporate realistic IoT workloads, communication constraints, and long-duration operational testing to bridge the gap between laboratory validation and field deployment. 

5) Formal privacy guarantees and regulatory compliance are distinct objectives. The introduction situates IoT pri-vacy within regulatory frameworks such as GDPR, HIPAA, and PIPL. However, as discussed throughout Sections III - V, formal (ϵ, δ )-DP guarantees quantify statistical indis-tinguishability, whereas regulatory compliance concerns data minimisation, accountability, and purpose limitation. A DP-trained model is not automatically regulation-compliant, and 17 

a regulation-compliant system may still leak through infer-ence attacks. Effective PPML deployment therefore requires alignment between formal privacy definitions, system-level safeguards, and governance mechanisms. Bridging this tech-nical–legal gap remains an underexplored research direction. 

6) Privacy guarantees must be analysed over system life-cycles, not single training rounds. Section IV introduced DP and its (ϵ, δ ) guarantees, which compose under repeated queries and retraining. In continuous IoT deployments, where models are periodically retrained, federated rounds execute iteratively, and streaming data generates repeated queries, privacy loss accumulates according to composition properties. Reporting per-round ϵ values without accounting for long-term aggregation may substantially underestimate effective lifetime exposure. Sustainable IoT PPML therefore requires lifecycle-aware privacy accounting, including composition-aware privacy accountants, global budget tracking, and explicit retraining policies. Without such mechanisms, cumulative pri-vacy leakage may silently exceed acceptable bounds in long-lived deployments. 

7) Communication overhead is a first-order privacy–performance bottleneck. Section IV-C show that in distributed PPML systems, communication of gradients, model updates, or encrypted ciphertexts frequently dominates computational cost. In bandwidth-constrained IoT networks, this overhead directly affects convergence time, energy consumption, and privacy budget utilisation. Techniques such as compression, sparsification, and quantisation reduce communication load but interact non-trivially with DP noise injection and secure aggregation protocols. Joint optimisation of communication efficiency and privacy parameters is therefore essential for practical IoT-scale PPML. Collectively, these lessons underscore that trustworthy PPML for IoT requires cross-paradigm integration, adversary-aware system design, lifecycle-conscious privacy accounting, and evaluation methodologies grounded in realistic deploy-ment constraints. Privacy in IoT is not merely a property of algorithms; it is an emergent characteristic of end-to-end system design. X. C ONCLUSION 

PPML has evolved into a critical research frontier for IoT ecosystems, where data misuse risks intersect with constraints in compute, bandwidth, and deployment heterogeneity. This survey has presented a structured, cross-paradigm analysis spanning perturbation-based mechanisms (DP and DP-BF-Encoding), distributed learning (FL and its hybrid variants), cryptographic protocols (HE, SMPC, TEEs), and generative synthesis (DP-GAN, Transformer-based GANs). For each paradigm, we examined formal privacy guarantees, compu-tational trade-offs, adversarial robustness, and deployability under IoT resource constraints. A central finding is that no single paradigm simultaneously satisfies formal guarantees, adversarial robustness, high utility, and lightweight deploy-ability. Hybrid architectures combining DP-enhanced FL with secure aggregation, lightweight encoding, or differentially private generative synthesis represent a necessary direction. The survey further mapped major attack vectors, including MIAs, gradient leakage, model inversion, and reconstruction attacks, to corresponding defences, and consolidated evalua-tion metrics, benchmark datasets, and open-source frameworks to support reproducible research. Looking ahead, priority chal-lenges include energy-aware and TinyML-compatible privacy schemes, privacy-preserving LLM fine-tuning under non-IID IoT distributions, post-quantum DP mechanisms, lifecycle-aware privacy accounting across federated rounds and retrain-ing cycles, and governance frameworks bridging algorithmic guarantees with the accountability requirements of GDPR, HIPAA, and PIPL. We advocate for a privacy-by-design mindset embedded throughout the ML lifecycle, where data protection is a co-designed, auditable, and regulation-aligned cornerstone of scalable, trustworthy IoT systems.
