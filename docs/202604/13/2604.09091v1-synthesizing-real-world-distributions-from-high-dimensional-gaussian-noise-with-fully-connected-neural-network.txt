# Synthesizing real-world distributions from high-dimensional Gaussian Noise with Fully Connected Neural Network 

Joanna Komorniczak 

Department of Systems and Computer Networks ,Wrocław University of Science and Technology, Wrocław, Poland 

Abstract. The use of synthetic data in machine learning applications and research offers many benefits, including performance improvements through data augmentation, privacy preservation of original samples, and reliable method assessment with fully synthetic data. This work pro-poses a time-efficient synthetic data generation method based on a fully connected neural network and a randomized loss function that trans-forms a random Gaussian distribution to approximate a target real-world dataset. The experiments conducted on 25 diverse tabular real-world datasets confirm that the proposed solution surpasses the state-of-the-art generative methods and achieves reference MMD scores orders of magnitude faster than modern deep learning solutions. The experiments involved analyzing distributional similarity, assessing the impact on clas-sification quality, and using PCA for dimensionality reduction, which further enhances data privacy and can boost classification quality while reducing time and memory complexity. 

Keywords: synthetic data, data augmentation, privacy preservation, class imbalance, neural networks 

1 Introduction 

The commonness of data acquisition, transmission, and processing in the modern world has had a significant impact on the privacy and security of user data [6]. Data privacy has become a critical issue not only in medical applications [27] but also in many other domains, such as automatic transaction approval [56], the Internet of Things [2], and even across social media platforms [38]. Many of those sectors employ intelligent systems that involve machine learn-ing models trained on original, private data. Recent years have highlighted the vulnerabilities of machine learning models, revealing that private user data used in the training process can be extracted from the final system [10]. Shokri et al. [59] used Membership Inference Attacks to indicate whether a specific sample was involved in training a neural network model. The work on Inversion Attacks [21] has shown that patient genotype can be reconstructed from the trained model. Later, those attacks also demonstrated vulnerabilities in computer vision, en-abling the reconstruction of face images [74]. neural network gradients. Those works emphasize that machine learning models tend to memorize the training data [72]. New regulations address those issues, requiring the anonymization of private data [3]. While machine learning systems are deliberately intended to solve actual problems and process real-world datasets, the use of synthetically generated data has recently become a priority to prevent access to sensitive and private training data [31]. In addition to the benefits connected with the use of synthetic data in privacy preservation, many data augmentation techniques allow for the improvement of classification quality in imbalanced domains [13], the reduction of overfitting [60], or the extension of datasets for supervised learning tasks when the cost of human annotation or the acquisition of data is significant [33]. 

Contribution This work proposes the Distribution Mapping with Shuffled Opti-mization (DiMSO) – a synthetic data generation tool. Instead of encoding the original data into a latent space, as with Variational Autoencoders (VAE), the proposed DiMSO samples a high-dimensional Gaussian distribution N (0 , 1) and learns a mapping function that transforms this random Gaussian noise into a target distribution using a fully connected neural network architecture. The main contributions of the presented work include: 

– Proposition of a time-efficient method that employs a fully connected neural network and a randomized loss function to model real-world distributions. 

– Experimental evaluation of the proposed approach on 25 diverse real-world tabular datasets. 

– Comparison with traditional and deep-learning-based state-of-the-art gener-ators regarding (a) their ability to model the target distribution and (b) the influence on recognition performance, involving five classification algorithms. 

– Proposition of a processing pipeline that uses Principal Component Analysis (PCA) combined with synthetic data generation. 

– Comparison of the execution time of the proposed methodology with state-of-the-art deep learning synthetic data generation methods. Compared to the neural-network-based generative models in the literature, the proposed approach focuses on simplicity and time efficiency. The model’s fully connected architecture and a loss function based on absolute error between randomly selected pairs of samples enable efficient and effective data generation while increasing data privacy. 

2 Related works 

Synthetic data generation offers many benefits in today’s machine learning. The primary goals of synthetic data usage include augmentation to improve a model’s generalization ability [40], increasing the fairness of intelligent systems [58], and preserving data privacy [70]. Even though many modern generative solutions focus on generating unstructured data for computer vision or natural language processing [61], there are many applications where generating tabular datasets is highly advantageous [29]. 2.1 Data augmentation 

Modern machine learning, with recent advances in deep learning solutions, is strongly data-driven [8]. Meanwhile, many problems to which such sophisticated methods are intended to be applied suffer from data scarcity [53] or the high cost of sample acquisition, especially for rare events or minority classes [13]. Augmentation techniques supplement the learning dataset with additional samples, usually aiming to resist model overfitting or aid learning without the additional cost of data collection [44]. Augmentation techniques are frequently used in deep learning, especially for image classification. Basic image augmenta-tion techniques used in computer vision include rotation, translation, filtering, and contrast changes, but deep learning approaches such as GANs and adver-sarial training are also used [60]. In tabular data, augmentation is more challenging because the samples lack an invariant structure like images or text [18]. Tabular data often involves man-ual sample collection. The feature values may span a wide range, take various forms (e.g., categories), or exhibit complex dependencies [68]. Existing tabular data augmentation solutions may implement various mechanisms to achieve the initial goal of the augmentation procedure. A recent review notes that data aug-mentation aimed at balancing an initially imbalanced classification problem may involve different manipulations than when the goal is to improve the fairness of the trained model or increase the dataset size [20]. Histogram Augmentation Technique (HAT) [54] is a general-purpose tabular data augmentation technique that aims to preserve the original distribution of samples. This method applies different generation techniques depending on whether a specific feature has a continuous or discrete distribution. 

2.2 Class imbalance 

Many approaches to data augmentation focus on imbalanced problems and per-form oversampling to improve the representation of the minority class [25]. Im-balanced datasets pose challenges to canonical machine learning methods [39]. In cases of significant imbalance, the concept of an underrepresented minority class may be disregarded by the learning method. Given that the minority class is often more critical to recognize [9], e.g., in medical diagnostics or fraud detec-tion [56], many data augmentation solutions have been proposed to balance the dataset by synthesizing minority class examples. Among the canonical oversampling methods, one should mention the Syn-thetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) [9], which generates syn-thetic data by linearly interpolating minority-class samples. Throughout the years, SMOTE has become a standard approach to learning from imbalanced data, along with its extensions designed for specific purposes [19]. Even though the initial algorithm was proposed as a solution to improve classification quality due to class imbalance, some recent papers note that SMOTE and its variants may outperform deep generative approaches in the synthetic tabular data gener-ation task [68], demonstrating strong distributional similarity between real-world and synthetic samples. 4 Joanna Komorniczak 

However, SMOTE has certain limitations, as linear interpolations may fail to capture multimodal distributions or complex feature dependencies present in the original data [14]. Other research notes that the distribution of synthetic samples does not exactly follow the original minority class distribution, which may affect the classification performance [15]. SMOTE, although canonical, is not the only approach to imbalanced data oversampling. Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) [41] and Variational Au-toencoders (VAE) [16] are deep-learning-based solutions that have been shown to effectively balance classification problems and improve recognition performance. GAN comprises two neural network models – a generator and a discriminator [24]. The generator aims to produce synthetic samples that would be classified by the discriminator as real ones, drawn from the actual dataset. VAEs first encode the real-world data into a specific (usually Gaussian) distribution in a latent space and then generate data by sampling the embeddings and decod-ing them back to their original dimensionality [34]. Despite the limitations of these generative models (like mode collapse [63]), they are currently state-of-the-art in synthetic data generation. Later modifications of these methods, such as SB-GAN and SB-VAE [1], add additional steps to synthetic data generation. The authors of these modifications observed that the distribution of the minor-ity class is significantly affected by outliers and noise, and they demonstrated that carefully selecting the instances used to train the models improves their performance. Combining GAN architecture with the analysis of samples in the frequency domain was also an interesting oversampling solution to the problem of detecting fraud in credit card transactions [56]. 

2.3 Data privacy 

In addition to improving classification quality in the presence of data scarcity and class imbalance, synthetic data plays a significant role in the context of privacy preservation [32]. SMOTE is a simple and effective baseline for data augmentation; however, it has significant limitations regarding data privacy. Recent research has shown [23] that privacy attacks, such as the Membership Inference Attack, as well as DistinSMOTE and ReconSMOTE, designed specifically with the SMOTE baseline in mind, allow for the reconstruction of the original training samples. With the limitations of the baseline solution in mind, SMOTE-DP [75] com-bines SMOTE with a differentially private data generator. The presented study used the Bayesian network to satisfy differential privacy [73]. The authors of UMAP-SMOTENC [3] combine SMOTE-NC and UMAP projection to preserve the privacy of the data while efficiently synthesizing new samples. The solution compresses the samples into the two-dimensional space using Manifold Approx-imation and Projection [42] prior to performing synthetic sampling. In contrast to the primary motivation for using SMOTE, the datasets generated by UMAP-SMOTENC retain the original class proportions, hence, they may involve im-balance. As noted by the authors of UMAP-SMOTENC, the data sampling in reduced dimensionality can improve data privacy. A canonical dimensionality reduction method, Principal Component Analysis (PCA), can also transform the data into a lower-dimensional space to reduce the memory and computational cost of model training and improve classification quality [50]. While the primary compo-nents of original PCA tend to provide much information about original samples, some PCA modifications focus directly on preserving data privacy [22,17]. Among the traditional machine learning models, one should also mention the Avatar method [27], which generates a local model to produce synthetic data of mathematically simulated individuals. The method can use PCA or other projection techniques to compute pairwise distances between samples and define the local area. Similar to oversampling, many solutions for synthetic data generation with privacy preservation in mind rely on the use of GANs and VAEs [57]. The TableGAN method [46] uses GANs combined with convolutional neural net-works. TGAN additionally employs LSTM with an attention mechanism [71] to capture correlations between features. MedGAN [11] combines an auto-encoder and GANs to simulate realistic data for medical purposes. The current state-of-the-art methods for tabular data are Conditional Tabular GAN (CTGAN) and Tabular VAE (TVAE) [70], which, due to their efficient, open-source im-plementations [47], still serve as a baseline for data generation and comparison with reference approaches. The authors of these solutions challenge traditional generative models to capture the specifics of tabular data, such as mixed data types or non-Gaussian, multimodal distributions. Both CTGAN and TVAE use fully connected neural network structures. A solution directly focused on differential privacy is PATE-GAN [32], which replaces the discriminator component of the GAN with Private Aggregation of Teacher Ensembles (PATE), resulting in asymmetrical training involving the gen-erator, discriminator-teachers, and discriminator-students. DP-GAN [30] later introduced a third component of GAN, denoted as a differential privacy identi-fier, to ensure data privacy. Other solutions with a similar motivation combine CTGAN with controlled noise injection [2] Solutions based on VAEs have also been proposed. The Oblivious Variational Autoencoder (OVAE) [66] uses differentiable oblivious decision trees in both the encoder and decoder structures of the VAE. The decision trees are aggregated into layers to capture complex data distributions. GenerativeMTD [62], designed for small datasets, uses a VAE-GAN architecture trained on pseudo-real samples generated based on the original sample’s nearest neighbors. Tab-VAE [64] dis-tinguishes types of features before encoding the data and employs a variational Gaussian mixture model to capture multimodal distributions. TTVAE [69] em-ploys a transformer architecture instead of fully connected layers to capture complex feature dependencies. One of the drawbacks of those deep learning solutions is the memory com-plexity and computational cost involved in their training and employment [43]. Moreover, as is typical for deep learning solutions, they often require large train-6 Joanna Komorniczak 

ing datasets [68]. A good alternative is a more traditional statistical solution, e.g., Gaussian Copula [47], which models each continuous feature as a Gaussian distribution and each categorical feature as a Truncated Gaussian distribution. Synthpop [45] models conditional distributions of features using CART decision trees. 

2.4 Reliable method evaluation 

Synthetic data is also used to evaluate machine learning methods. Those datasets are usually fully synthetic or semi-synthetic – generated from real-world dis-tributions but with specific characteristics, e.g., the target problem complexity

Examples of fully-synthetic datasets include moons and circles [48], whose non-linear decision boundaries aim to challenge machine learning methods or motivate their utility – e.g., in uncertainty quantification [35] or clustering [55]. 3 Distribution Mapping with Shuffled Optimization 

This work presents a Distribution Mapping with Shuffled Optimization (DiMSO) generation tool for synthetic data generation that transforms random high-dimensional Gaussian noise into a target real-world distribution using a fully connected neural network. The optimization process uses a randomized loss func-tion, where the samples for loss calculation are selected at each optimization step. Experiment 3 – The time complexity comparison The last experiment focused on the time complexity of the method. Since many modern generative models employ deep learning – such as VAEs or GANs – their use often places significant computational demands. The proposed DiMSO uses a simple architecture of a fully connected network with three hidden layers and a loss function of low computational complexity. Compared with canonical deep learning models, the generation time with DiMSO is expected to be lower than that of reference complex architectures. In this experiment, we compared generation times for TVAE and CTGAN. The reference methods, configured as described in Table 2, were used to generate the synthetic distribution, aiming to simulate tabular synthetic Madelon datasets with four clusters and 20 features [28]. The datasets contained 100 samples, and the same number of samples was requested in the generation procedure. In this experiment, all generators aimed to match the unlabeled data – they treated all samples as one class, hence, separating the data into specific classes was unnecessary. The generation procedure was replicated 10 times, each time synthesizing a different random distribution. After the generation with TVAE and CTGAN, the target MMD of the gen-erated distribution was measured. The proposed DiMSO, parameterized with three different loss functions, aimed to achieve the MMD of the deep learning solution in up to 1000 epochs. Once the target similarity metric was achieved, the optimization with DiMSO was stopped, and the processing time was saved for comparison. The similarity metric was checked every 10 epochs. The experiments were conducted using a 7-core cpu with an arm architecture and a maximum clock rate of 3.2 GHz. Since all methods evaluated in this experiment optimize the distributions in iterative mode (epochs), setting a target distribution similarity with MMD will reliably assess the time of methods’ operation and discard the factor related to their capability to model real-world data. Since DiMSO is a simpler model in terms of architecture, it may require more epochs to achieve target similarity, however, the time per iteration should be lower compared to reference deep learning approaches.

6 Conclusions and Limitations 

This work proposes a DiMSO method for time-efficient synthetic data genera-tion with a fully connected neural network and a randomized loss function. The proposed approach transforms random Gaussian noise of higher dimensional-ity than the real-world dataset to match a target distribution while optimizing a specific criterion. The presented research evaluated the proposed method, hy-perparameterized with three different loss functions, across 25 real-world tabular datasets, achieving the best results with the shuffle-based Randomized Absolute Error (RAE) loss. The method achieved competitive results with state-of-the-art methods on metrics measuring distributional similarity between synthetic and original data. The experiments evaluating the classification performance showed the most significant benefit from synthetic data use when it was generated with DiMSO. The presented research used normalized tabular data, both in distribution modeling and classification tasks. The PCA features used in the research were extracted from the original unmodified features, including the categorical values. Future work will extend the proposed approach with pre-processing and post-processing of feature types, allowing for the maintenance of the semantic meaning of features. Even though RAE gave the best results, this loss function is particularly sensitive to direct matching to specific samples when the number of training iterations is large. It is worth exploring early stopping during DiMSO training to prevent overfitting and reduce the generation time. Future experiments can also study the relationship between the impact of the generated dataset size and class cardinalities on similarity and classification quality metrics.
