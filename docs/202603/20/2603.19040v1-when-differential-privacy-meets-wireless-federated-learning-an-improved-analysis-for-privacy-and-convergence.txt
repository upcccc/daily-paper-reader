## WHEN DIFFERENTIAL PRIVACY MEETS WIRELESS FEDERATED LEARNING: AN IMPROVED ANALYSIS FOR PRIVACY AND CONVERGENCE 

## Yaoling Chen ⋆, Hao Liang †,⋆ , Xiaotong Tu ⋆⋆School of Informatics, Xiamen University, China

## Internet of Things Thrust, Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (Guangzhou), China 

ABSTRACT 

Differentially private wireless federated learning (DPWFL) is a promising framework for protecting sensitive user data. However, foundational questions on how to precisely char-acterize privacy loss remain open, and existing work is fur-ther limited by convergence analyses that rely on restrictive convexity assumptions or ignore the effect of gradient clip-ping. To overcome these issues, we present a comprehensive analysis of privacy and convergence for DPWFL with general smooth non-convex loss objectives. Our analysis explicitly incorporates both device selection and mini-batch sampling, and shows that the privacy loss can converge to a constant rather than diverge with the number of iterations. Moreover, we establish convergence guarantees with gradient clipping and derive an explicit privacy-utility trade-off. Numerical re-sults validate our theoretical findings. 

Index Terms — Wireless federated learning, differential privacy, convergence analysis, privacy-utility trade-off. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a standard paradigm for distributed learning, enabling multiple devices to collab-oratively train a shared model without exchanging raw data [1]. With the growing prevalence of wireless networks, FL has been increasingly deployed over wireless channels, giving rise to wireless federated learning (WFL). A prominent ap-proach is over-the-air FL (AirFL) [2], which leverages the su-perposition property of radio-frequency waveforms to allow simultaneous transmission and efficient model aggregation. Despite these advantages, WFL remains vulnerable to infer-ence attacks, as sensitive information can still be revealed from the shared model parameters [3]. To address such privacy risks, differential privacy (DP) [4] has become the de facto standard for ensuring individual privacy, offering strict theoretical guarantees. The key idea of DP is to inject carefully calibrated randomness, typically in the form of additive noise, to mask the contribution of any

single data sample. This mechanism has also been adopted in WFL, yielding differentially private WFL (DPWFL). A natu-ral problem then arises: What is the privacy loss of DPWFL as a function of the number of iterations? 

Several works have attempted to answer this. Building on advanced composition [4], Liu and Simeone [5] provided privacy guarantees along with convergence analysis under smooth and Polyak-Lojasiewicz (PL) conditions. In [6], the effect of device and mini-batch sampling was analyzed, yield-ing privacy guarantees via R´ enyi DP (RDP) composition. Later, Park and Choi [7] exploited the inherent randomness of local gradients to tighten privacy bounds. More recently, Liu et al. [8] investigated DP in multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems using the moment accountant [9], and derived convergence guarantees for strong convexity and smooth objective. However, these approaches primarily rely on composition theorems or the moment accountant, both of which assume that all intermediate models are revealed during training and thus tend to over-estimate privacy loss. In practice, this leads to overly conservative noise injection, degraded model utility, and introduces unnecessary commu-nication overhead. This raises a key question: Can we more accurately track the privacy trajectory throughout training? 

While substantial efforts have been made to strengthen privacy guarantees by avoiding the release of intermediate re-sults [10, 11, 12], these methods often lead to privacy bounds that increase ad infinitum with iterations or require restrictive (strongly) convexity assumptions. To address this, Liang et al. 

recently showed that privacy loss can converge under general smooth objectives and established a tight convergence guar-antee in the centralized setting. However, its implications for wireless federated learning remain unexplored. Meanwhile, existing convergence analyses for DPWFL either ignore the critical role of gradient clipping (an essential mechanism for enforcing DP) or hinge on restrictive assumptions (e.g., con-vexity or bounded sample-wise gradient) which are often vio-lated in practice, especially in training deep neural networks. This motivates another fundamental question: How can we establish convergence guarantees for general non-convex ob-jectives while explicitly accounting for gradient clipping? 

The purpose of this paper is to address these questions. In particular, we provide a precise characterization of the pri-

vacy trajectory for DPWFL while incorporating both device selection and mini-batch sampling. By leveraging inherent channel noise, we establish rigorous privacy guarantees and prove that the privacy loss converges to a constant rather than growing unboundedly with iterations. In addition, we analyze the role of gradient clipping on the convergence under gen-eral smoothness assumptions, and derive an explicit privacy-utility trade-off. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: (i) we derive a precise privacy characterization for DPWFL by jointly incorporating device-level and data-level sampling, and prove that it can converge to a constant; (ii) we rigorously characterize the effect of gradient clipping on DP-WFL, and establish an improved privacy-utility trade-off for general non-convex smooth objectives; (iii) we experimen-tally validate our theoretical findings 1.

4. NUMERICAL RESULTS 

In this section, we numerically evaluate the privacy level ϵ

of the DPWFL with δ = 10 −5, and compare it against DP-NOMA [5], a representative baseline. Unless otherwise spec-ified, we set the device sampling rate p = 1 , mini-batch sam-pling rate q = 1 , clipping norm c = 2 , domain diameter 

D = 0 .5, smoothness constant L = 1 , channel noise scale 

σ = 10 , dataset size |D| = 8 , and step-size η = 0 .1.As shown in Fig.1 (a), smaller domain diameters D yield a smaller converged privacy loss, which is consistent with our theoretical analysis. Fig.1 (b) further shows that smaller device selection rates p or mini-batch sampling rates q may lead to smaller ϵ, indicating stronger privacy protection can be achieved by sampling, again matching our theory. Also, our privacy bound consistently outperforms DP-NOMA, demon-strating substantial improvements in privacy characterization. 

5. CONCLUSION 

In this paper, we provided a comprehensive analysis of pri-vacy and convergence for DPWFL. We showed that by lever-aging inherent channel noise and incorporating both device and mini-batch sampling, the privacy loss can converge to a constant. We further established the convergence result with gradient clipping, derived an explicit privacy–utility trade-off, and validated our theory with experiments. 6. REFERENCES
