## Abstract 

This paper establishes the strict optimality in precision for frequency estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). We prove that a frequency estimator with a symmetric and extremal configuration, and a constant support size equal to an optimized value, is sufficient to achieve maximum precision. Furthermore, we derive that the communication cost of such an optimal estimator can be as low as log 2( d(d‚ąí1)

In addition, we introduce a modified Count-Mean Sketch and demonstrate that it is practically indistinguishable from theoretical optimality with a sufficiently large dictionary size (e.g., d = 100 for a privacy factor of őĶ = 1). We compare existing methods with our proposed optimal estimator to pro-vide selection guidelines for practical deployment. Finally, the performance of these estimators is evaluated experimen-tally, showing that the empirical results are consistent with our theoretical derivations. 

## 1 Introduction 

Frequencies are among the most fundamental statistics of a dataset. They serve as the building blocks for deriving other statistical properties, ranging from mean and variance to higher-order moments, and are often used to infer the un-derlying probability distribution. While computing frequencies is straightforward when an analyst has unrestricted access to a dataset, typically involv-ing a simple iteration using a hash map, the task becomes significantly more complex when raw individual values are inaccessible. This is often the case when robust data privacy protections are implemented to mask sensitive information. This paper focuses on a privacy-preserving technique known as Local Differential Privacy (LDP) [12], the localized counterpart to Differential Privacy (DP) [8], which has be-come the de facto standard for data privacy. Before reporting an aggregate statistic from a dataset, DP injects sufficient noise into the result to ensure that an adversary cannot deter-mine whether a specific data point was included in the original set. However, DP traditionally requires centralized data pro-cessing, meaning it collects and stores raw data from clients before adding noise to the final aggregation. This architecture leaves data vulnerable to server breaches and eavesdropping during the transmission of raw values. LDP was proposed to mitigate these risks by perturbing data locally on the client side before it is sent to the server. Consequently, even if intercepted, an adversary cannot effec-tively infer the original value from the perturbed output. The degree of this perturbation is quantified by a privacy bud-get, őĶ, representing the maximum amount of knowledge an adversary can gain from the localized result [14]. Since frequency is such a fundamental statistical property, numerous LDP algorithms have been developed to estimate the frequencies within a dataset. These algorithms are dis-cussed in detail in Section 1.1. Among them, the Subset Selec-tion mechanism proposed by [17,20] achieved state-of-the-art precision regarding L1 and L2 losses. These metrics, formally defined in Section 2.2, measure the overall precision across all estimated frequencies. Notably, since the introduction of Subset Selection, no subsequent LDP frequency estimator has surpassed its leading precision in these metrics. Although the leading precision has remained unsurpassed for years, it remains unknown whether these algorithms are strictly optimal. The Subset Selection paper [20] established a lower bound for LDP frequency estimation and proved that their algorithm is order-optimal when eőĶ ‚Č™ d, where 

d denotes the dictionary size. However, when comparing the achieved leading precision with this derived lower bound, a significant gap remains in the constant term; specifically, the derived lower bound can be as low as 1512 of the achieved precision. It remains unclear whether an algorithm exists that can bridge this gap, or if existing mechanisms have already reached strict optimality without a formal proof. This paper directly addresses these questions by establish-ing a strict lower bound for LDP frequency estimation preci-sion in terms of L1 and L2 losses:

## 1.1 Previous Work 

Numerous LDP algorithms have been developed to estimate frequencies. Randomized Response [11, 19], the first LDP algorithm existing even before the formal proposal of LDP, was designed to collect sensitive data and estimate their fre-quencies. However, the variance of its estimated frequencies increases linearly with the dictionary size, making it unsuit-able for large dictionaries. To resolve this issue, RAPPOR [9] was proposed to estimate frequencies with precision inde-pendent of dictionary size, though its communication cost remains linear to the dictionary size. Subsequently, Hadamard Encoding [3] was developed to encode a client‚Äôs original value through the Hadamard matrix, reducing the communication cost to the logarithm of the dictionary size. Apple Inc. [7] leveraged Hadamard Encoding to build a private Count Mean Sketch method. Local Hashing and asymmetric RAPPOR were invented by [18] to achieve leading precision for esti-mating low frequencies. Meanwhile, Subset Selection was developed by [17, 20] with leading precision regarding L1

and L2 metrics. Later, [1] proposed Hadamard Response, which achieves precision identical to Subset Selection in high-privacy regimes ( őĶ ‚Üí 0). Subsequently, [6] improved Hadamard Response by proposing a recursive version; it of-fers similar (though slightly lower) precision compared to the leading benchmarks while maintaining a communication cost that is only logarithmic to the dictionary size. Projec-tive Geometry was then developed by [10], asymptotically achieving the same precision as Subset Selection [20] with a logarithmic communication cost, although it requires specific dictionary sizes. Recently, [15] improved the original Count Mean Sketch by [7] and proved that it approaches the leading precision in L1 and L2 when the dictionary size is sufficiently large. Several existing studies analyze the optimality of LDP frequency estimation. [11] demonstrated that any optimized LDP algorithm can be converted into an extremal configura-tion. Subsequently, [18] proposed the symmetric configura-tion adopted in this paper and showed that all existing algo-rithms can be encompassed by this framework. While [20] established lower bounds for L1 and L2 losses when eőĶ ‚Č™ d,these bounds are not strict and have constant terms that are significantly smaller than the strict bounds derived in this work.
