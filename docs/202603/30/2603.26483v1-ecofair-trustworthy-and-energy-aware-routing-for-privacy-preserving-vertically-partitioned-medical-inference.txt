# ABSTRACT 

Privacy-preserving medical inference must balance data locality, diagnostic reliability, and deployment efficiency. This paper presents EcoFair, a simulated vertically partitioned inference framework for dermatological diagnosis in which raw image and tabular data remain local and only modality-specific embeddings are transmitted for server-side multimodal fusion. EcoFair introduces a lightweight-first routing mechanism that selectively activates a heavier image encoder when local uncertainty or metadata-derived clinical risk indicates that additional computation is warranted. The routing decision combines predictive uncertainty, a safe–danger probability gap, and a tabular neurosymbolic risk score derived from patient age and lesion localisation. Experiments on three dermatology benchmarks show that EcoFair can substantially reduce edge-side inference energy in representative model pairings while remaining competitive in classification performance. The results further indicate that selective routing can improve subgroup-sensitive malignant-case behaviour in representative settings without modifying the global training objective. These findings position EcoFair as a practical framework for privacy-preserving and energy-aware medical inference under edge deployment constraints.

# 1 Introduction 

The transition towards decentralised digital infrastructures has reshaped how sensitive information is processed, shared, and utilised across institutional and geographic boundaries. In highly regulated sectors such as healthcare, governed by frameworks including HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) and GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), data can no longer be indiscriminately aggregated into centralised repositories [ 1]. In addition, the risks associated with single-point-of-failure breaches, unauthorised access, and loss of data sovereignty have heightened the importance of privacy-preserving decentralised computation. In this setting, predictive value must be extracted and exchanged without exposing raw digital assets to external parties. This requirement is especially important in image-based medical applications, where high-resolution images may contain identifiable biometric information while remaining essential for accurate diagnosis. In the clinical setting considered in this study, a dermatological client processes skin lesion images, a tabular details stores demographic metadata, and a central server performs multimodal fusion on intermediate representations. By constraining communication to learned embeddings rather than raw pixels or

It is also observed that vertically partitioned learning architectures have emerged as a practical approach for privacy-preserving collaborative inference across data silos that hold complementary information about the same patient population, particularly in healthcare settings where images, metadata, and clinical records may be distributed across different parties [ 2 ]. In such settings, vertically partitioned learning offers a natural way to balance predictive utility with data locality by allowing each party to retain raw inputs while contributing intermediate representations for downstream inference [ 3, 4]. Despite these privacy benefits, real-world deployment introduces additional system-level constraints where the repeated exchange and processing of representations between edge devices and cloud servers can impose substantial energy cost, which is problematic for mobile dermatoscopes and resource-constrained clinical settings [ 5 ]. Existing responses, including quantisation, pruning, and knowledge distillation, can reduce computational burden but may also weaken representational quality and downstream reliability [ 6]. Besides, in safety-critical medical applications, energy efficiency cannot be pursued in isolation if it disproportionately degrades performance on clinically important or demographically vulnerable cases. EcoFair is designed to address these challenges in a unified post-deployment setting. Rather than presenting an end-to-end vertically partitioned training pipeline, this work studies a simulated vertically partitioned inference architecture in which raw image and tabular data remain local, only modality-specific embeddings are transmitted to the server, and a routing mechanism determines whether inference can be completed using the lightweight image encoder alone or whether the heavier image encoder must also be activated before multimodal fusion. To examine this deployment behaviour beyond a single fixed backbone choice, EcoFair is evaluated using multiple pretrained lightweight and heavyweight image encoders spanning moderate, strong, and extreme compute disparities, including MobileNetV2, MobileNetV3Small, ResNet50, DenseNet201, and EfficientNetB6. The aim is to improve deployment efficiency while preserving clinical utility and subgroup-aware safety. Accordingly, this study is guided by the following research questions: 1. RQ1: How can decentralised medical inference reduce edge-side energy consumption without transmitting raw biometric data to a central server? 2. RQ2: How can dynamic routing incorporate explicit clinical risk to improve safety when local model confidence is unreliable? 3. RQ3: To what extent can post-hoc routing improve subgroup-sensitive diagnostic performance without modifying the global training objective? In response to these questions, this paper makes the following contributions: • To address RQ1, we propose a privacy-preserving vertically partitioned inference framework in which raw image and tabular data remain local and only modality-specific embeddings are exchanged. Within this setting, EcoFair introduces an energy-aware routing strategy for reducing unnecessary use of the heavier cloud-assisted image pathway. • To address RQ2, we combine predictive uncertainty with a tabular neurosymbolic risk score based on patient age and lesion localisation, providing an interpretable routing signal for clinically higher-risk cases. • To address RQ3, we investigate whether routing-based escalation can improve subgroup-sensitive diagnostic behaviour post hoc, particularly for clinically important malignant cases, without altering the global loss formulation. The rest of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 presents the EcoFair methodology, including the vertically partitioned inference setting, routing mechanism, and tabular neurosymbolic risk design. Section 4 reports the experimental evaluation across energy, classification, and fairness perspectives. Section 5 discusses the main findings of this work. Section 6 outlines limitations of the current study and directions for future research, and Section 7 concludes the paper. 

# 2 Related Work 

The development of secure, efficient, and fair decentralised artificial intelligence (AI) has motivated substantial research across several intersecting areas of computer science. To position EcoFair within this landscape, this section reviews relevant work on privacy-preserving data exchange, decentralised system optimisation, and fairness-aware medical AI. A central requirement in cross-institutional collaborative computing is the preservation of data sovereignty and privacy during information exchange. Recent works have examined secure and auditable infrastructures for controlled cross-2A PREPRINT - M ARCH 30, 2026 domain data sharing. For example, Zhang et al. [ 7 ] introduced the Secure Auditable Sharing Protocol together with a Reputation-driven Proactive Sharing Mechanism to support trustworthy cohort-data exchange across isolated data centres. Related discussions in Web 3.0 literature have likewise emphasised the importance of moving towards read– write–own paradigms for user-controlled data management [ 8]. Within machine learning, vertically partitioned federated settings provide a natural mechanism for privacy-preserving collaboration when different parties hold complementary attributes of the same individuals, enabling joint predictive modelling without direct raw-data disclosure [ 2, 4 , 3 ]. In this context, EcoFair is aligned with privacy-preserving vertically partitioned inference frameworks that are increasingly advocated for clinical use [ 9], while focusing specifically on post-deployment optimisation rather than end-to-end federated training. The deployment of deep models in decentralised and edge environments introduces additional constraints related to energy, latency, and computational capacity [ 10 ]. A recent work has sought to mitigate these bottlenecks in Split Learning architectures by dynamically regulating local computation to handle resource heterogeneity [ 11 ]. EcoFair complements this system-level focus by addressing post-deployment energy efficiency alongside demographic fairness. Persistent reliance on remote processing can be prohibitive for battery-constrained clinical devices and resource-limited healthcare settings. To address this, prior works have explored model compression, lightweight architectures, and adaptive computation strategies to make dermatological classification viable on resource-constrained edge devices [ 12 , 13 , 14 ]. Likewise, dynamic routing has emerged as a useful mechanism for selectively allocating computational effort based on input difficulty or model confidence, thereby mitigating the computational waste of network "overthinking" on simpler samples [ 15 ]. In a related direction, Qasim et al. [16 ] examined the sustainability of federated networks and highlighted the trade-off between model complexity and energy cost. However, many routing strategies rely primarily on confidence thresholds that may be poorly calibrated in deep neural networks [ 17 ]. EcoFair builds on this line of work by combining uncertainty-based routing with an explicit metadata-driven clinical risk signal, thereby linking deployment efficiency to clinically meaningful decision support. In medical AI, trustworthiness requires more than average predictive performance. Deployed systems must remain clinically reliable across demographic groups, avoid amplifying historical biases, and provide reasoning signals that can support scrutiny in safety-critical settings [ 18 , 19 , 20 ]. In dermatology, one of the fields within the medical system, unfair behaviour may appear as reduced malignant-case sensitivity for minority subgroups or atypical lesion presentations, highlighting the need to evaluate fairness robustly across diverse skin tones [ 21 , 22 , 23 ]. At the same time, neurosymbolic methods have gained attention as a way to incorporate structured domain knowledge into learning systems and improve interpretability [ 24 ]. For example, the ZTID-IoV framework applied neurosymbolic design principles to interpretable zero-trust intrusion detection in decentralised vehicular networks [ 25 ]. EcoFair adopts a related principle in the medical setting by integrating neural image-based prediction with a symbolic risk score derived from explicit patient metadata, and by using this signal to guide post-deployment routing decisions under privacy and energy constraints. Taken together, these limitations define the problem addressed in this paper: how to support privacy-preserving medical inference under edge deployment constraints while avoiding disproportionate degradation on clinically important and demographically vulnerable cases. In EcoFair, fairness is viewed through the malignant-case Equal Opportunity perspective, with particular emphasis on improving the worst-group True Positive Rate (WG-TPR) and reducing the subgroup TPR gap. Existing efficiency-oriented approaches in decentralised and edge AI largely prioritise energy reduction, compression, or routing economy, but they typically provide limited analysis of whether these savings are achieved at the expense of subgroup-sensitive diagnostic reliability [ 26 ]. EcoFair is motivated by this gap and studies whether routing can jointly improve deployment efficiency and subgroup-aware safety in a vertically partitioned inference setting. 

# 3 Methodology 

EcoFair operationalises the central premise of this study: privacy-preserving medical inference should not be treated solely as a communication problem, but as a joint systems problem involving representation exchange, adaptive computation, clinically informed decision support, and subgroup-aware evaluation under edge deployment constraints. 

3.1 Formulation of the VFL Environment 

EcoFair is formulated as a simulated vertically partitioned medical inference architecture designed to preserve data locality while enabling multimodal diagnosis. The system follows an Edge-to-Edge-to-Cloud topology with two local clients and one central server. Party A1 acts as the image client and processes raw dermatological images captured at the edge. Party A2 acts as the tabular client and processes patient metadata available locally, such as age and lesion localisation. Party B denotes the central server, which receives modality-specific embeddings and performs multimodal 3A PREPRINT - M ARCH 30, 2026 fusion to produce the final diagnostic prediction. The objective is to support collaborative inference without transmitting raw image pixels or raw tabular attributes beyond their originating clients. Fig. 1 illustrates the full EcoFair workflow. For empirical evaluation, EcoFair is studied in a simulated deployment setting using a centralised 5-fold cross-validation pipeline. This setup is intended to model routing behaviour, privacy-preserving representation exchange, and edge-side energy consumption, rather than to implement end-to-end federated training or measure real network latency. On the image side, Party A1 first computes a lightweight image embedding and the associated routing signals derived from the local predictive distribution. Based on these signals, the heavier image encoder is either kept inactive or additionally activated on the same client. On the tabular side, Party A2 independently computes a tabular embedding together with a metadata-driven risk score. The server then receives the resulting modality-specific embeddings and performs multimodal fusion for the final prediction. Privacy preservation in EcoFair follows directly from this architectural constraint. Raw image and tabular inputs remain local to their respective clients, while only learned intermediate representations are transmitted for downstream fusion. This design reduces direct exposure of sensitive biometric and demographic information without relying on explicit cryptographic protocols or injected differential noise at inference time [27]. In a vertically partitioned deployment setting, the image client must first compute a lightweight representation for every input, but not every sample warrants the additional cost of a heavier encoder. EcoFair therefore, introduces a dynamic routing mechanism that operates directly on the local predictive output of the lightweight image pathway and decides whether the heavier image encoder should remain inactive or be additionally activated for the same sample. This design targets deployment efficiency at the point of inference rather than during training, and is consistent with adaptive computation strategies proposed for resource-constrained intelligent systems [12, 28]. 3.3 Tabular Neurosymbolic Risk Score 

Predictive uncertainty alone is not always sufficient for safe routing in clinical settings, since neural models may still produce confident but incorrect predictions. To reduce this risk, EcoFair augments the routing mechanism with a tabular neurosymbolic risk score derived from explicit patient metadata [ 31 , 20 ]. This component provides a simple and auditable symbolic signal that operates alongside the image-based uncertainty measures. The tabular client, Party A2, processes metadata variables that are clinically associated with malignancy risk, specifically patient age and anatomical site. Lesion localisation is included not only as an anatomical descriptor, but also because body-site risk patterns in cutaneous melanoma are known to vary with differential ultraviolet exposure and related epidemiological factors, although this relationship is not uniform across all melanoma subtypes [ 32 , 33 ]. 3.4 Fairness-Aware Routing Mechanism 

The final methodological component of EcoFair addresses subgroup-sensitive reliability in deployment. In standard training settings, optimisation is typically driven by aggregate objective functions, which can mask systematic disparities across demographic groups and clinically atypical cases [ 35 ]. In medical diagnosis, this may appear as reduced sensitivity for underrepresented cohorts even when overall performance remains acceptable. However, EcoFair departs from conventional fairness -inducing approaches as it does not impose fairness through an additional loss term or constrained optimisation objective during training. Instead, it examines whether fairness can be improved post hoc through routing behaviour at inference time. The central idea is that samples associated with higher uncertainty, narrower safe–danger separation, or elevated metadata-derived risk are more likely to correspond to cases for which lightweight-only inference is less reliable. By selectively activating the heavier image pathway for such cases, EcoFair provides an operational mechanism for reducing failure on clinically important and subgroup-sensitive samples. 4.1 Evaluation Setup and Protocol 

The evaluation uses three publicly available dermatology benchmarks: HAM10000 [ 37 ], PAD-UFES-20 [ 38 ], and BCN20000 [ 39 ]. These datasets were selected because they collectively capture clinically relevant heterogeneity in image acquisition conditions, demographic composition, and label imbalance. In particular, PAD-UFES-20 and BCN20000 introduce stronger out-of-distribution variation than HAM10000, while all three datasets reflect the imbalance patterns commonly observed in real-world dermatological classification tasks [ 40 ]. This makes them suitable for evaluating not only predictive performance, but also the deployment behaviour of routing-based systems under varying difficulty and subgroup composition. To support a principled analysis of deployment trade-offs, EcoFair does not rely on a single fixed image backbone. Instead, a broader screening stage is first used to profile multiple candidate encoders, after which three representative lite-heavy model pairs are selected for detailed framework-level evaluation. This design allows the experiments to examine EcoFair under moderate, strong, and extreme compute disparities between lightweight and heavyweight image pathways. The tabular neurosymbolic risk score is calibrated from the training-fold metadata. 4.2 Backbone Screening for Edge–Cloud Deployment 

To ensure that the architectural choices used in the EcoFair routing framework are principled rather than ad hoc, a comprehensive screening of ten widely used convolutional backbones was first conducted. For each architecture, deployment-oriented characteristics including parameter count, model size, embedding dimensionality, inference latency, GPU memory footprint, and energy consumption per sample were empirically measured during feature extraction. These measurements characterise the computational behaviour of each encoder independently of the routing mechanism and provide an objective basis for selecting representative model combinations for subsequent framework evaluation. To accurately quantify the computational cost of the feature extraction and inference phases, GPU energy consumption was dynamically tracked using pynvml, the official Python interface for the NVIDIA Management Library (NVML) [ 41 ]. This approach directly polls hardware-level power sensors, providing a highly accurate and widely validated standard for energy measurement in deep learning pipelines [42]. 4.3 Optimisation of Decentralised Energy Consumption 

A primary deployment constraint in edge-based medical inference is the energy cost associated with image processing under limited on-device resources. In EcoFair, this cost is determined by whether inference proceeds with the lightweight image encoder alone or whether the heavier image encoder is additionally activated for the same sample before multimodal fusion. The routing mechanism, therefore, acts directly on the dominant source of variable computation in the image client and provides a controllable trade-off between efficiency and diagnostic safety. Table 2 summarises the empirical per-sample energy consumption and routing statistics for the three selected model pairs across the evaluated datasets. On HAM10000, all three pairs achieve a reduction in energy consumption relative to their corresponding heavy baselines, with savings ranging from 28.09% for MobileNetV2–ResNet50 to 68.09% for MobileNetV3Small–EfficientNetB6. 4.5 Subgroup-Sensitive Fairness 

Subgroup-sensitive fairness is a central requirement in medical deployment because acceptable average performance can still conceal systematic failure on vulnerable cohorts. In this study, fairness is evaluated primarily through the Equal Opportunity perspective, which examines parity in True Positive Rate (TPR) across demographic subgroups for clinically important malignant cases [ 43 ]. Under this criterion, a system is considered more equitable when it improves worst-group sensitivity and reduces disparity across subgroups, rather than merely increasing average performance on the dominant population. EcoFair does not enforce fairness through a modified training objective or an explicit fairness penalty. Instead, it investigates whether fairness can be improved post hoc through routing behaviour at inference time. Samples associated with higher uncertainty, narrower safe–danger separation, or elevated metadata-derived risk are more likely to be difficult for lightweight-only inference and are therefore more likely to trigger selective heavy-model activation. As shown in Fig. 4 and summarised in Table 4, the fairness benefit of EcoFair should therefore be interpreted as a deployment-time rescue mechanism meaning it aims to improve subgroup-sensitive malignant-case performance relative to the baselines by selectively escalating samples that are more likely to be clinically ambiguous. # 5 Discussion 

The empirical results position EcoFair as a deployment-oriented framework rather than a universally dominant classifier. Across the evaluated datasets, its benefit depends on the interaction between routing policy, dataset difficulty, and the computational disparity between lightweight and heavyweight image pathways. The strongest results are observed when routing operates over a sufficiently meaningful compute gap, allowing selective heavy-model activation to improve classification balance and subgroup-sensitive behaviour while still reducing inference cost in representative settings. A second important finding is that fairness improvement should be interpreted as a deployment-time property of the rout-ing policy rather than as a guarantee induced by training. EcoFair does not enforce subgroup parity through constrained optimisation or loss modification. Instead, it improves worst-group malignant-case behaviour in representative settings by identifying uncertain or clinically higher-risk cases and escalating them selectively. This makes the framework particularly relevant for practical medical AI deployment, where average predictive performance alone is not sufficient. At the same time, the results show that the framework is not uniformly optimal across all model pairings and datasets. More challenging data distributions increase routing frequency, which can reduce the net efficiency advantage for some backbone combinations. This is an important outcome in itself, because it shows that backbone disparity and routing design must be considered jointly when assessing the usefulness of selective inference in privacy-preserving medical systems. 

# 6 Future Work 

Several directions remain for future investigation. First, the current evaluation is conducted in a simulated vertically partitioned inference setting rather than on a physical distributed deployment. A natural next step is to implement EcoFair on real edge hardware and embedded platforms in order to measure end-to-end latency, communication overhead, and energy usage under practical operating conditions. Second, the current framework relies on the availability and quality of local tabular metadata. Future work should therefore examine the robustness of the neurosymbolic risk component under missing, corrupted, or biased metadata and investigate more reliable strategies for metadata validation and uncertainty-aware tabular reasoning. Finally, the present results show that the effectiveness of EcoFair is pair-dependent. Future work should extend the framework to broader backbone families, stronger calibration strategies, and adaptive routing policies that are more explicitly optimised for joint energy, performance, and subgroup-sensitive fairness across heterogeneous deployment environments. 

# 7 Conclusion 

The development of practical digital healthcare infrastructures depends on balancing privacy preservation, diagnostic reliability, and deployment efficiency. In this work, EcoFair has been studied as a simulated vertically partitioned inference framework designed to address these objectives jointly. The results show that privacy-preserving multimodal inference can be combined with selective heavy-model activation to support clinically meaningful deployment trade-offs without transmitting raw image or tabular data beyond their originating clients. With respect to RQ1 and RQ2, EcoFair demonstrates that lightweight-first inference, uncertainty-aware routing, and metadata-driven neurosymbolic risk scoring can reduce unnecessary heavy-model activation while preserving a mechanism for clinically informed escalation. The empirical results further show that the efficiency benefit of this design is pair-dependent: meaningful energy savings are achieved when the computational disparity between lightweight and heavyweight pathways is sufficiently large, while more modest pairings offer weaker efficiency gains. With respect to RQ3, the results indicate that routing can also improve subgroup-sensitive behaviour at deployment time without modifying the global training objective. Although this fairness benefit is not uniform across all model pairs and 13 A PREPRINT - M ARCH 30, 2026 datasets, EcoFair shows that selective escalation can act as a practical safety mechanism for improving worst-group malignant-case sensitivity and reducing disparity in representative settings. Overall, EcoFair should be interpreted not as a universally optimal classifier, but as a deployment framework for privacy-preserving medical inference in which routing, metadata-aware risk signals, and multimodal fusion are used to balance energy, performance, and fairness under edge constraints.
