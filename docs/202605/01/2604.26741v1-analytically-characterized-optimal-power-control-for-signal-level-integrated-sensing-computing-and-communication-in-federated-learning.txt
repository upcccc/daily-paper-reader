# Analytically Characterized Optimal Power Control for Signal-Level-Integrated Sensing, Computing and Communication in Federated Learning 

Paul Zheng, Yao Zhu, Xiaopeng Yuan, Yulin Hu, and Anke Schmeink 

Abstract —In the Internet-of-Things (IoT) era, efficient func-tionality integration is essential to address the growing demands of communication, computation, and sensing. Signal-level inte-grated sensing, computing, and communication (Sig-ISCC) is envisioned, where a single waveform simultaneously supports sensing, computing and communication via over-the-air compu-tation (AirComp). Meanwhile, federated learning (FL) is widely regarded as a promising distributed machine learning framework that enables network intelligence in a privacy-preserving and secure manner, and exhibits strong synergy with AirComp, which alleviates the communication bottleneck of FL. In this paper, we study uplink Sig-ISCC design for AirComp-FL with joint target detection. We formulate the joint power and receive-scaling control problem, where edge devices’ transmitted signals should serve both sensing and AirComp purposes. The goal is to minimize the AirComp aggregation distortion subject to a joint target-detection requirement. Although the resulting problem is non-convex in the original variables, we show that it admits an equivalent convex reformulation after a suitable variable transformation. By exploiting analytical optimality properties, we develop a robust, optimal, and polynomial-time-complexity algo-rithm that efficiently achieves the optimal transmit powers and receive scaling factor. Simulation results validate the optimality and numerical robustness of the proposed algorithm and show its superior FL performance compared to baseline methods. 

Index Terms —Federated Learning, Integrated Sensing, Com-puting and Communication (ISCC), Target Detection, Over-the-Air Computation (AirComp), 6G. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Distributed edge learning (DEL) paradigms such as feder-ated learning (FL) [2] are becoming increasingly important owing to their ability to preserve data privacy and reduce communication costs [3]. FL trains a global model by re-peatedly exchanging locally trained model updates (instead of raw data) between edge devices (EDs) and a parameter server. It proceeds iteratively in the form of communication rounds (CRs): in each CR, a subset of selected EDs perform local training and upload their updates to the server for aggregation, i.e., simple (weighted) averaging in the original FedAvg [2]. This framework has been considered for, and in some cases already deployed in various real-world mobile edge

applications [4], e.g., Google keyboard prediction [5]. Since most EDs are wirelessly connected and frequent exchange of model updates is required, wireless communication constitutes a crucial bottleneck for such DEL systems [6]. Since the server only requires the average of the local updates, and neural networks are robust to small perturbations of model parameters, analog transmission leveraging over-the-air computation (AirComp) has emerged as an effective technique to address the communication bottleneck of FL, yielding what is known as over-the-air federated edge learning (AirFEEL) [7], [8], [9]. AirComp exploits the superposition property of multi-access channels: with appropriate pre- and post-processing, analog signals are transmitted and certain nomographic functions of the transmitted signals (here, the average) are obtained directly over the air [10]. AirFEEL enables model aggregation across massive numbers of EDs in each CR without increasing the number of communication resource blocks as the number of participating EDs grows. Optimal power control for such schemes has been studied in [11], [12], and for multi-antenna base stations, joint client selection and receive beamforming were designed in [7]. The convergence of such schemes under heterogeneous data statistical distributions has been established in [13], [14]. One of the most promising 6G technologies is integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) [15], [16], which inte-grates radar sensing and communication functionalities into a unified system. Since AirComp integrates communication and computation, combining ISAC with AirComp leads to signals that simultaneously serve sensing, computing, and communication, referred to as signal-level integrated sensing, computing and communication (ISCC) (Sig-ISCC). Such a framework was first proposed in [17] using distinct superposed coded signals for each functionality. A similar framework for vehicular networks was addressed via deep reinforcement learning in [18]. The fully integrated case (i.e., using the same coded signal for all three functionalities) was studied in [19] and subsequently extended to Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing (OFDM) systems in [20]. This framework has also been applied to robotic scenarios, where the sensing performance is characterized by the beampattern difference from the optimal sensing beampattern [21]. We note that, in addition to the presented Sig-ISCC frame-work, two other types of ISCC widely exist in the literature. One is the coexistence design of ISAC and computing ser-vices, where computation may be performed in a traditional manner via a mobile edge computing (MEC) server or via 

> arXiv:2604.26741v1 [cs.IT] 29 Apr 2026 2

AirComp signal superposition. Zhao et al. [22] proposed a joint communication and sensing beamforming design for ISAC-aided MEC systems, in which the base station (BS) allocates resources across all functionalities and can partially offload computation tasks to nearby edge servers. Resource allocation for a similar system supporting federated learning was studied in [23] based on multi-task learning. In the context of AirFEEL, the coexistence design has focused on the coexistence of the BS performing ISAC while EDs conducting uplink aggregation via AirComp under interference from ISAC signals. Asaad et al. [24] considered a framework where the BS performs downlink ISAC, and the uplink AirComp aggregation is affected by interference from target echoes of the BS’s ISAC signals. They proposed joint client scheduling and BS beamforming to maximize the number of scheduled EDs while guaranteeing sensing and AirComp performance. A similar framework was studied in [25], where the BS continues downlink ISAC while receiving the uplink AirComp model aggregation. Another type can be termed functional-level ISCC. It treats sensing as a data acquisition stage for an edge computing or learning task, and communication as the means to transmit sensed data, extracted features, or learned updates to an edge server for computing or fusion. In this stream, the key coupling lies in how sensing quality, communi-cation distortion and latency, and computing resources jointly affect task-level learning or inference metrics. Recent works study AirFEEL while explicitly accounting for sensing noise and aggregation distortions [26], and AirComp has also been adopted for over-the-air fusion of sensed features for edge AI inference [27]; extensions to online learning, unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV)-enabled collection, and generalization-error analyses can be found in [28], [29], [30], [31], [32], [33]. In parallel, functional-level ISCC under conventional digital links has been studied for task-oriented edge inference [34] and federated edge learning [35], with numerous extensions covering privacy, vertical FL, vehicular perception, and aerial deployments (see, e.g., [36], [37], [38], [39], [40], [41], [42]). This paper focuses on Sig-ISCC, the tightest level of inte-gration of the three functionalities, since sensing, computing, and communication are all realized through the same wave-form. Such a high degree of integration offers the substan-tial potential for resource efficiency. Applying Sig-ISCC to AirFEEL implies that the uplink AirComp signal is also used for the sensing task. The sensing requirement couples non-trivially with the AirComp aggregation quality, necessitating joint resource design. Only a few works in the literature consider such Sig-ISCC for FL. Du et al. [43] employed superposed coded sensing and communication signals (as in [17]) and exploited the communication component for sensing instead of treating it purely as interference. Pang and Zhang [44] proposed a holistic framework combining signal-level and functional-level ISCC, where deep learning modules infer ED locations/CSI from received superposed communication signals and then use the inferred CSI for sub-sequent communication and learning designs. In our previous work [45], we considered an individual sensing requirement for each uplink transmitter, which translates into per-ED minimum power constraints; this implies additional sensing transmissions from EDs that are not selected for AirComp-FL and thus introduces extra interference to AirComp aggregation (see also [46, Section 3.3]). However, in many practical deployments, sensing targets and EDs are widely distributed and may exhibit heterogeneous sensing geometries. Enforcing an individual sensing requirement for each ED is unrealistic and unnecessary, as EDs can jointly perform sensing tasks. We therefore study such Sig-ISCC for FL with a joint target detection task [47] and impose a joint sensing requirement at the BS by fusing sensed information across EDs. This work investigates therefore the tightest form of ISCC, Sig-ISCC, considering joint detection as the sensing task. In the considered system, EDs participate in AirComp aggrega-tion while performing joint detection of a target. Conventional AirComp power-control designs typically rely on ranking EDs according to their channel gains, with the optimal power taking either the maximum power or the channel-inverting level [11], [48]. The additional joint sensing requirement breaks this monotonicity structure due to the coupling of communication and sensing performance, and makes the power allocation substantially more challenging. The main contributions are summarized as follows:  

> •

We propose, to the best of our knowledge, the first uplink shared-waveform power control framework for signal-level integrated sensing, computing, and communication (Sig-ISCC) in AirFEEL. Unlike existing works on joint sensing and AirComp-based FEEL, which either operate at the functional level by treating sensing as a data collec-tion procedure for FEEL, or consider the coexistence level 

of integration through the coexistence of downlink ISAC and uplink AirComp, our framework exploits a single shared waveform to simultaneously support communica-tion and computation via AirComp-FL, and joint target detection.  

> •

We formulate the resulting design as a joint transmit-power and receive-scaling optimization problem, with the objective of minimizing the convergence gap of AirComp-FL while satisfying a certain joint target de-tection task requirement.  

> •

Based on our analytical findings, we reformulate the original nonconvex problem as an equivalent convex opti-mization problem via a proposed variable transformation. Building on this, we establish key structural optimality properties showing that the optimal solution can be char-acterized through the root of a monotone differentiable function. Based on this characterization, we develop a polynomial-time algorithm with provable global optimal-ity and robustness to root-finding inexactness.  

> •

Simulation results validate the optimality, confirm the robustness of the proposed algorithm, and demonstrate the importance of optimal power control for AirComp-FL performance under sensing constraints. The remaining sections are organized as follows. Section II describes the system model. Section III derives closed-form optimality properties and develops the globally optimal algo-rithm. Section IV presents simulation results, and Section V concludes the paper. 3

D. Algorithm Development 

Building upon the theoretical characterization in Theorem 7, we now develop Algorithm 1 to solve (P2) . The key insight from Theorem 7 is that the optimal active set S(λ∗,α ∗) can be determined by systematically searching through all K2(K −

1) /2 possible set configurations characterized by the index pair (n, i ). The algorithm exploits the structure established in Theorem 5 and Theorem 6: the ordering of {ιk(α)}k∈K 

changes at most K(K − 1) /2 times as α varies; for each interval [αn, α n+1 ), the permutation πn remains fixed, and the active set Sn,i is uniquely determined by the rank index i.The algorithm proceeds by iterating over all α-intervals indexed by n and λ-interval indexed by i. Within each interval, the conditions from Theorem 7 are checked: λ∗

must satisfy LHS n,i (λ∗) = RHS n,i (λ∗) for a λ∗ ∈

[ιπn(i)(α), ι πn(i+1) (α)) . Since LHS n,i (λ) is increasing and 

RHS n,i (λ) is decreasing in λ within the feasible region, a root-finding [54] method efficiently finds the unique crossing point 

λ∗. For each candidate solution, the algorithm verifies that the computed α(iii ) 

> n,i

(λ∗) lies in the correct α-interval and that λ∗

satisfies the required λ-range constraints. The resulting valid candidate yields the optimal solution. The overall complexity of Algorithm 1 is O(|K| 3). Sorting the O(|K| 2) transition values incurs O(|K| 2 log( |K| )) complexity. The algorithm then iterates over O(|K| 2) α-intervals, checking O(|K| ) candidate solutions per interval. Since the logarithmic term is dominated, the overall complexity takes the stated form. Since the algo-rithm has such analytical characterization, all the operations may be transformed into matrix/tensor operations, resulting in further acceleration.

We formulated the joint transmit-power and receive-scaling design and proposed a variable trans-formation that transforms the original non-convex problem into an equivalent convex reformulation. Based on extensive optimality analysis, we developed an analytically characterized polynomial-time optimization algorithm based on root-finding of monotonic differentiable functions. Robustness to root-finding errors has also been proven. Simulation results validate both the optimality and numerical robustness of the proposed design relative to a popular off-the-shelf solver.
