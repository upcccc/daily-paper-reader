> arXiv:2604.27456v1 [cs.CR] 30 Apr 2026 Abstract
> Access to genomic data is highly regulated due to its sensitive nature. While safeguards are essential, cumbersome data access processes pose a significant barrier to the development of AI methods for genomics. Synthetic data generation can mitigate this tension by enabling broader data sharing without exposing sensitive information. Synthetic genomic data are produced by training generative models on real data and subsequently sampling artificial data that preserves relevant statistics while limiting disclosures about the underlying individuals. In some settings, a single data holder may have sufficient data to train such generative models; however, in many applications data must be combined across multiple sites to achieve adequate scale. This need arises, e.g., in rare disease studies, where individual hospitals typically hold data for only a small number of patients. The solution we present in this paper enables multiple data holders to jointly train a synthetic data generator without revealing their raw data. Our approach combines secure multiparty computation (MPC) to ensure input privacy, so that no party ever discloses its data in unencrypted form, with differential privacy (DP) to provide output privacy by mitigating information leakage from the released synthetic data. We empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method by generating high-utility synthetic datasets from multiple real RNA-seq cohorts in federated settings, showing that our approach enables privacy-preserving data synthesis even when data are distributed across institutions.

# 1 Introduction 

Patient data, and genomic data in particular, is subject to strict access controls. Even when datasets are advertised as “open”, researchers are often required to navigate long and cumbersome access procedures, such as negotiating complex contractual agreements with data controllers, completing extensive training requirements, or performing analyses within controlled data enclaves [28]. In contrast, AI researchers are used to minimal barriers when accessing, exploring, and experimenting with new datasets. This mismatch significantly constrains AI work in biomedicine. At the same time, datasets containing protected health information cannot be made publicly available without careful safeguards. Concerns regarding the privacy and confidentiality of clinical and genomic data are well justified and have led to increasingly strict controlled-access requirements [19, 20, 21]. A promising method for operationalizing sensitive patient data for research while protecting patient privacy is synthetic data generation (SDG). A common approach to SDG is to train a generative model on real data and subsequently use the trained model to create artificial data that preserves important characteristics of the real data without disclosing sensitive information about individuals [10]. While in some scenarios a single data holder (e.g., a hospital) may have sufficient data on its own to enable SDG, in many cases data from multiple sites needs to be combined. Such federated settings are common, for instance, with rare diseases, where each clinical site has data for a small patient cohort. 

In this paper we present a method for training privacy-preserving synthetic RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data generators in federated settings. Our approach adopts cryptographic techniques based on secure multiparty computation (MPC) [5] to support a federated training process in which each data holder encrypts their data on site before sending it as input. MPC servers perform training operations on the input data while it stays encrypted, thus providing input privacy . Furthermore, we use differential privacy (DP) [7] to provide formal, individual-level privacy guarantees for the released synthetic data, thus providing output privacy .The heart of our solution is an MPC-protocol for Private-PGM. Private-PGM is a marginal-based SDG algorithm that learns a probabilisitic graphical model (PGM) from marginal probability distributions estimated on (a discretized version of) the real data, and subsequently samples from the model to generate synthetic data [16]. Private-PGM offers formal DP guarantees by perturbing the estimated marginals with noise. It has been shown to work well for generation of synthetic bulk RNA-seq data in centralized settings, i.e., where all the real data resides with one data holder [4, 18]. Our extension of Private-PGM to federated settings consists of cryptographic protocols for quantile binning and estimating marginal probability distributions over the combined data of the data custiodians while it remains encrypted. To this end, we draw inspiration from MPC protocols in the 

CaPS framework previously proposed by Pentyala et al. [23] for the marginal-based SDG algorithms 2MWEM-PGM and AIM [16, 17]. We found, however, that a straightforward implementation of these MPC protocols for Private-PGM does not scale well with the high dimensionality of RNA-seq data. Different from Fu et al., who recently proposed to address the inefficiencies of CaPS by optimizing secure sort-and-count operations [9], we replace iterative “per sample per gene” computations by one dot product over a vector of all samples per gene. Using a variety of TCGA and leukemia datasets, we demonstrate that our approach for federated synthetic RNA-seq data generation can produce synthetic data in as little as a few minutes, depending on the MPC scheme, with a level of quality that is at par with the centralized setting. 

# 2 Preliminaries 

2.1 Secure Multiparty Computation for Input Privacy 

Secure multiparty computation (MPC) is an umbrella term for cryptographic approaches that allow two or more parties to jointly compute a specified output from distributed private information without revealing it to one another [5]. In this paper, we follow the “MPC as a service” paradigm, where data holders delegate the computations to a set of non-colluding, but otherwise non-trusted servers. We adopt secret-sharing based MPC where each data holder encrypts their private data 

x by splitting it into shares (see below for an example) and distributing the shares among a set of MPC servers S = {S1, S 2, . . . , S K }. Though x can be reconstructed when all shares are combined, none of the MPC servers by themselves learn anything about the value of x. These servers run MPC protocols to jointly perform computations over the secret shares, in our case computations for generating synthetic data. As all computations are done over the secret shares, the servers do not learn the values of the inputs nor of intermediate results, i.e., MPC provides input privacy .The security guarantees of an MPC protocol are defined by the type of adversary it can withstand. These are typically categorized into passive and active threat models, reflecting the power an ad-versary has over the MPC servers it corrupts. In the passive (or “semi-honest”) setting, all parties follow the instructions of the protocol, but the adversary attempts to learn private information from the internal state of the corrupted servers and the messages that they receive. MPC protocols that are secure against passive adversaries prevent such leakage of information. In contrast, an active (or “malicious”) adversary may corrupt an MPC server to arbitrarily deviate from the protocol, includ-ing colluding with others to breach privacy or corrupt intermediate computations. One way to detect and prevent this is by using a “brain-and-pinky” architecture [8]. For each virtual communication, one real party referred to as “brain” is elected to transmit all the messages to all real parties in the receiving virtual party, referred to as the “pinky”. Right before the final outputs are revealed, all the parties perform a single consistency check. If a malicious “brain” deviated from the protocol and sent a corrupted message, it will conflict with at least one of the “pinky’s” locally computed views. Such inconsistency would trigger an immediate abort before any private data is leaked or an incorrect result is accepted. The protocols that we propose in Section 4 are sufficiently generic to be used in settings with passive or active adversaries. This is achieved by changing the underlying MPC scheme to align with the desired security setting. To give the reader a concrete understanding, we give a brief description of a specific MPC scheme based on replicated secret sharing with K = 3 computing parties [1]. MPC works for inputs defined over a finite field or ring. Since inputs for SDG algorithms are finite precision real numbers, the data holders convert all of their inputs to fixed precision [3] and map these to integers modulo q, i.e., to the ring Zq = {0, 1, . . . , q − 1}, with q a power of 2. A private value x in Zq is secret shared among servers (parties) S1, S 2, and S3 by selecting uniformly random shares x1, x 2, x 3 ∈ Zq such that x1 + x2 + x3 = x mod q, and giving ( x1, x 2) to S1, ( x2, x 3) to S2,and ( x3, x 1) to S3. We denote a secret sharing of x by [[ x]]. From now on, servers will compute on the secret shares of x rather than on x itself. The 3 servers are capable of performing operations such as the addition of a constant, summing of secret-shared values, and multiplication by a publicly known constant by doing local computations on their respective shares. To multiply secret shared values [[ x]] and [[ y]], we can use the fact that 3x · y = ( x1 + x2 + x3)( y1 + y2 + y3) mod q. This means that S1 computes z1 = x1 · y1 + x1 · y2 + x2 · y1

mod q, S2 computes z2 = x2 · y2 + x2 · y3 + x3 · y2 mod q, and S3 computes z3 = x3 · y3 + x3 · y1 + x1 · y3

mod q. The next step is for the servers to obtain an additive secret sharing of 0 by choosing random values u1, u 2, u 3 such that u1 + u2 + u3 = 0 mod q. This can be done using pseudorandom functions. Each server Si then computes vi = zi + ui mod q. Finally, S1 sends v1 to S3, S2 sends v2 to S1,and S3 sends v3 to S2. This allows the servers S1, S 2, and S3 to obtain the replicated secret shares (v1, v 2), ( v2, v 3), and ( v3, v 1), respectively, of the value v = x · y.In Section 5, we evaluate our MPC protocol for federated SDG in the above 3PC MPC scheme, which can be proven secure against semi-honest adversaries [1]. For the malicious case, we use the MPC scheme proposed by [6] as implemented in MP-SPDZ [11]. 

2.2 Differential Privacy for Output Privacy 

Differential privacy (DP) [7] is a privacy notion that offers theoretical bounds on how much a single individual can influence the outcome of some computation or algorithm. This means that even if an adversary has access to the output of the computation, they cannot determine whether any specific individual’s data was included in the input dataset. DP bounds the difference in outputs resulting from two datasets D and D′ differing in one record, set by privacy budget ϵ. ( ϵ,δ)-DP is a common relaxation of the stricter ϵ-DP notion, permitting a small chance of mechanism failure quantified in δ, i.e., with probability at most δ, the mechanism may violate ϵ-differential privacy. Formally, a randomized algorithm M satisfies ( ϵ,δ)-DP if and only if for any pair of neighboring datasets D and 

D′, i.e., datasets that differ in only one record, and any y ∈ Range (M ): 

P r [M (D) = y] ≤ eϵP r [M (D′) = y] + δ (1) An ( ϵ, δ )-DP algorithm M is usually created out of an algorithm M ∗ by adding noise that is proportional to the sensitivity of M ∗, in which the sensitivity reflects the maximum impact changing a single record in D can have on the output of M ∗.

2.3 Private-PGM for Synthetic Data Generation 

In this paper, we adapt the marginals-based SDG algorithm Private-PGM [16] to federated settings. Private-PGM fits an undirected graphical model to differentially private noisy measurements of low-dimensional marginals, from which synthetic records are drawn by sampling the learned model. The method operates on a dataset D with discrete features x = {x1, x 2, . . . , x d}. Each feature xi

has a discrete domain Ω i. For a subset q of features, the marginal μq(D) counts, for each combination of values t ∈ Ωq, how many times that combination appears in D, where Ω q = Q 

> xi∈q

Ωi.Private-PGM begins by computing DP marginals μq(D) + N (0 , σ qI), where N is Gaussian noise with scale σq determined based on specified DP parameters ( ϵ, δ ). Then it estimates the joint marginal distribution that best explains all the noisy measurements. Specifically, following the implementation of Chen et al. [4] for RNA-seq data, the algorithm first computes 1-way marginals μg capturing the count distribution of each gene g and the label μy, as well as 2-way marginals μg,y capturing the joint distributions between each gene g and the label y. DP noise is then injected into these statistics based on the DP privacy budget. It then estimates the joint marginal distribution that best explains all the noisy measurements, while in parallel inferring the parameters of the graphical model using through belief propagation on a junction tree. Finally, it reconstructs a synthetic dataset from these noisy measurements using a graphical-model-based inference engine. We refer to McKenna et al. [16] for more details. 

# 3 Related Work 

Synthetic RNA-seq Data Generation. SDG algorithms have previously been developed and ap-plied for generation of synthetic RNA-seq data in the centralized setting, i.e., when all real data used to train the generator resides with one data holder. Existing research encompasses both generation 4of synthetic bulk RNA-seq data [22] and synthetic single cell RNA-seq data [14, 26]. Regarding bulk RNA-seq data, which is the focus of this paper, Chen et al. [4] produced a comprehensive compari-son of SDGs, demonstrating that the marginal-based Private-PGM algorithm [16] can generate bulk RNA-seq counts with high downstream utility while offering formal differential privacy guarantees. We confirmed the effectiveness of Private-PGM through our participation in the Critical Assessment of Massive Data Analysis (CAMDA) Health Privacy Challenge [18]. 1 At the conclusion of this in-ternational benchmarking competition in 2025, Private-PGM achieved the highest utility synthetic RNA-seq data (generated based on real TCGA data) of all submissions. While there may still be room for future improvement [22], these results suggest that Private-PGM is a strong starting point for developing MPC protocols to enable SDG in federated settings where data is distributed across multiple holders. 

Federated Synthetic Data Generation. Research on secure SDG from distributed data sources, or federated SDG, is nascent. Most existing work focuses on image data and some on tabular data [12]. Initial federated synthesis approaches adhere closely to the original federated learning (FL) paradigm in which each client performs local neural network training and exchanges only parameter values instead of raw data with a central aggregator [29]. These approaches pay for privacy with a loss in utility compared to the centralized setting, and they are designed and evaluated for clients who each have thousands of instances available for local model training. This is in stark contrast with the scenarios that could actually benefit the most from federated SDG, provided the proper technical solutions are in place, such as the rare cancer setting in which a data holder may have data for only a handful of individuals. Very little research has been done on training synthetic data generators across encrypted data silos. Pentyala et al. proposed the CaPS framework (Collaborative and Private Synthetic Data Generation) with the first MPC protocols for marginal-based tabular data generation [23]. Their method has since been shown to yield synthetic data with a substantially higher fidelity than a more traditional FL approach [15]. Experiments in [15, 23] are however performed in a so-called horizontal patitioning set-up in which it is assumed that each data holder (hospital) computes marginals for their own data on-site and subsequently sends eencrypted shares of those marginals to MPC servers to start further processing within MPC. For generation of synthetic RNA-seq data with Private-PGM, however, we need the data holders to send encrypted shares of the gene expression levels (not the marginals) to the MPC servers so that the servers can execute an MPC protocol for quantile binning across all data. Computation of marginals is subsequently performed on secret sharings of the binned data, without decrypting it. RNA-seq data is highly dimensional, resulting in a large number of marginals that needs to be computed in this way. An initial attempt to use CaPS for generation of synthetic RNA-seq data [24] does not scale because of an inefficient implementation of the computation of the marginals over gene expression levels. Fu et al. [9] recently identified the inefficiencies of CaPS 

and proposed to address them by optimizing secure sort-and-count operations [9]. In this paper, we present an alternative solution that lends itself well to RNA-seq data, namely replacing iterative “per sample per gene” computations by one dot product over a vector of all samples per gene. This leads to an efficient and effective solution, which to the best of our knowledge, is the first one in the literature to enable synthetic RNA-seq data generation with input privacy and output privacy.

Such sequential processing resulted in a high number of repeated operations and network communication rounds, leading to slower execution speeds and large memory overhead. To resolve this, we apply a dot-product optimization: instead of sequentially iterating over individual samples, we compute the 1-way and 2-way marginal counts securely with vectorized dot products between the feature and the label indicator vectors (Line 11 for 1-way marginals and Line 13 for 2-way marginals). 

Secure perturbation with noise for differential privacy with πBATCH-GAUSS . We extend this vectorization to the DP noise phase as well. After the computing servers generate secret-shared Irwin-Hall approximated Gaussian noise (see Protocol 5 based on [23]), we flatten the secret-shared marginal vectors and inject this noise in a single vectorized addition in Protocol 4. This replaces thousands of element-wise network requests with one collective batched operation, resulting in significantly faster compilation.

# 5 Empirical Evaluation Results 

Datasets. We evaluate our approach on four datasets across different cancer types. The first dataset, which we designate as ALL, contains 1,181 samples across four leukemia subtypes [27]; it was used in a previous study of centralised SDG methods for genomic data [4]. The second, which we designate as AML, is a more recent dataset of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) subtypes containing 1,224 bulk RNA-seq samples across 14 subtype labels [25]. We also evaluate our method on two public benchmark datasets from the CAMDA 2025 competition on privacy-preserving genomic data generation: TCGA-BRCA, a breast cancer dataset with 1,089 samples across five subtypes, and TCGA-COMBINED, a larger collection of 4,323 samples spanning ten cancer tissue types. Together, these datasets span diverse domains, class distributions, and label cardinalities, enabling a thorough evaluation of our method under conditions analogous to real-world federated rare disease settings. We follow the preprocessing pipeline of Chen et al. (2024) [4]. We apply DESeq2 normalization [13] to minimize the influence of varying sequencing depths and RNA compositions, followed by gene filtering and ensuring log-normalization to account for the well-known skewness of RNA-seq counts. The categorical labels are encoded as integers. After filtering, the datasets ALL, AML, TCGA-BRCA, and TCGA-COMB were reduced to 958, 1,000, 978, and 978 features, respectively.

Discussion. Our evaluation demonstrates that the proposed MPC protocol (input privacy and output privacy) achieves performance on par with both non-private (no input privacy, no output privacy) and centralized (only output privacy) baselines. Specifically, as the privacy budget ( ϵ)increases, the synthetic data approaches the utility and biological value of non-private real data, in the downstream classification task accuracy (Fig. 2), differential expression preservation (Fig. 5 in Appendix C), and one dimensional marginal distribution similarity (Fig. 4 in Appendix C). Our approach consistency delivers fidelity and utility comparable to the centralized setting, as can be seen in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4 in Appendix C. Importantly, these privacy-preserving benefits are achieved at a low computational cost; as detailed in Tab. 1, protocol compilation times are negligible (under 10 seconds), and execution times remain feasible across both active and passive attacker scenarios, even as the number of features increase 3.

# 6 Conclusion 

We introduced an efficient MPC-based protocol for privacy-preserving generation of synthetic bulk RNA-seq data in federated settings, combining strong input privacy with formal differential privacy guarantees for released data. By re-engineering Private-PGM for encrypted, high-dimensional ge-nomics data and leveraging vectorized MPC primitives, our approach attains utility and biological fidelity comparable to centralized baselines while remaining computationally practical under both passive and active adversary models. Empirical results across multiple real-world cancer datasets demonstrate that high-quality synthetic genomic data can be generated jointly across institutions without disclosing raw patient records. We view this work as a concrete step toward alleviating per-sistent data-sharing barriers in genomics and enabling secure, scalable collaboration in rare disease and multi-site biomedical research.

13 A MPC Primitives 

In our proposed MPC protocol for federated synthetic data generation, we build upon the cryp-tographic primitives available in the MP-SPDZ [11] library. We use the following primitives as sub-protocols: 

• Secure random number generation πRAND : At the start of this protocol, the parties (MPC servers) agree on a fractional precision l for the power 2 ring representation; at the end, they have a secret sharing [[ r]] of a uniformly distributed random number r, generated securely via the bitwise XOR of locally generated random bits. 

• Secure sorting πSORT : At the start of this protocol, the parties have a secret-shared array of 

n elements [[ x]] (and optionally an associated array of payloads [[ y]]); at the end, they have a secret-shared array [[ x′]] where the elements are sorted according to a specified order, without revealing the underlying values or the resulting data permutation to any party. 

• Secure multiplication πMUL : At the start of this protocol, the parties have secret-shared values [[ a]] and [[ b]]; at the end, they have a secret sharing of the product [[ c]], where c = a · b.

• Secure division πDIV : At the start of this protocol, the parties have secret-shared values [[ a]] and [[ b]]; at the end, they have a secret sharing of the quotient [[ c]], where c = a/b .

• Secure dot product πDOT : At the start of this protocol, the parties have secret shares of two vectors [[ a]] and [[ b]] of length n; at the end, they have a secret sharing of their dot product [[ c]], where c = Pni=1 aibi.

• Secure equality test πEQ : At the start of this protocol, the parties have a secret-shared values [[ a]] and [[ b]]; at the end, they have a secret shared [[ c]], where c = 1 if a = b, and c = 0 otherwise. 

• Secure less than test πLT : At the start of this protocol, the parties have secret-shared values [[ a]] and [[ b]]; at the end, they have a secret shared [[ c]], where c = 1 if a < b , and c = 0 otherwise. 

• Secure reveal πREVEAL : At the start of this protocol, the parties have a secret-shared value [[ a]], with the shares of a spread across K servers; at the end, the shares are aggregated and reconstructed so that the parties have the decrypted plaintext value a.We build our solution using these cryptographic primitives to ensure that our final solution is also cryptographically secure, based on the universal composition theorem, which states that a protocol remains secure when composed with other or the same MPC protocols [2].
