# Federated Few-Shot Learning on Neuromorphic Hardware: An Empirical Study Across Physical Edge Nodes 

## Steven Motta Gioele Nanni March 13, 2026 

# Abstract 

Federated learning on neuromorphic hardware remains unexplored because on-chip spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) produces binary weight updates rather than the floating-point gradients assumed by standard algorithms. We build a two-node federated system with BrainChip Akida AKD1000 processors and run approximately 1,580 experimental trials across seven analysis phases. Of four weight-exchange strate-gies tested, neuron-level concatenation (FedUnion) con-sistently preserves accuracy while element-wise weight averaging (FedAvg) destroys it ( p = 0 .002 ). Domain-adaptive fine-tuning of the upstream feature extractor accounts for most of the accuracy gains, confirming fea-ture quality as the dominant factor. Scaling feature di-mensionality from 64 to 256 yields 77 .0% best-strategy federated accuracy ( n = 30 , p < 0.001 ). Two indepen-dent asymmetries (wider features help federation more than individual learning, while binarization hurts fed-eration more) point to a shared prototype complemen-tarity mechanism: cross-node transfer scales with the distinctiveness of neuron prototypes. 

# 1 Introduction 

Neuromorphic processors such as the BrainChip Akida AKD1000 [BrainChip, 2023a] execute spiking neural network (SNN) [Maass, 1997, Roy et al., 2019] infer-ence and on-chip learning at milliwatt power budgets, making them attractive for edge deployment [Shi et al., 2016]. Their native learning mechanism, spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) [Bi and Poo, 1998], pro-duces sparse binary weight updates rather than the floating-point gradients that federated learning algo-rithms [McMahan et al., 2017, Kairouz et al., 2021] ex-pect. No prior work has attempted to federate STDP-learned models across physical neuromorphic devices: existing studies operate entirely in simulation and rely on surrogate-gradient training instead of on-chip plas-ticity. We build a two-node federated system with Akida AKD1000 coprocessors on Raspberry Pi 5 boards and run approximately 1,580 experimental trials across seven analysis phases. Our contributions are: 1. A two-stage architecture that works around the Akida IP version mismatch (v2 feature extrac-tor, v1 STDP edge learner) together with an on-device fine-tuning pipeline using quantization-aware training [Jacob et al., 2018] on commodity Raspberry Pi hardware. 2. Four federation strategies for STDP weight matrices. Neuron-level concatenation (FedUnion) preserves accuracy across all tested conditions; element-wise averaging (FedAvg) consistently de-stroys the sparse selectivity patterns that STDP produces. 3. An experimental framework covering 42 STDP configurations × up to 30 trials × seven analysis phases ( ∼1,580 runs total), with seeded splits, soft-ware baselines, bootstrap CIs, and Cohen’s d effect sizes. 4. Feature dimensionality scaling from 64 to 256 dimensions, and a prototype complementarity 

mechanism that explains two observed asymme-tries: wider features amplify the federation gain while binarization amplifies the federation penalty. 

# 2 Related Work 

Neuromorphic Computing and SNNs. Spiking neural networks (SNNs) [Maass, 1997] communicate via discrete spikes rather than continuous activations, enabling event-driven computation at milliwatt-scale power [Roy et al., 2019, Pfeiffer and Pfeil, 2018]. Hard-ware platforms include Intel Loihi [Davies et al., 2018], a research manycore chip with programmable learn-ing rules, and IBM TrueNorth [Merolla et al., 2014], which integrated one million neurons on a single die. The BrainChip Akida AKD1000 [BrainChip, 2023a] differs from these research platforms in that it ships as a standard PCIe peripheral and supports on-chip STDP-based learning [Bi and Poo, 1998] alongside in-ference. Akida has been applied to single-device key-word spotting [BrainChip, 2023a,b] and visual classifi-cation [BrainChip, 2023c]; no multi-device or federated deployments have been reported. 

Federated Learning. Federated Averaging (Fe-dAvg) [McMahan et al., 2017] trains models across de-centralized nodes without sharing raw data. Subse-quent work has tackled non-IID distributions [Li et al., 2020, Zhao et al., 2018], communication cost [Konecny et al., 2016], device heterogeneity [Li et al., 2023], and numerous open problems surveyed by Kairouz et al. 1

Federated Learning on Neuromorphic Hard-ware. Table 1 summarises the small body of work in this area; all of it is simulation-only. Skatchkovsky et al. [2020] trained SNNs on MNIST-DVS across two simulated nodes, transmitting only weights above a threshold and showing convergence on par with cen-tralized training at lower communication cost. Yang et al. [2022] proposed LEAD, a leader-election proto-col in which the best-performing node’s weights guide aggregation; on SHD and DVS-Gesture with 3+ simu-lated nodes they reported 94 –96% accuracy. Venkate-sha et al. [2021] federated SNNs on CIFAR-10 with 5+ simulated clients using surrogate-gradient backpropaga-tion. A common thread is that all three rely on surro-gate gradients rather than on-chip STDP, and none de-ploy on physical hardware. Our experiments are, to our knowledge, the first to exchange STDP-learned weights between physical neuromorphic processors. 

Few-Shot Learning and Transfer Learning. Few-shot learning aims to classify new categories from min-imal examples [Wang et al., 2020]. Prototypical net-works [Snell et al., 2017] learn embedding spaces in which class prototypes are compared via distance func-tions; STDP’s per-neuron weight vectors play an anal-ogous role, as we discuss in §6.1. Fine-tuning pre-trained feature extractors for new domains [Pan and Yang, 2010] is well established for CNNs but unexplored in combination with neuromorphic STDP learners that require binary inputs. Feature binarization has been studied in binary neural networks [Courbariaux et al., 2016], yet how binarization interacts with federated ag-gregation of STDP weights is an open question that our experiments address directly. 

# 3 Proposed Method 

## 3.1 Two-Stage Pipeline: Bridging IP Versions 

A critical engineering constraint is the Akida SDK’s IP version architecture. The pretrained DS-CNN keyword spotting model [Zhang et al., 2017] converts to Akida v2, but the FullyConnected layer required for STDP edge learning is only available in v1. These versions 

cannot be mixed within a single model. Our solution is a two-stage pipeline :1. Stage 1, Feature Extractor (v2, software): 

The DS-CNN [Zhang et al., 2017, BrainChip, 2023b] is converted from Keras to Akida v2, with the classification head removed. In the base con-figuration, this maps MFCC spectrograms [Davis and Mermelstein, 1980] of shape (49 , 10 , 1) to 64-dimensional int8 feature vectors. In Phase 2+, the DS-CNN is fine-tuned on target classes before head removal (§4.2). 2. Stage 2, Edge Learner (v1, hardware): Astandalone v1 model with a single FullyConnected 

layer compiled with the AkidaUnsupervised 

STDP optimizer. It accepts 1-bit binary inputs and maps to the AKD1000 for on-chip learning. 

Wide feature variants. For feature dimensional-ity scaling experiments (§5.5), the DS-CNN classifi-cation head is replaced with a wider projection: a

QuantizedDense layer with 128 or 256 output units, followed by a Dequantizer . After cnn2snn.convert() ,which stops at the Dequantizer boundary, the result-ing Akida model outputs 64-dimensional features as before. The wider projection weights are saved sepa-rately as NumPy .npy files, and the final wide features are computed via post-Akida matrix multiplication in NumPy: fwide = f64 · Wproj . This workaround addresses a cnn2snn limitation where non-quantized layers after the Dequantizer are ignored during conversion. 

Hardware boundary. The NumPy projection means that for wide feature experiments, only the 64-dim backbone feature extraction runs through the Akida-converted model (on the ARM CPU, not the AKD1000), and the 64 → D projection is entirely in software. The STDP edge learner still runs on-chip on the AKD1000, so on-chip learning is preserved, but the feature pipeline is increasingly software-dependent. We return to this caveat in §6.7. Between stages, features are binarized using per-feature thresholds computed from training data (§3.4). Figure 1 illustrates the complete two-stage pipeline. 

## 3.2 Federation Strategies

Reporting convention. We report two federated metrics: (1) per-strategy accuracy (e.g., FedUnion ac-curacy), which reflects a deployable system choice; and (2) best-strategy accuracy , the maximum across all four strategies per trial. The best-strategy metric is an 

oracle upper bound : it assumes the operator knows which strategy performs best for each configuration and trial. We emphasize per-strategy results (particularly FedUnion) as the primary comparison, and flag best-strategy figures as upper bounds where they appear. 

## 3.3 Multi-Round Federation 

We execute 5 rounds of iterative federation. After each round, merged weights are injected into both nodes and local STDP retraining continues from the federated starting point. We compare two retraining regimes: FedAvg-based (injecting element-wise aver-aged weights before retraining) and FedUnion-based 

(injecting concatenated neuron prototypes before re-training). Under FedUnion-based retraining, neuron counts stabilize after round 1, as both nodes start from the same merged model.

## 3.5 STDP Edge Learning Configuration 

The STDP edge learner is parameterized by three hy-perparameters: 

• Neurons per class (npc ): Number of STDP neu-rons allocated to each class, each learning a sparse prototype vector via competitive Hebbian learn-ing [Bi and Poo, 1998]. 

• num_weights (nw ): Number of input connections per neuron (out of D). Sparsity is critical because 

nw = D causes all weights to saturate at 1, pre-venting learning. 

• learning_competition (lc ): Lateral inhibition strength during STDP, controlling the degree of winner-take-all competition among neurons. 

• input_bits = 1 : Binary inputs required for STDP (fixed). 3Phase 1 uses the default configuration (nw = 20 ,npc = 50 , lc = 0 .1). Phase 2 and Phase 3 sweep over all three parameters (§4.6). 

# 4 Experimental Setup 

## 4.1 Hardware 

Our system consists of two nodes connected by a dedi-cated Ethernet link (Figure 2): 

• Node “Claudio” : Raspberry Pi 5 (BCM2712, 8 GB RAM) with BrainChip Akida AKD1000 via PCIe, direct IP 10.0.0.1. 

• Node “Paolo” : Raspberry Pi 5 (BCM2712, 8 GB RAM) with BrainChip Akida AKD1000 via PCIe, direct IP 10.0.0.2. Both run Raspberry Pi OS (kernel 6.12.47) with the Akida PCIe kernel module and Python 3.12 virtual environments containing Akida SDK 2.19.1, Quan-tizeML 1.2.3, and TensorFlow 2.19.1. For autonomous operation, the orchestrator runs di-rectly on Claudio, coordinating both nodes over the di-rect Ethernet link. This eliminates dependence on ex-ternal connectivity; a Mac provides remote monitoring via Tailscale VPN. 

## 4.2 Feature Extractor Fine-Tuning 

The pretrained DS-CNN [Zhang et al., 2017] (23,779 parameters, 33 output classes) was trained on Google Speech Commands’ original vocabulary, which excludes our target classes (backward, follow, forward). Phase 1 results (§5.1) revealed that feature quality is the dominant performance bottleneck, motivating domain-adaptive fine-tuning [Pan and Yang, 2010]. We fine-tune directly on the Raspberry Pi 5 ARM CPU using quantization-aware training [Jacob et al., 2018]: 1. Architecture modification : The 33-class classi-fication head is replaced with a 3-class head using 

QuantizedDense(3) from QuantizeML. 2. Selective unfreezing : All layers up to the third depthwise separable block are frozen (10 of 20 lay-ers); the final two separable blocks plus the new head are trainable. 3. Training : Adam optimizer (lr = 0 .001 ), sparse categorical cross-entropy, batch size 32, 20 epochs, 80/20 train/validation split. Training data: 

∼4,000 WAV files across 3 classes from Speech Commands. 4. Conversion : The model converts directly to Akida via cnn2snn.convert() . The 3-class head is then popped to yield the headless feature extrac-tor. Fine-tuning completed in 139 seconds on the RPi 5, reaching 81 .1% validation accuracy. Three fine-tuning variants are used across phases: 

• Target-finetuned : Fine-tuned on the target classes (backward/follow/forward). Used in Phases 2–3. 

• Disjoint-finetuned : Fine-tuned on non-target Speech Commands classes (yes/no/stop), exclud-ing all target classes. Used in Phase 3 disjoint val-idation (§5.4). 

• Wide variants : Target-finetuned with 128- or 256-dimensional projection heads (§5.5). 

## 4.3 Dataset and Non-IID Partitioning 

We use Google Speech Commands v0.02 [Warden, 2018], selecting three novel classes excluded from the pretrained DS-CNN’s vocabulary: backward , fol-low , and forward . Audio samples are preprocessed into Mel-frequency cepstral coefficient (MFCC) spec-trograms [Davis and Mermelstein, 1980] of shape 

(49 , 10 , 1) .Data is partitioned non-IID to simulate realistic edge deployment where each node encounters different data distributions [Zhao et al., 2018]: 

• Shared class (backward): Both nodes receive 50 training samples. 

• Claudio exclusive (follow): Only Claudio re-ceives 50 samples. 

• Paolo exclusive (forward): Only Paolo receives 50 samples. Each node trains on 100 samples (50 per class). Ashared evaluation set of 100 samples per class (300 total) covers all three classes. To investigate feature extractor alignment, we additionally evaluate a pre-trained class set (yes , no , stop ) within the original DS-CNN vocabulary. For the disjoint validation experiments (§5.4), the feature extractor is fine-tuned on the pretrained class set (yes/no/stop) rather than the target classes, ensur-ing the extractor has never seen backward, follow, or forward during any training stage. 

## 4.4 Seeded Multi-Trial Design 

We run 10 independent trials with different random seeds (seed ∈ { 42 , 43 , . . . , 51 }). Each seed determines which audio samples are selected for training and eval-uation via seeded shuffling, with non-overlapping splits enforced.

Software baselines are numpy-only implementations running directly on the Raspberry Pi, evaluated along-side STDP in every trial: 

• k-NN (k = 5 ) [Cover and Hart, 1967]: Evalu-ated on both binary features (Hamming distance) and int8 features (Euclidean distance). ## 5.1 Phase 1: Federation Strategy Anal-ysis (Default Configuration) 

Phase 1 uses the original pretrained DS-CNN (no fine-tuning) with default STDP parameters (nw = 20 ,npc = 50 , lc = 0 .1). Statistical significance is assessed via the Wilcoxon signed-rank test comparing each fed-erated strategy against individual accuracy. Table 2 presents the primary Phase 1 results. The strategies partition into three groups: 1. Structure-preserving (FedUnion, FedBest): Maintain or improve accuracy (47 .3–48 .0% ). FedUnion significantly improves Paolo’s accuracy (44 .4 → 48 .0% , p = 0 .002 ) by providing neurons for its unseen class. 2. Weight-averaging (FedAvg): Significantly de-grades accuracy to 38 .4% (p = 0 .002 ). Element-wise averaging of integer STDP weights destroys sparse selectivity patterns. 3. Partial averaging (FedMajority): Intermediate at 41 .2% . Max-pooling preserves more structure than averaging but still disrupts learned patterns. STDP ( 47 .3% ) significantly outperforms both linear (40 .8% , p = 0 .002 ) and MLP ( 37 .1% , p = 0 .002 ) clas-sifiers despite giving gradient baselines 50 epochs with cosine annealing. However, k-NN ( 52 .0% ) outperforms STDP without any learning phase.

## 5.4 Disjoint Class Validation 

Table 9 addresses the data asymmetry concern (§6.6): when the feature extractor is fine-tuned on entirely dif-ferent classes (yes/no/stop) and has never seen the target classes (backward/follow/forward), STDP still achieves 56 .2% best federated accuracy, 13 .0 pp be-low the target-finetuned extractor ( 69 .2% ). While this demonstrates that STDP can learn novel class structure from domain-adapted features, the 13 .0 pp gap is sub-stantial, indicating that class-specific feature alignment contributes meaningfully to overall accuracy. ## 5.10 Communication Efficiency and En-ergy 

Weight exchange remains extremely lightweight: 

• Per-node payload: npc × 3 × D int8 values. At npc = 25 , D = 64 : 4,800 bytes; at D = 256 : 19,200 bytes. 

• Total exchanged per round (both directions +metadata): ≈20–40 KB depending on feature di-mensionality. This is orders of magnitude smaller than typical fed-erated learning. A single FedAvg round with a ResNet-18 requires ∼45 MB per node [McMahan et al., 2017]. On-chip STDP learning requires ≈150 mW × 5 ms 

= 0 .75 mJ per node. The complete pipeline (including on-device fine-tuning, feature extraction, STDP learn-ing, and weight exchange) runs in under 3 minutes per experimental trial. Caveat: Feature extraction cur-rently runs on the ARM CPU ( ∼500 mJ per 100 sam-ples), dominating total system energy. 

# 6 Discussion 

A single question threads through the results: what determines how much accuracy a node gains from its partner’s weights? We argue below that the answer is prototype complementarity , and that this mechanism explains the observed effects of aggregation strategy, feature width, and binarization alike.

Both asymmetries are explained by prototype com-plementarity : the degree to which neuron prototypes from different nodes encode non-overlapping informa-tion. Each STDP neuron learns a sparse binary weight vector of length D, selecting nw of D input features. When D = 64 and nw = 30 , each neuron’s prototype covers 47% of the feature space. Two neurons from dif-ferent nodes that respond to the same concept will have highly overlapping weight patterns, limiting the new in-formation gained by FedUnion concatenation. When 

D = 256 and nw = 100 , each prototype covers only 39% 

of the space, making neurons from different nodes more likely to select complementary subsets of features. Binarization reduces complementarity from the oppo-site direction: collapsing int8 features to 1-bit discards the magnitude variation that would otherwise differen-tiate prototypes. In other words, any intervention that increases prototype distinctiveness ampli-fies the federation advantage , while any interven-tion that reduces it amplifies the federation penalty. This is consistent with the finding that npc (neurons per class) has minimal effect, as the bottleneck is per-neuron diversity, not neuron count. The mechanism predicts that the federation advan-tage would diminish with IID data (since prototypes would converge regardless of feature width). How-ever, our N = 4 simulation (§5.8) reveals an important caveat: adding more nodes with proportionally less per-node data does not increase net prototype quality, be-cause the diversity gain from additional participants is offset by the quality loss from smaller local datasets ( 25 

vs. 50 samples per class). 

## 6.2 Why Neuron-Level Aggregation Works 

STDP training produces neurons whose weight vectors are sparse and sharply tuned: each neuron fires for a narrow subset of inputs, with a few strong connections encoding that selectivity [Bi and Poo, 1998]. Element-wise averaging of two such vectors smears the strong connections toward the mean and destroys the selec-tivity pattern, much as averaging two different faces produces a blurry composite. Concatenating the vec-tors keeps both prototypes intact and lets the classifier pick the one that best matches each test input, an idea closely related to prototypical networks [Snell et al., 2017]. This explains why FedUnion dominates across all phases, configurations, and multi-round settings, while FedAvg degrades cumulatively as it compounds the av-eraging distortion over rounds. 

## 6.3 Feature Quality as the Dominant Factor 

Every experiment we ran points to the same conclu-sion: the quality of the feature extractor matters more than any other design choice. Switching from the origi-nal DS-CNN vocabulary to in-domain classes (Phase 1) yields a double-digit k-NN improvement. Domain-adaptive fine-tuning [Pan and Yang, 2010] with identi-cal STDP hyperparameters produces the single largest accuracy jump in the study (Table 7), accounting for roughly nine-tenths of the total individual gain. Widen-ing the feature space from 64 to 256 dimensions adds a further large step in federated accuracy (Table 10), and unlike hyperparameter tuning, shows no sign of satura-tion at the widths we tested. The mechanism behind the width effect is straightfor-ward: each STDP neuron connects to nw of D inputs, so a larger D lets neurons select more discriminative subsets without covering half the feature space. The practical cost (a 4× increase in weight payload) takes the per-round exchange from ∼5 KB to ∼20 KB, still negligible on any real network link. 10 6.4 The Binarization Bottleneck 

The fine-tuned DS-CNN reaches 81% validation accu-racy, yet STDP on the same 64-dim features tops out around 69% after binarization, a gap of roughly 12 pp. Comparing k-NN on int8 vs. binary features (Table 6) isolates the binarization cost: it is six times larger in the federated setting than individually, consistent with the prototype complementarity picture. Entropy thresh-olds do not recover the lost information ( p > 0.13 at every dimensionality tested). Widening features to 256 dimensions narrows the gap to the upstream classifier to about 4 pp by providing more binary bits in aggregate, but does not close it entirely. 

## 6.5 Value Proposition of Neuromorphic Hardware 

The headline accuracy comparison deserves direct con-frontation: k-NN on int8 features achieves 76 .1% fed-erated accuracy with no neuromorphic hardware what-soever , while the best STDP result ( 77 .0% ) relies on a pipeline where only the final STDP learning step exe-cutes on the AKD1000. The effective delta is +0 .9 pp, within experimental noise. The case for neuromorphic hardware rests on deploy-ment advantages :

• Constant-time inference. The STDP edge layer runs fixed-latency spike-based inference ( ∼5 ms), whereas k-NN [Cover and Hart, 1967] requires 

O(nD ) distance computation per query. 

• Native weight exchange. STDP produces a compact int8 weight matrix (20–40 KB) that con-stitutes the federation payload. k-NN federation requires transmitting all training features, a 10 ×

larger payload. 

• Energy. On-chip STDP learning operates at milli-watt budgets versus orders-of-magnitude more for software k-NN on the ARM CPU. The takeaway is not that STDP beats software classi-fiers (it does not), but that on-chip learning can match them while offering architectural advantages that soft-ware methods lack. 

## 6.6 Data Asymmetry Resolved: Dis-joint Validation 

A methodological tension existed: the fine-tuned fea-ture extractor had seen the target classes, partially un-dermining the few-shot framing. The disjoint valida-tion (§5.4) addresses this: a feature extractor fine-tuned on entirely different classes achieves 56 .2% best feder-ated accuracy, 13 .0 pp below target-finetuned ( 69 .2% ). The disjoint result still exceeds the Phase 1 unfinetuned baseline ( 47 .8% ) by +8 .4 pp, confirming that domain-adaptive fine-tuning provides useful features even for unseen classes. The deployment scenario (fine-tune on a representative speech domain, then learn novel classes via STDP) is viable, but the class-alignment bonus is a significant performance factor. 

## 6.7 Limitations 

1. Two-node setup and FedUnion scalability. 

The primary experiments use N = 2 nodes. The N = 4 simulation (§5.8) reveals no benefit: FedUnion-N4 is indistinguishable from FedUnion-N2 ( p = 0.28 ), and FedAvg-N4 is significantly worse ( p = 0 .01 ). The simulation confounds node count with per-node data quantity, so whether 

N > 2 would help with fixed per-node data remains untested. At N > 2, FedUnion’s neuron count scales linearly with participants, creating scalabil-ity pressure on inference latency. 2. Single dataset, single domain, three classes. All experiments use Google Speech Commands [Warden, 2018] with three keyword classes. No cross-validation across different class sets, datasets, or task domains is performed. 3. Neuromorphic hardware contributes negli-gible accuracy at the highest pipeline levels. 

As discussed in §6.5, the +0 .9 pp delta is within ex-perimental noise; the case for the AKD1000 rests on deployment advantages rather than accuracy gains. 4. Statistical power. Phases B, D, and F use n = 10 

trials, adequate for large effects but insufficient for moderate ones. Critical comparisons (Phases C, E, H) use n = 30 trials. 5. No adaptive multi-round protocol. Current multi-round retraining uses fixed parameters across rounds. Adaptive schemes might enable genuine iterative improvement. 6. Software-dependent feature pipeline. The wide feature projection (§3.1) is entirely in soft-ware, so the highest-accuracy results depend on an increasingly software-heavy pipeline. 

# 7 Conclusion 

This paper reports, to our knowledge, the first federated learning experiments on physical neuromorphic hard-ware, totaling ∼1,580 trials on two Akida AKD1000 processors. Three results stand out. First, neuron-level aggregation (FedUnion) consistently outperforms weight averaging (Fe-dAvg) , across every phase, configuration, and multi-round setting we tested. The reason is that STDP neu-rons encode knowledge as sparse prototype vectors; av-eraging destroys these prototypes, while concatenation preserves them. Second, feature quality is the single most im-portant lever . Fine-tuning the upstream DS-CNN accounts for roughly nine-tenths of the accuracy gain over the baseline, and widening the feature space from 64 to 256 dimensions pushes the best federated result to 77 .0% (n = 30 , p < 0.001 ). 11 Third, both the feature-width and binarization ex-periments point to a shared prototype complemen-tarity mechanism: any change that makes per-node prototypes more distinctive amplifies the federation gain, while any change that makes them more similar (e.g., binarization, or splitting data across more nodes) reduces it. The complete pipeline runs on commodity Raspberry Pi hardware in under three minutes per trial and ex-changes only 20–40 KB of weights per round.
