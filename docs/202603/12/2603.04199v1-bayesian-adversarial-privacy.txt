# Bayesian Adversarial Privacy 

Cameron Bell ∗, Timothy Johnston ∗, Antoine Luciano ∗, and Christian P Robert ∗,†

Abstract. 

Theoretical and applied research into privacy encompasses an incredibly broad swathe of differing approaches, emphasis and aims. This work introduces a new quantitative notion of privacy that is both contextual and specific. We argue that it provides a more meaningful notion of privacy than the widely utilised framework of differential privacy and a more explicit and rigorous formulation than what is commonly used in statistical disclosure theory. Our definition relies on concepts inherent to standard Bayesian decision theory, while departing from it in several important respects. In particular, the party controlling the release of sensitive information should make disclosure decisions from the prior viewpoint, rather than conditional on the data, even when the data is itself observed. Illuminating toy examples and computational methods are discussed in high detail in order to highlight the specificities of the method. 

MSC2020 subject classifications: Primary 62C10, 62F15; secondary 62B10. 

Keywords: Bayesian decision theory, data privacy, privacy-utility trade-off, statistical disclosure control, differential privacy, ex ante risk. 

# 1 Introduction 

Privacy is an important topic of research in law, philosophy, and data science, and exten-sive corpora of work are to be found in each of these domains. In legal and philosophical fields, considerable attention has been devoted to clarifying the concept of privacy, e.g., whether or not it depends on malicious intent, whether or not it should be grounded in contract or tort law, and how it relates to broader social norms. However, majority of the scholars in these fields adopt the position that privacy occurs within a rich web of convention and context, see e.g. Nissenbaum (2004); Cofone (2023); Rachels (1975). Comparatively, in order to be mathematically tractable, research into privacy in data science and mathematics usually considers privacy more formally and therefore abstractly. Let us consider two common frameworks. 

## 1.1 Differential Privacy 

Over the past two decades, a substantial portion of the statistical and theoretical computer science literature on data privacy has focused on Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006), a perturbative framework that constrains the sensitivity of an output, released, distribution as a function of its input, private, data.

An equivalent formulation in terms of hypothesis testing is given in Balle et al. (2020). The formal and context-independent nature of this framework yields several attrac-tive properties. It admits precise mathematical analysis and composes coherently under natural probabilistic operations (Dwork and Roth, 2014). A substantial literature has developed comparing alternative definitions (Desfontaines and Pejó, 2019), establishing privacy guarantees for broad classes of algorithms (Chourasia et al., 2021), and studying the trade-off between privacy constraints and statistical accuracy (Eigner et al., 2014). At the same time, differential privacy as defined above is formulated independently of any prior distribution, statistical consideration, adversarial strategies, or task-specific loss function. Its guarantees are therefore uniform and worst-case in nature. While this robustness is perceived as a central strength of the concept, it also implies that privacy protection is not calibrated relative to a specific inferential objective or contextual no-tion of risk. Several works have highlighted structural tensions between uniform privacy guarantees and highly structured inferential settings (Kifer and Machanavajjhala, 2011; Ghosh and Kleinberg, 2016a), and it is unlikely that differential privacy type definitions could ever protect against inferences (Near et al., 2025). In addition, adding randomiza-tion results in inefficiency for statistical inference without necessarily protecting privacy (Kenny et al., 2021). Another objection (Bambauer et al., 2013) is that DP is simply impractical, in the sense that the privacy parameters of realistic algorithms end up being extremely large significant, hence enabling losses of privacy. An important parameter ϵ, as given in (1), has essentially the same role in all DP definitions. It is intended to take a value close to zero. However, it typically accumulates linearly with each operation performed, and as a result, in applications ϵ is usually above one and sometimes even higher than twenty; see (Del Vasto-Terrientes et al., 2025). For this reason, DP may be more fruitfully considered as a kind of theoretical model, or as a kind of sensitivity analysis. Indeed, ideas developed in the community may be useful for those studying data privacy from other perspectives. However, for more realistic problems, there have been many difficulties in using differential privacy to obtain meaningful privacy guarantees in practice (Del Vasto-Terrientes et al., 2025). ## 1.2 Statistical Disclosure Control 

The notion of Statistical Disclosure Control (SDC), developed mainly by national statis-tics institutes (NSIs), seeks to process statistical data so that it may be disclosed without leaking confidential information. The handbook by Hundepool et al. (2012) provides an excellent introduction to this subject. It covers census data releases as well as data orig-inating from business and social surveys. Formally, as for DP, an SDC procedure may be viewed as a transformation or randomised mechanism q(· | x). The central objective of SDC is to balance disclosure risk and data utility .Types of disclosure risk include identity disclosure , which occurs when a specific unit can be re-identified from the released data, and attribute disclosure , which arises when sensitive characteristics can be inferred, even without explicit identification. Such risks may stem from low-frequency counts or from external information available to an adversary (so-called linkage attacks). In order to achieve a balance with data utility, it may be necessary to process the data, for instance by adding noise, merging cells in tabular data, simply suppressing contentious aspects, or providing synthetic data. These operations can be encoded via different criteria in the software packages μ-ARGUS and 

τ -ARGUS, widely used by NSIs (Hundepool, 2004). After transformation, disclosure risk is evaluated using deterministic criteria applied to the released dataset η ∼ q(· | x).For example, a dataset satisfies k-anonymity if there is no record that does not appear at least k times, meaning that, even if an adversary can match this record with its appearance in another dataset, there are k possible ways of doing so (Slavković and Seeman, 2023). In parallel, information loss measures are used to assess the degradation in statis-tical utility induced by the transformation. These may include comparisons between original and released tabulations, preservation of marginal totals, covariance structures, or other summary statistics. The aim is to ensure that q reduces disclosure risk without excessively distorting important properties of the data. For illustrative purposes, consider a town with only two companies from a given sector. Since each company knows their own revenue, publishing a merged statistic for the revenue of both companies enables both companies to learn the revenue data of their competitor. For this reason, it may in this case be necessary to merge this town’s data with another town’s, thereby reducing disclosure risk. Unlike differential privacy, SDC does not impose a uniform worst-case constraint on the mechanism q. Risk is instead evaluated empirically for the realised dataset. More-over, SDC is typically not formulated within a Bayesian framework: prior information about x or about an adversary’s knowledge is usually not explicitly modelled when as-sessing disclosure risk or information loss. This is a significant drawback since, in most cases, published data follow a history of prior releases, from which informative priors can be constructed. Furthermore, in order to protect privacy, it is often necessary to hide the exact method of disclosure used. As stated in Slavković and Seeman (2023), the implemented method “could not be disclosed transparently without revealing probabilistic informa-tion about the individuals whose data were altered due to disclosure limitation”. This 4 Bayesian Adversarial Privacy 

can lead to confusion for users, since adding noise may lead to small inconsistencies (Eurostat, 2024). More importantly, the secrecy about the release mechanism weakens statistical analysis, since the statistical model is partly unknown and the resulting vari-ance due to, say, adding noise is not accounted for. Additionally, this means that an adversary could potentially derive more information from the release than intended, given structural information that could, in itself, be inferred. 

## 1.3 What Should a Quantitative Definition of Privacy Deliver? 

To summarise the above discussion, DP is theoretically elegant and illuminating, but does not provide a very reassuring guarantee in practice. On the other hand, SDC is well established, but uses methods of assessment that are somewhat ad hoc and rely on the secrecy of the disclosure method. How then to come up with a privacy framework which is rigorous and explicit, but meaningful in a real-world context? Furthermore, in order to proceed in a Bayesian fashion, with the goal of deliver-ing optimal inference, we shall have to assume that all actors act rationally, i.e. their decisions are based on some mathematical construct. Privacy is often viewed through a more human lens, though, and it may therefore require context beyond our mathe-matical framework. As pointed out in Cofone (2023), when considered more universally, privacy is not “information”, and information releases that are hidden away on, say, a government data release will be perceived as less privacy-violating than those, say, publicised by mass media. One may then discuss how much context should be included in the statistical ap-proach to privacy. Adopting a Bayesian model requires setting a prior (or prior pre-dictive) distribution for the data. Conditionally on our chosen release mechanism and the subsequent output, this prior yields a posterior distribution on the data that fully describes what an adversary could extract from a given release. Alongside the distribution of the data, the goals of the statistical experiment are also important in order to measure the suitability of a release mechanism. Since conceal-ing information from adversaries would also reduce information available to “friendly” statisticians (there is no “free lunch” in privacy Kifer and Machanavajjhala (2011)), we wish to be as specific as possible about our aims. To this end, our measure of utility and privacy shall be explicitly linked to two decisions made by both a statistician and an adversary, respectively, the success of each being measured by a loss function. Within this formalised environment, we can proceed directly and efficiently from what we wish to hide and what we wish to achieve, and how exactly we wish to quantify the success of these goals. This construct is, in a sense, achieving the most general definition possible by positing “privacy as a trade-off” in a rational Bayesian setting, thus formalised by a utility function (Robert, 2001). Since the posterior distribution of the data (and of any parameter we are inferring) given the release depends on the release mechanism over the entire data space, it comes to reason that we cannot choose the method of release based on the observed data only. Intuitively, doing so would itself reveal additional information about this data. Therefore, we will measure and choose the suitability of a given data release mechanism Bell, Johnston, Luciano and Robert 5by integrating over the data prior. In this way, we follow the convention of the literature on Bayesian experimental design (Chaloner and Verdinelli, 1995; Rainforth et al., 2024). 

## 1.4 Existing perspectives 

Let us conclude by providing an entry on the related statistical literature. The production on DP is so vast that we shall only survey perspectives somewhat adjacent to our own. Connections with Bayesian statistics have been given in a number of works. The “random mechanism” of posterior sampling is considered in works such as Dimitrakakis et al. (2017), although this is essentially just a variant of classical DP. In particular, the data are considered fixed (not sampled from the prior distribution) as in classical DP. More meaningfully Bayesian DP variants were explored in Ghosh and Kleinberg (2016b); Kasiviswanathan and Smith (2008); Yang et al. (2015), amounting to the conditions that, given a prior on the data, the posterior distribution given the released statistic does not change the distribution of any data point too much. However, this does not cover the case when an adversary has additional (specific) information, and there is a mismatch between the specificity given by the prior distribution and the one-size-fits-all style DP analysis. Bayesian assessments of statistical disclosure have been given in several works. In Fienberg et al. (1997), adversarial attempts to infer additional information about in-dividuals from a randomised data release are assessed via simulations. In Hu (2018), methods of reconstructing real data from synthetic data are discussed, and in Reiter et al. (2014), posterior odds of an adversary matching datasets are considered, albeit with a worst-case analysis. However, whilst the Bayesian frameworks of these works are common to our own, none of these works feature truly systematic ways of measuring privacy and utility. Additional works have considered other notions of Bayesian privacy. In Eilat et al. (2021), mechanisms with a restriction on the KL divergence between the prior and posterior were considered. Furthermore, several works have considered information in an adversarial or minimax sense. The concept of “informational size” related to the extent agents’ beliefs are modulated by information releases (see McLean and Postlewaite (2001) and related works), and “rational inattention” (Sims, 2003) considers the problem of constructing a random variable Y that yields information about another variable X

subject to an information flow restraint. Another vast area of research that loosely relates to data release under privacy is what is known as federated learning (Kairouz and McMahan, 2021), where data in separate silos is processed in such a way as to remain separate. This area is too large to be effectively surveyed here, though it is worth noting that extensive connections have been made with Bayesian analysis (Cao et al., 2023) and DP (Wei et al., 2020). Federated learning has proved extremely effective in practical settings, such as with healthcare data (Sheller et al., 2020). In future work, we would be interested in studying the privacy of federated methods using our framework. Finally, a recent preprint by Bon et al. (2026) develops a related Bayesian decision-theoretic notion of privacy. Here, one considers a mechanism private if, for every choice 6 Bayesian Adversarial Privacy 

in a family of priors, for every x in the data-space, and for every choice of a scoring rule within some family, the probability that there is a high difference in optimal inference between prior and posterior (with respect to the information release q(·| x)) is low. This alternative perspective therefore adopts a more minimax variant of privacy, if somewhat in line with our framework, with the additional complexity that a worst-case is taken over a family of priors and loss functions. However, we note that the authors’ framework recovers the regular (ϵ, δ ) differential privacy and that several structural features of DP carry over to the wider notion, including post-processing and composition rules.

## 2.3 Comparison with Differential Privacy and Statistical Disclosure Control 

We briefly contrast Bayesian adversarial privacy (BAP) with differential privacy (DP) and statistical disclosure control (SDC). Relative to DP, the BAP framework is explicit about both objectives: what statistical goal is pursued and what aspect of the data is to be protected. The loss functions LB

and LE encode these objectives directly, introducing contextual information into the definition of privacy. Moreover, risk is evaluated by averaging over the prior distribution on (θ, x ), rather than assigning equal weight to arbitrarily unlikely datasets, as in the worst-case formulation of DP. Bell, Johnston, Luciano and Robert 13 The framework is also practical and transparent. Since mechanisms are evaluated through explicitly specified priors and loss functions, different designers can tailor the analysis to reflect their normative or practical priorities. In contrast, DP guarantees are often assessed post hoc, primarily to certify that a given mechanism satisfies a formal privacy definition, rather than to optimise a context-specific trade-off between utility and disclosure risk. Regarding SDC, key limitations arise from inconsistencies in the information avail-able to different parties and from ad hoc disclosure-risk measures. By embedding the problem in a fully Bayesian decision-theoretic setup, BAP ensures coherence: all parties reason from explicitly defined probabilistic models, and privacy and utility are assessed through well-defined risks. For scenarios outside of an explicitly Bayesian setting, such as releasing census data, one might think that SDC is better equipped to analyse mechanisms. To illustrate the tension between statistical utility and privacy, and the importance of evaluating mechanisms ex ante, we consider a simple toy example. Consider a medical study with a binary outcome and a rare sensitive condition, so that inference about treatment effects may conflict with protecting individual-level information. To model the tension between inference and privacy, we consider two coins. ## 4.3 Notable Differences 

The two Gaussian examples reveal structural differences in the privacy–inference rela-tionship. When Bob and Eve are focused on closely related features of the data, as in the case where Eve targets ¯X, privacy and inference become intrinsically coupled. Since ¯X is sufficient for θ, improving Bob’s posterior necessarily reduces Eve’s uncertainty. In this regime, all mechanisms exhibit a genuine trade-off: reductions in RB are accompanied by reductions in RE . The optimal tuning parameters balance these opposing effects, and no mechanism can substantially improve inference without incurring a privacy cost. By contrast, when Eve targets the maximum of the sample, Bob’s objective and Eve’s objective depend on structurally distinct aspects of the data. In this case, compressing the dataset to the sufficient statistic ¯X already eliminates much of the information relevant to Eve while preserving all inferential content for Bob. As a result, Alice can construct releases that strictly improve Bob’s inference without reducing Eve’s risk, demonstrating that privacy and inference need not always be antagonistic. These examples also emphasise the role of sufficient statistics in privacy design. If the statistic to be protected is not sufficient for the target of the inference, releasing only sufficient statistics may yield substantial privacy gains at no inferential cost. However, when privacy and inference target the same sufficient statistic, more delicate mechanisms are required. Finally, in both settings, the one-bit release emerges as optimal within the families considered. When Eve targets the mean, this mechanism extracts maximal inferential content about θ, though its performance is sensitive to the choice of threshold τ . When Bell, Johnston, Luciano and Robert 27 Eve targets the tails, the optimal one-bit release coincides with the mechanism that reveals only Bob’s Bayes-optimal decision under full disclosure, previously discussed at the end of Section 2.2. In this regime, Bob will make the same decision as if he had observed the full data, while almost all ancillary information about the dataset is concealed from Eve. Taken together, these examples illustrate a central message of the framework: the feasibility of privacy-preserving inference depends critically on whether the adversary’s objective is aligned with, or orthogonal to, the statistician’s goals. Consequently, the mechanism must be chosen accordingly, as no single release rule could be uniformly optimal across every adversarial objective. 

# 5 Conclusion 

As discussed in our introduction, the most commonly used formalisation of privacy, differential privacy, is widely adopted and enforced for its mathematically elegant rep-resentation and its ability to validate some randomisation mechanisms, though it can be seen as disconnected from a genuine and realistic protection of the privacy of data owners, and not directly aligned with the objectives of inference. It also fails to de-liver a spelt-out, crystal-clear confidentiality guarantee to such owners. As illustrated in Dimitrakakis et al. (2017), the assumptions DP imposes on the likelihood function and on the underlying MCMC sequence generally impede the statistical aspects. It fur-thermore fails to account for the inferential aspects of the procedure, and in particular, it vividly lacks both informational and decision-theoretic Bayesian features. By looking at worst-case scenarios and imposing randomisation and insufficiency, DP’s guarantees need not coincide with Bayesian optimality criteria. The same holds for SDC, which is practically verified but also formulated independently of a Bayesian model. In this paper, we have introduced a privacy framework fully embedded within a Bayesian decision-theoretic perspective. Privacy requirements are formalised through a first loss function, which quantifies breaches according to what is to be protected, at what cost, and for what purposes. By distinguishing privacy from inferential objectives, the framework explicitly captures the inherent trade-off between disclosure protection and statistical utility. The inferential objective is encoded through a second loss function associated with a statistical model, or more generally with a substitute in the setting of generalised Bayesian inference (Dempster, 1968; Bissiri et al., 2016; Matsubara et al., 2022), in-cluding score-based methods, variational approximations, or approximate MCMC pro-cedures. Combining both components into a single decision criterion from the perspec-tive of the data handler yields a coherent Bayesian formulation of the privacy–utility trade-off. For simplicity, we adopt a linear aggregation of the two losses, where the coefficient 

λ in Equation (8) plays the role of a Lagrange multiplier in a constrained optimisation interpretation. This formulation allows us to evaluate the informational content and privacy leakage of summary statistics (as in ABC, (Marin et al., 2011)), as well as of 28 Bayesian Adversarial Privacy 

perturbed or synthetic data generated from the posterior predictive distribution. It also provides simulation-based tools for quantifying ex ante privacy risk under a given release mechanism. While the present work establishes a decision-theoretic framework for Bayesian pri-vacy, several directions remain open for further investigation. A first issue concerns the specification of Eve’s prior distribution on x and her loss function, whose calibration is central to the resulting privacy assessment. Exploring alternative forms of Alice’s combined loss function, in particular those incorporating stronger penalties for severe disclosure events, is another important direction. Connections with safe Bayesian procedures (Grünwald et al., 2024) may provide use-ful tools for addressing robustness and model misspecification, as well as for analysing the impact of information asymmetries between agents. From a computational perspec-tive, more efficient methods for comparing release mechanisms are needed in order to scale the framework to complex models and to design novel privacy-preserving proce-dures. Another direction of interest arises from the computational requirements of finding and evaluating optimal decisions over Bayesian posteriors conditioned on insufficient statistics. Since both Bob and Eve’s posteriors require distributions conditionally on η,these will not generally be available in closed form. It is therefore vital for Alice to be able to accurately calculate and assess their Bayes rules so that she can correctly calculate the risk of a mechanism. This likely will require the development of problem-specific Monte Carlo methods. In analogy with posterior predictive model assessment (Gelman et al., 2013), the proposed Bayesian privacy construct could also be used to provide ex ante privacy evaluations for stakeholders under specified modelling assumptions. Finally, extending the framework to realistic large-scale settings remains a substantial challenge, including both large datasets and high-dimensional contexts in which multiple variables jointly increase the risk of disclosure through record linkage (Domingo-Ferrer et al., 2015).
