Abstract 

Federated Learning (FL) enables collaborative training of medical AI models across hospi-tals without centralizing patient data. However, the exchange of model updates exposes criti-cal vulnerabilities: gradient inversion attacks can reconstruct patient information, Byzantine clients can poison the global model, and the Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL) threat renders today’s encrypted traffic vulnerable to future quantum adversaries. We introduce ZKFL-PQ (Zero-Knowledge Federated Learning, Post-Quantum ), a three-tiered cryptographic protocol that hybridizes (i) ML-KEM (FIPS 203) for quantum-resistant key encapsulation, (ii) lattice-based Zero-Knowledge Proofs for verifiable norm-constrained 

gradient integrity, and (iii) BFV homomorphic encryption for privacy-preserving aggrega-tion. We formalize the security model and prove correctness and zero-knowledge properties under the Module-LWE, Ring-LWE, and SIS assumptions in the classical random oracle model . We evaluate ZKFL-PQ on synthetic medical imaging data across 5 federated clients over 10 training rounds. Our protocol achieves 100% rejection of norm-violating up-dates while maintaining model accuracy at 100%, compared to a catastrophic drop to 23% under standard FL. The computational overhead (factor ∼20 ×) is analyzed and shown to be compatible with clinical research workflows operating on daily or weekly training cycles. We emphasize that the current defense guarantees rejection of large-norm malicious updates; robustness against subtle low-norm or directional poisoning remains future work. 

Keywords: Post-Quantum Cryptography, Federated Learning, Zero-Knowledge Proofs, Ho-momorphic Encryption, Medical AI, ML-KEM, Lattice-Based Cryptography 

## 1 Introduction 

The digitization of healthcare generates enormous volumes of sensitive data—medical imaging, genomic sequences, electronic health records—which are ideal substrates for training powerful AI models. Privacy regulations (GDPR, HIPAA) and ethical constraints, however, prevent centralized data aggregation across institutions. Federated Learning (FL) [1] offers an elegant solution: clients (hospitals) train models locally and share only parameter updates (gradients) with a central aggregation server. Despite its privacy-preserving premise, FL suffers from three fundamental vulnerabilities. First, gradient inversion attacks [2] can reconstruct raw patient data from shared model updates with alarming fidelity. Second, Byzantine attacks allow malicious or compromised clients to poison the global model by submitting adversarial gradients [3]. Third, and most critically for long-lived medical data, the Harvest Now, Decrypt Later (HNDL) strategy [4] enables a

Medical data requires confidentiality guarantees spanning patient lifetimes. An MRI scan encrypted with RSA-2048 today may be decryptable within 15–20 years [5], well within the period during which the data must remain protected. This creates an urgent need for quantum-resilient cryptographic protocols for FL.

We propose ZKFL-PQ , a three-tiered cryptographic framework for federated learning that addresses all three vulnerabilities simultaneously: (i) Quantum-resistant channels via ML-KEM (FIPS 203) [6], ensuring that key exchange and data encapsulation resist both classical and quantum attacks. (ii) Verifiable gradient integrity via lattice-based Zero-Knowledge Proofs, enabling each client to prove that its update satisfies predefined constraints (e.g., bounded ℓ2-norm) without revealing the update itself. (iii) Privacy-preserving aggregation via BFV homomorphic encryption [7], allowing the server to compute the average of encrypted gradients without accessing individual contri-butions. We formalize the protocol, prove its security properties under standard lattice hardness as-sumptions, and validate it experimentally on synthetic medical imaging data with 5 federated clients over 10 rounds, including a Byzantine adversary that is detected with 100% accuracy.

## 3 Threat Model 

We consider a federated learning system with N clients (hospitals) and one aggregation server. The adversary A has the following capabilities: 

T1 Passive eavesdropping: A can observe all network traffic between clients and the server. 

T2 Byzantine clients: A can corrupt up to f < N/ 2 clients, which may submit arbitrary (malicious) model updates. 

T3 HNDL adversary: A records all encrypted traffic and possesses a future quantum com-puter capable of solving the factoring and discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time. 

T4 Honest-but-curious server: The server follows the protocol but attempts to learn in-formation from client updates.

Properties (3) and (4) follow from Theorem 4.3. The protocol is constructed as a layered composition. Each layer operates on independent randomness: 

• ML-KEM uses fresh randomness per encapsulation. 

• BFV uses fresh error polynomials per encryption. 

• The ZKP uses fresh masking vectors per proof. No information leaks between layers beyond the intended outputs. Under correct parameteriza-tion and assuming no side-channel leakage, the overall security reduces to the conjunction of the underlying hardness assumptions. We note that this argument is modular; a full UC-style composition proof is outside the scope of this work.

## 5 Experimental Evaluation 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate ZKFL-PQ on a synthetic medical imaging classification task simulating four diag-nostic categories (normal, benign lesion, malignant type A, malignant type B). The experimental parameters are: 

• Data: 1,000 synthetic samples with 784 features (mimicking 28 × 28 flattened imaging biomarkers), generated from class-conditional Gaussian mixtures with spatial correlations. We intentionally use an easily separable task to isolate the effect of Byzantine attacks from model capacity limitations. 

• Partitioning: 5 clients with non-IID distribution via Dirichlet allocation ( α = 0 .5). 

• Model: Two-hidden-layer MLP ( 784 → 128 → 64 → 4) with 108,996 parameters. 

• FL parameters: 10 rounds, 3 local epochs per round, SGD with η = 0 .01 , batch size 32. 

• Byzantine scenario: Client 3 turns malicious at round 4, injecting random gradients scaled by factor 50. 

• Cryptographic parameters: ML-KEM-768 ( n=256 , k =3 , q =3329 ), BFV ( n=512 , q =2 32 −5, t =2 16 ), ZKP norm threshold τ = 5 .0.We compare three configurations: (a) Standard FL with simulated TLS, (b) FL + ML-KEM 

(post-quantum transport only), and (c) ZKFL-PQ (full hybrid protocol). All experiments were run on a single machine (Intel Xeon, 16GB RAM) using a pure Python/NumPy implementation.

## 6 Related Work 

Post-Quantum Federated Learning. Patel [12] explored lattice-based HE for medical imag-ing FL but did not address Byzantine resilience or integrate ZKPs. Lu et al. [13] proposed hybrid PQ encryption for FL but without formal security proofs or homomorphic aggregation. 

Zero-Knowledge Proofs in ML. zkML [14] applied ZKPs to verify ML inference but not federated training. Ghodsi et al. [15] used commitment schemes for FL integrity but lacked post-quantum security. 

Byzantine-Resilient FL. Blanchard et al. [3] introduced Krum for Byzantine-robust aggre-gation. Krum achieves ∼80–90% accuracy under similar attack scenarios but provides no crypto-graphic guarantee —it uses statistical distance heuristics that can be defeated by carefully crafted adversarial gradients. Our ZKP-based approach provides provable rejection of norm-violating updates with soundness under SIS.

## 7 Discussion 

Clinical Applicability. The ∼20 × overhead is acceptable for medical FL scenarios where training occurs daily, weekly, or on-demand rather than in real-time. A training pipeline that takes 1 minute under standard FL would require ∼20 minutes under ZKFL-PQ—well within a nightly batch window. 

Scalability. The current bottleneck is the schoolbook polynomial multiplication ( O(n2)) used in ML-KEM and BFV. NTT-based implementations achieve O(n log n), which would reduce ML-KEM overhead by an order of magnitude. GPU-accelerated HE libraries (e.g., OpenFHE, SEAL) would further improve the HE encryption phase. 

Threshold Calibration. The norm threshold τ must be carefully calibrated: too low rejects legitimate updates from clients with unusual data distributions; too high allows subtle poisoning. Limitations. 

• Synthetic data only: Our evaluation uses synthetic medical imaging data. While this suffices for a proof of concept, validation on real multi-centric datasets (e.g., MRI, CT scans) is essential before clinical deployment. 

• Partial HE coverage: To keep computational costs manageable, we encrypted only 512 out of 108,996 parameters. A full-parameter HE implementation would increase commu-nication by ∼100 ×, necessitating optimized libraries. 

• Limited adversarial coverage: The current ZKP enforces only an ℓ2-norm bound. While this guarantees rejection of large-magnitude malicious updates, it does not prevent subtle low-norm, directional, or backdoor-style poisoning attacks. 

• Multiple cryptographic assumptions: The security of ZKFL-PQ relies on three dis-tinct hardness assumptions (MLWE, RLWE, SIS). While each is well-studied, their com-bination broadens the attack surface. 

• Trusted decryptor: The current protocol assumes a trusted party holds the BFV se-cret key. Integrating distributed threshold decryption would eliminate this single point of failure. 

• ROM vs. QROM: As noted in §4.3, the Fiat–Shamir transform is analyzed in the classical ROM; a full QROM treatment remains future work. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We have presented ZKFL-PQ, a three-tiered cryptographic protocol for federated learning that provides quantum-resistant communication (ML-KEM), verifiable gradient integrity (lattice-based ZKPs), and privacy-preserving aggregation (BFV homomorphic encryption). Experimen-tal evaluation demonstrates that ZKFL-PQ achieves 100% Byzantine detection while preserving model accuracy, at a computational overhead of ∼20 × that is compatible with clinical research workflows. Our security analysis is conducted under standard lattice assumptions (MLWE, RLWE, SIS) in the classical random oracle model . The underlying hardness assumptions are conjectured to be post-quantum secure; the Fiat–Shamir transform inherits standard ROM caveats, and a full QROM analysis remains future work. Future work will focus on: (1) NTT-accelerated implementations for order-of-magnitude speedups; (2) extending the ZKP to richer constraint languages (e.g., per-layer norm bounds, directional consistency); (3) integrating distributed threshold decryption to eliminate the trusted decryptor assumption; (4) validation on real-world multi-centric medical imaging datasets; and (5) QROM-secure Fiat–Shamir instantiation.
