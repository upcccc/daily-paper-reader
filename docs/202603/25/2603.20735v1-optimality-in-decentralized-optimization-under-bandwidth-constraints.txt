## Abstract 

We consider a realistic decentralized setup with bandwidth-constrained communi-cation and derive optimal time complexities for non-convex stochastic parallel and asynchronous optimization (up to logarithmic factors). We develop the correspond-ing methods, Grace SGD and Leon SGD, for both homogeneous and heterogeneous settings. Unlike previous work, our optimal bounds are characterized in terms of 

min-cut/max-flow quantities and rely on tools from GomoryвҖ“Hu trees and Steiner Tree Packing problems, providing tighter and more practical complexities.

Communication times. Our main goal is to derive the optimal time complexity in the setup where communication times cannot be ignored. To the best of our knowledge, this work considers a new communication assumption motivated by practical bottlenecks. For example, consider two identical GPUs with the same computation time h, connected by a communication link. Graph-Bandwidth Communication Model Motivated by the previous example, in general, we assume that there exists a directed connected weighted graph G = ( V, E, b ) with n = |V | vertices and |E| edges. Each vertex

## 3 Lower Bound in the Homogeneous Setting 

In this section, we show that Grace SGD and the result in Theorem 1.5 are optimal up to logarithmic factors within a large family of optimization methods. The reader will see that we also use Algorithm 2 to state the lower bound, which is not a coincidence. The вҖңcommunication sideвҖқ of the lower bound proof is constructive and enables the design of Grace SGD. Obtaining lower bounds in the homogeneous setting is significantly more technically challenging, since the standard trick of placing different blocks of a hard function on distant nodes cannot be used. We consider the standard class of zero-respecting algorithms (Nesterov, 2018; Arjevani et al., 2022), which includes SGD-like methods, Adam, gossip methods (e.g., (Nedic et al., 2017)), and even compressed decentralized methods (e.g., Choco-SGD (Koloskova et al., 2019), BEER (Zhao et al., 2022)). The optimization protocol is flexible and presented in Protocol 5. Every worker runs two parallel loops: i) in the first computation loop, worker i computes stochastic gradients locally, adds these vectors to local information Ii, and the algorithm is allowed to generate the next query point using Ii; ii) in the second communication loop, worker i, using Ii, prepares a set of coordinates, which can be random and not necessarily of size d, and the algorithm sends them using any routing strategy that obeys Assumption 1.4. This way, the protocol allows the use of local steps, minibatching, gossip communication, and compressed communication. The formal description of the allowed ways to prepare coordinates is presented in Assumption G.1, where we allow algorithms to use mappings that do not take into account local information and do not depend on {Ii}iвҲҲ[n] when choosing the indices in the sparsifiers. Assumption G.1 covers sending the full vector, sending a predefined block of the vector as is done in AllReduce algorithms, or even a random subset of coordinates, supporting Rand K or Perm K compressors (Szlendak et al., 2021).

## 7 Related Work 

Classical theory. Starting with one of the seminal works (Nemirovskij and Yudin, 1983), the optimization field began investigating the optimality of methods. Initially, the focus was mainly on the oracle complexity, e.g., the number of function and gradient evaluations needed to achieve a given accuracy. One of the first results was obtained in the convex setting where it was shown that the optimal complexity for finding an Оө-approximate solution is Оҳ( pLR 2/Оө ) (Nesterov, 1983, 2018), where R is the distance between the initial point and an optimal point. For the nonconvex setting, the optimal complexity Оҳ( LвҲҶ/Оө) was shown by Carmon et al. (2020) in the deterministic setting and 

Оҳ( LвҲҶ/Оө + Пғ2LвҲҶ/Оө2) by Arjevani et al. (2022) in the stochastic setting. 

Gossip protocol. One way to extend the classical results to the decentralized setting is to use the gossip protocol. As in Graph-Bandwidth Communication Model, workers are associated with nodes and communication links with edges of the graph. Then, a mixing matrix W вҲҲ RnГ—n is constructed such that wij > 0 if nodes i and j are connected, and wij = 0 otherwise. The upper and lower bounds are then constructed in terms of the number of communication rounds required to find an Оө-stationary point (Boyd et al., 2006; Scaman et al., 2017; Koloskova et al., 2019; Lu and De Sa, 2021). However, this approach arguably has the following drawbacks: (i) this protocol allows methods to communicate only with neighbors and to use a single operation yi вҶҗ P 

> j

wij xj , where xj is the sent vector and yi

is the received vector. Such a protocol restricts many practical ways to disseminate information in the graph. For instance, the split vector and online in-network aggregation strategies, discussed in Section 1 and commonly used in practice, are ignored; (ii) the obtained complexities are typically defined in terms of iteration complexity and depend on the spectral gap of a mixing matrix, whereas our approach uses time complexities with explicit dependence on computation and communication time parameters {hi} and {bij }; (iii) the obtained results use вҖңone-zeroвҖқ encoding, assuming that all communication links are the same, whereas our approach uses bandwidths, a practical way of defining connectivity between two workers or switches. 

Time complexities. The classical oracle complexity is a valid and intuitive metric for comparing meth-ods with one worker/GPU/CPU/server. However, modern optimization requires hundreds, thousands, or even millions of workers, requiring the analysis of parallel and asynchronous algorithms , where a conceptually different metric is needed. Let us consider a recent paper by Mishchenko et al. (2022), where the authors compare Synchronous SGD and Asynchronous SGD. The oracle complexity of both methods is the same; moreover, the iteration complexity of Synchronous SGD is better. However, under the time complexity , a more appropriate metric for parallel methods, Asynchronous SGD is provably better. In particular, assuming the Computation Model and using the analysis by Cohen et al. (2021); Koloskova et al. (2022); Mishchenko et al. (2022), it is possible to show that Asynchronous SGD has provably better time complexity than Synchronous SGD. In this section, we provide more details on how to implement Synchronous SGD and obtain (2), as discussed in Section 1.1. One way to do it is to fix any pivot worker (server) v that aggregates the stochastic gradients. Then, consider any other worker i. Choose any path p between workers i and v in 

G. Worker i sends вҲҮf (xk; Оҫki ) along this path p, which takes at most d/min (i,j )вҲҲp bij вүӨ d/min (i,j )вҲҲE bij 

seconds. There is an important caveat: all workers send their vectors to worker v in parallel, potentially occupying the same edges and causing congestion (e.g., if two workers send their vectors through the same edge, it leads to a 2Г— slowdown because the edge would process 2 Г— d coordinates). One known way to fix this is to perform online in-network aggregation : each worker streams its vector coordinate-by-coordinate, while intermediate nodes wait for the first coordinate from their children, aggregate these values, and immediately forward the result further. Thus, every edge carries only a single aggregated stream instead of multiple separate vectors, avoiding additional congestion factors, and at most d coordinates pass through each edge. 51 J Practical Guidelines and Numerical Experiments 

In this section, we discuss practical guidelines and conduct numerical experiments of the new algorithm, Grace SGD. As we explain in Section 2, Grace SGD is simple. It is just a stochastic gradient method with preprocessing (Algorithm 2) and the optimal-bandwidth AllReduce (Algorithm 3). Implementing Algorithm 2 is straightforward. Finding a Gomory-Hu tree is a standard graph problem, and there are many open-source libraries with implementations (for instance, use gomory_hu_tree 

from NetworkX (Hagberg et al., 2007)). Besides that, Algorithm 2 is a standard one loop function where we find all steps can be implemented in Python. At the same time, in general, implementing the optimal-bandwidth AllReduce algorithm described in Algorithm 3 is arguably trickier. The first non-trivial step is to solve the Steiner Tree Packing 

problem. While it is a classical problem in computer science, Lau (2004) only recently proposed a polynomial-time algorithm that finds at least вҢҠОұ ВҜG(S)/26 вҢӢ edge-disjoint trees that contain every node in S вҠҶ V, where Оұ ВҜG(S) is the minimum value of an S-cut in ВҜG (Definition E.5 and Theorem F.1). Once the Steiner Tree Packing is solved (up to a constant factor), the reduce and broadcast steps can be implemented in practice using the standard communication libraries. The description of the polynomial-time algorithm in Lau (2004) is somewhat non-trivial. Fortunately, for standard graph structures, implementing an optimal-bandwidth AllReduce is more straightforward. In particular, consider the 2-Torus G from Figure 7. Without loss of generality, assume that b = 1 and 

n = (2 k + 1) 2 for some k вүҘ 1. We fix the node with coordinate (k, k ) and refer to it as the pivot node (see Figure 9). In this graph, every node has 4 outgoing edges. The idea of this optimal-bandwidth AllReduce is that every node i splits its local vector into four blocks (ai, 1, a i, 2, a i, 3, a i, 4) and sends them in different directions. The goal is now to choose the directions in such a way that each block eventually reaches the pivot worker without cycles, while intermediate nodes use online in-network aggregation to combine blocks with the same index and avoid congestion. Formally, for all (i, j ) with iМё = k and jМё = k, the node sends the first block to (i + 1 mod (2 k + 1) , j ),the second block to (i, j + 1 mod (2 k + 1)) , the third block to (i вҲ’ 1 mod (2 k + 1) , j ), and the fourth block to (i, j вҲ’ 1 mod (2 k + 1)) . For nodes with i = k or j = k, the node sends the first block to (i, j + 1 mod (2 k + 1)) , the second block to (i + 1 mod (2 k + 1) , j ), the third block to 

(i, j вҲ’ 1 mod (2 k + 1)) , and the fourth block to (i вҲ’ 1 mod (2 k + 1) , j ).See visualization for k = 2 in Figure 9. For instance, node (3 , 0) sends the first block to (4 , 0) , which aggregates its own block with it, and sends the sum to (0 , 0) , which also adds its first block. And it happens until the total sum arrives to (2 , 2) . Notice that (2 , 1) aggregates the sum from nodes (2 , 0) 

and (1 , 1) .

Compared to the naive strategy, where every worker sends the full vectors to the pivot worker, using this routing algorithm and online in-network aggregation, we can speed up the reduce operation by 

4Г—, since each block arrives at the central node via different and independent paths. The improvement by 4Г— is expected since the value of a min-cut in the graph is 4. The broadcast can be implemented similarly by reversing the edges. We could have obtained a 2вҖ“4Г— speedup using Algorithm 3, but the strategy specialized for the 2-Torus is arguably simpler. For the ring graph, the algorithm is similar and one get a 2Г— communication speed up. In general, for a p-Torus, the speedup should be Оҳ( p) (at least with Algorithm 3 due to Theorem F.1).
