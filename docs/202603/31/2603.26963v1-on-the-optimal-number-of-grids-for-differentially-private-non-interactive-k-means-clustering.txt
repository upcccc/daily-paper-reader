# On the Optimal Number of Grids for Differentially Private Non-Interactive K-Means Clustering 

Gokularam Muthukrishnan *, Anshoo Tandon *

Abstract —Differentially private K-means clustering enables releasing cluster centers derived from a dataset while protecting the privacy of the individuals. Non-interactive clustering tech-niques based on privatized histograms are attractive because the released data synopsis can be reused for other downstream tasks without additional privacy loss. The choice of the number of grids for discretizing the data points is crucial, as it directly controls the quantization bias and the amount of noise injected to preserve privacy. The widely adopted strategy selects a grid size that is independent of the number of clusters and also relies on empirical tuning. In this work, we revisit this choice and propose a refined grid-size selection rule derived by minimizing an upper bound on the expected deviation in the K-means objective function, leading to a more principled discretization strategy for non-interactive private clustering. Compared to prior work, our grid resolution differs both in its dependence on the number of clusters and in the scaling with dataset size and privacy budget. Extensive numerical results elucidate that the proposed strategy results in accurate clustering compared to the state-of-the-art techniques, even under tight privacy budgets. 

Index Terms —Differential privacy, K-means clustering, Non-interactive data analysis, Grid-size selection, Laplace mechanism. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# DATA clustering [1] is a fundamental unsupervised ma-chine learning technique that, when applied to datasets with attributes from several users, identifies meaningful user groups and reveals the latent structures within the data. The 

K-means clustering is a widely adopted clustering method for both low and high-dimensional data (e.g., [2]). However, the user attributes often contain sensitive information, making it is critical to ensure that no individual’s data can be distinguished or re-identified from the clustering. Differential privacy (DP) [3] is a strong and widely adopted framework for preserving user privacy [4], and over the years, several differentially private clustering algorithms have been developed. Ghazi et al. [5] provided the approximation guarantee for DP clustering. Private K-means clustering has been studied under the distance-based privacy notion in [6] and under the estimation error-based notion in [7]. Authors of [8] studied private hierarchical clustering of graphs, where privacy of the vertex interactions is preserved. A genetic algorithm-based DP 

K-means clustering scheme has been proposed in [9] and a private variant of the K-means++ algorithm has been proposed in [10]. The sample-and-aggregate framework has been used for private clustering in [11] and [12], though their approach

relied on strong assumptions on the input data. In [13] and [14], DP K-means algorithms based on maximum coverage were proposed, showing guarantees comparable to [15], but the results quite far from those of the non-private Lloyd’s algorithm. Recently, scalable implementations of private K-means clustering for very large datasets have been studied in [16] and [17]. In [18], cluster-wise description of the dataset is obtained in a differentially private manner. Broadly speaking, private clustering algorithms can be classified into two categories, namely, interactive and non-interactive . Interactive algorithms deplete the entire privacy budget for the clustering task, and the data cannot be reused for any other purpose without further degradation of privacy guar-antee. In contrast, non-interactive algorithms (also known as synopsis-based approaches) first generate a private summary of the data distribution, which can be reused for multiple queries or downstream tasks without additional privacy leakage. 

DPLloyd [19], [20], the private version of Lloyd algorithm for K-means clustering, is the prominent example of interac-tive schemes. It requires careful distribution of the available privacy budget across iterations: too few iterations prevent convergence, while too many increase the amount of noise injected to preserve privacy. IBM’s diffprivlib [21] im-plements DPLloyd with a budget allocation strategy proposed in [22], and decreasing exponential budget distribution is used in [23]. Another private version of Lloyd algorithm has been proposed in [24], where the centroids are resampled in each iteration through exponential mechanism and the convergence is guaranteed. Chang et al. [25] proposed a non-interactive private cluster-ing scheme based on private coreset construction [26], [27] for the local model of DP, where the dataset is privately compressed into a smaller, weighted set of points through recursive locality-sensitive hashing [28] and a classical, non-private clustering algorithm is executed on them; the frame-work is later extended to the central model in [29]. This method outperforms [15] and scales well for massive datasets, but requires several hyperparameters to be set a priori , neces-sitating extensive parameter tuning to achieve best results. Uniform grid non-interactive methods partition the data domain into equal-sized grids, providing a simple and efficient structure for releasing private histograms [22], [30]. One of the most prominent works in this category is the Extended Uni-form Grid K-Means (EUGkM) algorithm [22], which has been shown to outperform interactive DP clustering algorithms in various settings, particularly in lower dimensions. However, its construct is not directly motivated by the clustering objective, rather adapted from [30], which focused on range queries; con-

We briefly recapitulate several key concepts, in part to establish notations and conventions that will be followed in this article, and motivate our proposed grid selection rule. 

A. Differential Privacy 

Let D ∈ X N denote the dataset that contains data records 

xl ∈ X , l ∈ NN , collected from N individuals. We term a pair of datasets (D, qD) that differ by only a single data record as neighboring (or adjacent) datasets, and we denote D⟩⟨  qD; in particular, we consider add-remove model [33], [34], where qD

can be constructed by adding or removing a single data record from D, i.e., D \ qD + qD \ D = 1 .A trusted central curator holds the sensitive dataset D and is responsible for securely releasing the result of the query 

f : X N → Y on dataset D. DP guarantee ensures that it is difficult to infer any individual’s presence in D from the response by essentially randomizing it; the algorithm M that provides randomized output to the query referred to as the 

private mechanism .

Definition 1 (Differential Privacy [35]) . A randomized mecha-nism M : X N → Y is said to guarantee ϵ-differential privacy 1

(ϵ-DP in short) if for every measurable set E ⊆ Y and every pair of neighboring datasets D⟩⟨  qD,

P{M (D) ∈ E} ≤ eϵ P{M ( qD) ∈ E} , (1) where ϵ ∈ R+ is the privacy budget .

> (ϵ, δ )-DP (or approximate DP) [36], allows the indistinguishability bound to hold up to an additive slack δ∈[0 ,1] .

The definition imposes an information-theoretic bound on an adversary’s power to discern whether the input dataset is 

D or qD. The parameter ϵ quantifies the amount of information that may be revealed about any individual’s presence; smaller values of ϵ correspond to stronger privacy guarantees. When the query output is a numeric vector, the straightfor-ward way to randomize it is by adding noise. In the additive noise mechanism , DP is achieved by perturbing the query result f (D) ∈ RM as M(D) = f (D) + Z, where Z ∈ RM is the noise that is sampled from a known distribution. Numerous additive noise mechanisms have been proposed and studied in the literature [35]–[43]. The Laplace mechanism [35] achieves DP by adding noise sampled from a Laplace distribution, with appropriately calibrated scale, to each coordinate of the response. Because of the exponential tails of the noise distribution, the Laplace mechanism can ensure ϵ-DP with 

bounded privacy loss [44].

IV. S IMULATION RESULTS 

We evaluate the performance of our proposed scheme, RUG-NIK, against several state-of-the-art DP K-means clustering methods, namely, EUGkM [22], DPLloyd implementation in IBM’s diffprivlib [21], and Google’s coreset-based clus-tering (GCC) [29]. All methods are tested on synthetic datasets generated as described in [22] for a variety of configurations. Table II summarizes the average WCSS achieved by differ-ent techniques across these settings for a wide range of privacy budgets ϵ. Lower WCSS indicates better clustering quality. The results show that our proposed scheme consistently out-performs all baseline schemes across all considered settings. Furthermore, RUGNIK offers a significant improvement over EUGkM that becomes more pronounced in regimes with stronger privacy constraints (i.e., smaller ϵ) and larger numbers of clusters, where the choice of grid resolution plays a more significant role. Table III compares the number of grids M selected by EUGkM and our proposed scheme. It is evident that EUGkM, due to its independence from the number of clusters K,results in degenerate choices in high-privacy regimes, where the number of grids is smaller than the number of clusters (M < K , marked with ⋆), thereby rendering the clustering process illogical as the grid size is too coarse to distinguish the required number of clusters. In contrast, RUGNIK appropri-ately scales the grid resolution by explicitly accounting for K,ensuring high clustering accuracy even under strong privacy constraints. V. C ONCLUSIONS 

In this work, we studied the problem of non-interactive differentially private K-means clustering, and we derived the optimal grid resolution for uniform gridding. Our selection rule is theoretically grounded and explicitly incorporates the number of clusters in the grid size. The efficacy and robustness of our refined grid resolution are validated using extensive empirical analysis under a wide range of settings, and we demonstrated superior performance over the existing state-of-the-art and benchmark open-source implementations such as 

diffprivlib [21], and Google’s coreset-based clustering [29], with significant improvements in high privacy regime.
