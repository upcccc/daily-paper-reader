# Privacy as Commodity: MFG-RegretNet for Large-Scale Privacy Trading in Federated Learning 

Kangkang Sun, Jianhua Li, Senior Member, IEEE, Xiuzhen Chen, Member, IEEE, 

Weizhi Meng, Senior Member, IEEE, Minyi Guo, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promi-nent paradigm for privacy-preserving distributed machine learn-ing, yet two fundamental challenges hinder its large-scale adop-tion. First, gradient inversion attacks can reconstruct sensitive training data from uploaded model updates, so privacy risk persists even when raw data remain local. Second, without adequate monetary compensation, rational clients have little in-centive to contribute high-quality gradients, limiting participation at scale. To address these challenges, a privacy trading market is developed in which clients sell their differential privacy budgets as a commodity and receive explicit economic compensation for privacy sacrifice. This market is formalized as a Privacy Auction Game (PAG), and the existence of a Bayesian Nash Equilibrium is established under dominant-strategy incentive compatibility (DSIC), individual rationality (IR), and budget feasibility. To overcome the NP-hard, high-dimensional Nash Equilibrium computation at scale, MFG-RegretNet is introduced as a deep-learning-based auction mechanism that combines mean-field game (MFG) approximation with differentiable mech-anism design. The MFG reduction lowers per-round computa-tional complexity from O(N 2 log N ) to O(N ) while incurring only an O(N −1/2) equilibrium approximation gap. Extensive experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10 demonstrate that MFG-RegretNet outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in incentive compatibility, auction revenue, and social welfare, while main-taining competitive downstream FL model accuracy. 

Index Terms —Federated Learning, Privacy Auction, Privacy Trading, Mean-Field Game, Differential Privacy 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# FEDERATED Learning (FL) [1] has emerged as the pre-dominant paradigm for privacy-aware distributed machine learning, keeping raw data on local devices while sharing only model gradients with a central server. Yet a fundamental tension persists: service providers require high-quality data to build accurate models, while users are increasingly reluctant to disclose sensitive information without adequate compensa-tion or privacy guarantees. This tension is exacerbated by two structural deficiencies that undermine practical FL adoption at scale.

First, FL does not eliminate privacy risk. Model gradients themselves constitute a serious privacy vulnerability: gradient inversion attacks [2] can reconstruct original training samples from uploaded gradients with high fidelity. Differentially pri-vate FL (DP-FL) [3] mitigates this risk by injecting calibrated noise into gradients before upload, but this introduces a new dilemma: different clients have heterogeneous privacy valu-ations : some are willing to upload relatively clean gradients (ϵ large) for higher compensation, while others require strong protection ( ϵ small) and therefore contribute noisier, less useful gradients. A one-size-fits-all privacy policy fails to respect this heterogeneity, resulting in either privacy harm or avoidable accuracy loss. 

Second, FL lacks effective incentive mechanisms for privacy-sensitive participants. In the absence of monetary compensation, rational users have little motivation to expend local computation, communication bandwidth, and crucially privacy budget. Existing Federated Learning Market (FLM) approaches trade raw datasets, trained model weights, or FL training capacity [4], [5], [6], but none treats privacy itself as the traded commodity. The core insight is that the differential privacy budget ϵi, rather than data, models, or computation, which should be the primary object of trade ,with users receiving compensation proportional to the privacy they sacrifice. 

Three Key Technical Challenges. Realising this vision raises three fundamental challenges that prior work has not jointly addressed. 

Challenge 1: Privacy Market Design. How should the privacy trading market be formally structured? A rigorous market design must specify: (i) how users express their hetero-geneous privacy valuations; (ii) how a data buyer (e.g., an FL server) specifies its accuracy requirements and budget; and (iii) how a trusted broker/auctioneer allocates privacy budgets and determines payments. Critically, the mechanism must satisfy 

Dominant-Strategy Incentive Compatibility (DSIC), so that truthful reporting of privacy valuations is each user’s optimal strategy, and Individual Rationality (IR), so that participation is always at least as good as abstaining. Without these guar-antees, rational users will strategically misreport valuations, corrupting both market efficiency and FL model quality. 

Challenge 2: Scalability of Auction Equilibrium. Even with a well-defined mechanism, computing the Nash Equilib-rium (NE) of a privacy auction game among N participants is intrinsically difficult. Each user’s optimal bid depends on the strategies of all other N − 1 participants, so the NE problem has dimension O(N 2) and is NP-hard in general. For the large-

scale FL settings of interest ( N in the hundreds to thousands), exact NE computation is computationally intractable. How can approximate the auction equilibrium efficiently without losing theoretical guarantees? 

Challenge 3: Near-Optimal Mechanism Design under Unknown Valuation Distributions. Challenge 2 reduces the equilibrium problem to O(N ), but does not specify how to find a revenue-maximising allocation and payment rule. Clas-sical approaches (e.g., Myerson’s optimal auction theory [7]) yield closed-form solutions only under restrictive distributional assumptions. In FL, privacy valuations are heterogeneous, correlated across rounds, and drawn from an unknown distribu-tion. How can a mechanism be learned that is approximately revenue-optimal and DSIC-compliant, without assuming any closed-form prior over valuations? 

Approach and Contributions. The three challenges above are addressed through a unified framework combining a novel 

privacy trading market , a mean-field game (MFG) approxi-mation , and a deep learning-based automatic auction solver ,termed MFG-RegretNet . Inspired by the foundational privacy auction work [8] and recent progress in differentiable mecha-nism design [9], this framework makes the following specific contributions.  

> •

Privacy Trading Market (PTM). A formal privacy trading market for FL is established, in which each FL client treats its personalised differential privacy budget ϵi as a tradeable commodity. Clients bid for compensation commensurate with their privacy cost c(vi, ϵ i) = vi · ϵi,and the FL server purchases privacy budgets subject to a total expenditure constraint B. To the best of current knowledge, this is the first work to formalise privacy itself, rather than data, models, or computation, as the primary unit of trade in a federated learning market.  

> •

Privacy Auction Game (PAG) with Theoretical Guar-antees. The interaction among clients and the server is modeled as a Privacy Auction Game (PAG) and design a procurement mechanism satisfying DSIC, IR, Dominant-Strategy Truthfulness (DT), and Budget Feasibility (BF). It is proven (Theorem 1) that under these properties, truth-ful reporting of privacy valuations constitutes a Bayesian-Nash Equilibrium of the PAG, providing a rigorous incentive foundation for the proposed market.  

> •

Mean-Field Game Approximation. To overcome the 

O(N 2) scalability barrier, the population of FL clients is modeled as a mean-field flow μt ∈ P (V × E ) and reduce the multi-agent Nash problem to a coupled Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman / Fokker-Planck system. It is shown (Theorem 3) that the resulting Mean-Field Equilibrium 

ε-approximates the true Nash Equilibrium with error 

O(1 /√N ), enabling tractable computation in large-scale FL deployments while retaining rigorous approximation guarantees.  

> •

MFG-RegretNet: Deep Learning Auction Solver. MFG-RegretNet is proposed as a differentiable mechanism that addresses Challenge 3 by parameterising allocation and payment rules as neural networks conditioned on the scalar mean-field statistic bMFG (addressing Challenge 2). Rather than treating bMFG merely as a context feature, a dedicated MFG alignment regulariser forces learned pay-ments to agree with the mean-field payment functional, so that MFG equilibrium structure enters the training gradient. Approximate DSIC is enforced by minimising ex-post regret via augmented Lagrangian optimisation, without any closed-form prior over privacy valuations. This study lies at the intersection of four research streams, summarised in Table I, with extended discussion in Ap-pendix X-A 1.

Privacy-preserving FL. Gradient inversion attacks [2] demonstrate that an honest-but-curious server can reconstruct raw samples from shared gradients. The dominant defence is Differential Privacy: DP-SGD and its federated variant [3] inject calibrated Gaussian noise into gradients before upload, offering (ϵ, δ )-DP at the cost of model utility. Secure aggre-gation [14] uses cryptographic masking so the server observes only the aggregate. A critical gap persists: all these approaches treat ϵ as a fixed system parameter, never as a user-controlled commodity tradeable for compensation. 

FL incentive mechanisms. Incentive design for FL has been studied via Stackelberg games [15], [16], MFG-based reputation auctions [12], matching games [17], and DRL-based pricing [18]. Auction-based FL (AFL) [19] has emerged as a prominent sub-field, with CSRA [11] providing robust reverse auctions for DP-FL with truthfulness and budget-feasibility guarantees. However, all existing AFL works treat privacy as a cost constraint, trading data, models, or compute capacity rather than the privacy budget itself. 

Privacy auctions and data markets. Ghosh and Roth [8] laid the theoretical foundation for privacy-as-commodity by formulating a reverse auction that elicits individual privacy valuations. Fleischer and Lyu [10] extended this to correlated privacy costs; Ghosh et al. [20] addressed unverifiable valua-tions. More recently, Hu et al. [13] proposed MFG-based data pricing for federated markets; Wu et al. [21] treated privacy as a truthfully reported resource in FL, which is the closest prior art to our PAG framing. The key limitation of all prior work is a focus on static, single-round, centralised settings; none addresses the multi-round distributed FL context. 

Differentiable mechanism design. Myerson [7] gives revenue-optimal auctions under restrictive distributional as-sumptions inapplicable to heterogeneous FL clients. Regret-Net [9] parameterises allocation and payment rules as neural networks trained to minimise ex-post regret, circumventing distributional priors; DM-RegretNet [5] applied this to FL

GAME 

In this section, we introduce the privacy trading market ,where privacy as a commodity trades among data owners (DOs) and data buyers (DBs). We then design a privacy auction game to incentivise participants to share data. 

A. Privacy Trading Market 

The privacy trading market comprises three roles: Data Owners (DOs), an Auction Broker (AB), and Data Buyers 

(DBs). As illustrated in Fig. 1, the auction proceeds in six steps. DOs N = {1, . . . , n } hold local sensitive datasets 

D = {D 1, . . . , Dn}. We assume that DOs are single-minded :a DO will provide raw sensitive data only if the privacy preservation it requests is satisfied [28]. DO i protects her 4

sensitive data through a mechanism Mϵi satisfying (ϵi, δ i)-PLDP. DBs purchase sensitive data from DOs to complete analysis or model training tasks. The AB is a credible auc-tioneer that performs multiple rounds of auctions. Since a DO may misreport its privacy valuation v(D), the mechanism must incentivise truthful reporting. We assume the AB is fully trusted and a reverse auction mechanism is used [8], [29]. 

Assumption 1 All buyers agree to use privacy as a common evaluation metric, making the auction a fair game. Buyers have strong beliefs in the true value of their data; v represents the user’s estimation of data privacy during the auction. Buyers do not collude with each other during the auction process. 

Remark 1 (Threat Model and Trust Assumptions) Our framework operates under the following threat model. (1) Trusted Auction Broker (AB). We assume the AB is asemi-trusted, non-colluding third party that faithfully executes the auction mechanism and does not leak bid information to other clients. This is consistent with existing privacy auction literature [8], [29] and is a standard assumption in mechanism design. In practice, the AB can be instantiated as a secure multi-party computation (SMPC) node or atrusted execution environment (TEE), as explored in adjacent works. (2) No Client Collusion. Clients are assumed to act independently (Assumption 1). Collusion among a subset of clients could allow coordinated bid manipulation to inflate payments; analysing collusion-resistant mechanisms is an important direction for future work. (3) Relationship to Secure Aggregation. Our mechanism operates at the auction layer (determining which privacy budget each client uses), independently of the gradient aggregation layer. It is fully compatible with secure aggregation protocols [14] that mask individual gradients from the server: the AB only observes reported bids (vi, ϵ i), not raw gradients. Combining our market mechanism with secure aggregation thus provides both economic incentives (this work) and cryptographic privacy guarantees (orthogonal protocol layer). (4) Disclosure of ϵi.

Clients report their declared privacy budget ϵi as part of the bid. Under DSIC, truthful reporting is a dominant strategy, so the disclosed ϵi does not create additional privacy leakage beyond what is inherent in the auction participation itself. B. Privacy Auction Game (PAG)

V. P RIVACY -A WARE AUCTION FEDERATED LEARNING 

MECHANISM 

We combine the privacy auction game with FL to incentivise users to share data for three reasons. First, users are concerned about privacy risks from local model leakage. Second, without revenue, users have limited incentive to participate in FL train-ing. Third, a unified privacy policy ignores user heterogeneity, reducing model accuracy. We propose the Privacy Auction Game-based FL (PAG-FL) framework (Fig. 2). 

A. PAG-FL Framework 

PAG-FL involves three entities: 1) FL Server (FLS). FLS publishes model training tasks (e.g., image classification, text translation) and purchases client gradients via the FL architecture. 2) FL Broker (FLB). FLB is the auction institution for user data privacy. It recruits FLCs eligible for a given task and runs the privacy auction to determine allocation and payment. 3) FL Clients (FLC). FLCs are users or organisations that provide raw training data. Each FLC trains locally, applies a DP mechanism, and uploads a private gradient to FLB for auction. The operational steps are: 1) Publish Task. FLS publishes a learning task to FLB, which recruits eligible FLCs. 2) Registration. FLCs register with FLB and declare initial privacy requirements (ϵ, δ ).3) Submit Bid. Each FLC i submits a bid bi = ( vi, d i, u i, ϵ i),where vi is a valuation, di is model size, ui is local model accuracy, and ϵi is the privacy budget. 4) Privacy Payment. FLB runs the auction (PAC mechanism, Algorithm 1) and FLS pays selected FLCs. 5) Private Model. Each selected FLC applies its personalised DP mechanism to the local gradient. 6) Model Aggregation. In Section IV, we formulated the PAG as the N -player optimisation problem P1 (Eq. (11)). Although Theorem 1 guarantees the existence of a Bayesian-Nash Equilibrium under the PAC mechanism (Algorithm 1), computing that equilibrium becomes computationally intractable as the num-ber of FL clients N grows. A. Experimental Setup 1) Datasets and Models: We conduct experiments on two widely used benchmark datasets. MNIST [35] contains 70,000 grayscale handwritten-digit images ( 28 ×28 pixels, 10 classes); we train a three-layer MLP with ReLU activations. CIFAR-10 [36] contains 60,000 RGB images (32 ×32 pixels, 10 classes); we train a lightweight CNN with two convolutional blocks followed by a fully connected classifier. Both datasets are partitioned among clients using a Dirichlet allocation with concentration parameter α ∈ { 0.1, 0.5} to simulate non-IID data heterogeneity, where smaller α produces more severe heterogeneity. 

2) Parameter Settings: We conduct experiments with the number of clients N ∈ { 10 , 50 , 100 , 200 , 500 } for scalability evaluation, and fix N =100 for all other experiments. The MFG-RegretNet architecture comprises an Auction Header Network (AHN) with 3 hidden layers of 128 units, an Al-location Network with column-wise Softmax outputs, and a Payment Network with Sigmoid activations, totaling approx-imately 50K trainable parameters. Training proceeds for 200 outer iterations, each containing 25 inner gradient steps on batches of L=64 sampled bid profiles. Regret approximation uses R=25 steps of projected gradient ascent (PGA) with step size η=0 .01 . We use the Adam optimizer ( α=10 −3), with augmented Lagrangian IR penalty γ=10 and DSIC penalty schedule ρ0=1 growing by factor κ=1 .5 every few iterations up to ρmax =100 . Privacy valuation distributions, budget set-tings, and FL training hyperparameters are detailed in Tables II and III, respectively. Each experiment is repeated ≥ 5 times with different random seeds and results are reported as mean 

± standard deviation. All experiments run on an Intel Xeon E5-2680 v4 with 256 GB RAM and an NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU.

> •

> •

> •

> •

MFG-Pricing [13]: MFG-based data pricing without an explicit auction mechanism (large-scale FL baseline).  

> •

C. Discussion of Results and Limitations 

Experiments validate three points: low regret with strong auction efficiency (RQ1/RQ3), near-linear deployment trends in runtime and memory (RQ2), and competitive FL accuracy with approximation error decreasing as N grows (RQ4), consistent with the O(N −1/2) bound. Four limitations remain. 

(L1) Static rounds: composition across rounds is not mod-eled; a dynamic PAG with remaining-budget state and R´ enyi-DP accounting is needed. (L2) Fairness: high-sensitivity clients may be under-served under skewed valuations; add ser-vice floors, compensation floors, or group-regularized regret. IX. C ONCLUSION 

This paper establishes a new paradigm for federated learning incentive design by treating the personalised privacy budget ϵi

as a tradeable commodity rather than a fixed system constraint. We formalised the Privacy Auction Game with DSIC, IR, DT, and budget feasibility guarantees (Theorem 1), and used mean-field approximation to reduce deploy-time complexity from 13 

O(N 2 log N ) to O(N ) with an O(N −1/2) equilibrium gap (Theorem 3). Built on this foundation, MFG-RegretNet learns allocation and payment rules without closed-form valuation priors via augmented Lagrangian optimisation and MFG align-ment. Experiments on MNIST and CIFAR-10 show that MFG-RegretNet consistently improves incentive compatibility and auction efficiency while remaining competitive in downstream FL model accuracy compared with VCG, PAC, RegretNet, and MFG-Pricing baselines. A. Related Work: Extended Survey 

Privacy-Preserving Techniques in FL. Gradient inversion attacks [2] show that an honest-but-curious server can recon-struct raw samples from shared gradients. DP-SGD [3] clips gradients and injects calibrated noise for client-level (ϵ, δ )-DP. Secure aggregation [14] uses cryptographic masking so the server sees only the aggregate. Both treat ϵ as a fixed parameter, not a traded commodity. 

FL Incentive Mechanisms. Coalitional games [39], Stack-elberg games [15], [16], MFG-based mechanisms [12], and matching games [17] address incentive design. AFL [19] is an active sub-field; CSRA [11] provides robust reverse auctions for DP-FL. All existing AFL works trade data, models, or compute rather than privacy budgets. 

Privacy Auctions. Ghosh & Roth [8] laid the theoretical foundation; Fleischer & Lyu [10] extended to correlated costs; Ghosh et al. [20] handled unverifiable reports; Wang et al. [40] studied negative payments; Zhang et al. [28] addressed auc-tions under privacy constraints; Hu et al. [13] proposed MFG-based data pricing; Wu et al. [21] is the closest prior art without a learned differentiable auction. All prior works are static, single-round, and centralised. 

Differentiable Mechanism Design. Myerson [7] gives revenue-optimal auctions under restrictive assumptions. Re-gretNet [9] parameterises allocation/payment rules as neu-ral networks trained to minimise ex-post regret. DM-RegretNet [5] applies this to FL model trading but inherits 

O(N 2) pairwise dependency. MFG-RegretNet reduces this to 

O(N ) and operates on privacy budgets as the commodity.
